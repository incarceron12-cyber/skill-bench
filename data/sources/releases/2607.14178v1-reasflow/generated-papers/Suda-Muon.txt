SUDA-Muon: Decentralized Muon Optimization
via a Unified Primal-Dual Framework

          Abstract. Fully decentralized Muon is difficult for a specific reason: the nonlinear ma-
          trix sign operator msgn does not commute with gossip averaging. As a result, ex-
          tending Muon from centralized training to sparse peer-to-peer networks is not just a
          matter of adding communication; one must decide how polarization, tracking, and
          consensus correction should interact. We propose SUDA–Muon, which places Muon’s
          matrix polarization directions inside the SUDA primal–dual backbone parameterized
          by ( A,B,C ). The same template recovers ED/D2 , EXTRA, gradient tracking, and re-
          lated decentralized schemes, so the communication mechanism can vary while the
          Muon geometry is kept fixed. We prove a topology-separated
                                                                   √        non-asymptotic rate in
          the nuclear-norm geometry: the leading term O((1 + σ/ N )K −1/4 ) is independent of
          the graph, and topology appears only through faster-decaying corrections involving
          the SUDA contraction rate and the spectral gap. We also show by a smooth coun-
          terexample that tracking before polarization is not optional: removing it can trap the
          averaged iterate at a non-stationary point. We further give a linear-speedup counterex-
          ample that separates federated and fully √decentralized Muon: server-side averaging
          before polarization enjoys the usual σ/ N variance reduction, whereas local polar-
          ization before averaging does not, even under exact averaging on a complete graph.
          Experiments on CIFAR-100 and GPT-2 fine-tuning support the same qualitative pic-
          ture. On short-horizon ring benchmarks, decentralized Muon variants are often close;
          in the 20-node, 100-epoch non-IID CIFAR-100 setting both SUDA-based variants out-
          perform D E M UON, with SUDA–M UON -ED performing best, while under near-IID
          partitions the gap between different SUDA–Muon instances becomes much smaller
          and the preferred topology changes.

Fully decentralized training is attractive in settings where data and compute are naturally
spread across agents and a central coordinator is either unavailable or undesirable [3, 4,
8]. In this paper, however, we are interested in a narrower question than decentralized

learning in general: how should one extend Muon, a matrix-structured optimizer, to a
sparse peer-to-peer network? The main obstacle is that Muon’s matrix sign operator
msgn is nonlinear, whereas decentralized communication is built from linear averaging.
Once these two operations are combined, averaging and polarization need not agree, so
the usual intuition from decentralized SGD no longer applies.
    To tackle this problem, we consider the decentralized matrix optimization problem

                                    min f ( X ) ≜           f i ( X ),                          (1.1)

where agent i ∈ [ N ] ≜ {1,..., N } only has access to its local objective f i and can query a
stochastic first-order oracle. At iteration k, given a local iterate Xik , agent i samples ξ ik and
obtains a stochastic gradient

                                   Gik ≜ ∇ Fi ( Xik ;ξ ik ) ∈ Rm×n ,                            (1.2)

which satisfies standard unbiasedness and bounded-variance conditions. The agents
are connected by an undirected communication graph encoded by a symmetric, dou-
bly stochastic mixing matrix W ∈ R N × N , and they can exchange messages only with their
neighbors according to W.
    In this setting, decentralized optimization already offers several correction mecha-
nisms for heterogeneity, including gradient tracking, EXTRA, and ED/D2 [8, 13, 15].
SUDA is particularly appealing because it expresses many of these schemes inside one
primal–dual recursion parameterized by three matrices ( A,B,C ) built from W [2]. On
the optimizer side, Muon replaces a coordinatewise step by a matrix polarization direc-
tion and has shown strong empirical performance in centralized and federated settings
[7, 9, 10, 14]. What is still missing is a framework that lets these two viewpoints meet
cleanly: we would like to vary the communication backbone without redesigning the
Muon update from scratch every time.
    Current decentralized Muon results do not yet provide that picture. DeMuon [6] com-
bines Muon with gradient tracking and works over general graphs, but it is built around
one specific communication template. This leaves four questions open. Can Muon be
placed inside a unified SUDA-style backbone? Can one obtain a topology-separated rate
in the nuclear-norm geometry induced by Muon, rather than an analysis in which net-
work effects and optimizer geometry are entangled from the start? Is tracking before
polarization genuinely necessary, or merely convenient? And, compared with server-
based/federated Muon, can a fully decentralized Muon update still exploit the usual
linear-speedup effect from averaging stochastic gradients?
    We answer these questions with SUDA–Muon, which keeps Muon’s matrix geometry
but places it inside the SUDA primal–dual recursion. The point is not only to obtain one
more decentralized Muon method. It is to expose which parts of the behavior come from
the communication mechanism and which parts come from the polarization step itself.
    Our contributions are the following.

2      Related Work
2.1     Matrix-Aware Optimizers and the Muon Family in Centralized Training
Muon was introduced as an optimizer that acts directly on matrix-valued parameters
through a polarization step instead of treating every layer as a flattened vector [7]. Sub-
sequent empirical work reports strong performance on modern architectures, including
large language models, and recent theory interprets Muon as steepest descent under a
spectral-norm constraint [9, 12, 16]. For our purposes, the important point is not merely
that Muon is matrix-aware, but that its update rule is nonlinear at the matrix level. That
nonlinearity is exactly what complicates decentralization.
    A broader literature studies structured optimizers such as Adafactor and Shampoo [5,
11]. These methods also exploit matrix or tensor structure, but they do not raise the same
question considered here. Our focus is narrower: how to combine Muon’s polarization
step with fully decentralized correction mechanisms over sparse graphs.
4                              ReasFlow Paper Copilot / CSIAM Trans. Appl. Math., x (20xx), pp. 1-50


2.2   Decentralized Optimization and Heterogeneity Correction
Decentralized SGD is simple but biased under heterogeneous data. EXTRA, Exact
Diffusion/D2 , and gradient tracking were developed to remove or reduce that bias
through additional correction terms [13, 15, 17]. SUDA is especially relevant because
it places several of these methods inside one primal–dual template parameterized by
( A,B,C ), making it possible to compare communication mechanisms without redesign-
ing the algorithm each time [1, 18].
    Most of this literature, however, works in a Euclidean setting: gradients are commu-
nicated linearly, and the optimizer itself is vector-based or at most diagonally precondi-
tioned. The resulting theory says a great deal about topology and bias correction, but it
does not address what happens when the optimizer applies a nonlinear matrix transfor-
mation before the update.

2.3   Distributed Muon: Decentralized and Federated Extensions
Distributed extensions of Muon are already beginning to appear. Federated variants such
as FedMuon study star-topology settings and show that naive local Muon combined with
FedAvg can be unstable under heterogeneity, which motivates additional server-side cor-
rection [10, 14]. Those works are informative about heterogeneity, but they do not address
fully decentralized communication.
    DeMuon is, to our knowledge, the clearest fully decentralized Muon method on gen-
eral graphs [6]. It demonstrates that combining Muon with gradient tracking is viable,
but it is organized around one specific communication template. Our paper differs in em-
phasis: we ask for a unified SUDA-style backbone for Muon, a topology-separated rate
within that backbone, a direct statement that tracking before polarization is not merely
helpful but necessary in general, and an explicit counterexample separating the linear-
speedup behavior of federated and fully decentralized Muon.

3.2     Algorithm Description
The framework is parameterized by the SUDA communication matrices ( A,B,C ), which
are low-degree polynomials of the mixing matrix W. Different choices recover well-
known decentralized schemes as special cases:

      • ED/D2 : A = C = W and B2 = I − W 2 .

      • EXTRA: A = C = I +2W and B2 = I −2W .

      • Gradient Tracking (ATC-GT): A = C = W and B = I − W.

Remark 3.1. DeMuon [6] uses a closely related gradient-tracking mechanism combined
with Muon directions, but applies mixing directly to the primal variables rather than
through the SUDA primal–dual backbone. Specifically, DeMuon does not maintain the
dual variable Yk and instead performs a gossip-style averaging step on Xk after the Muon
update. This subtle structural difference means DeMuon is not recovered as a special
case of Algorithm 1, but its gradient-tracking component shares the same motivations
analyzed in our convergence theory.

In all cases, the Muon polarization direction msgn(Hk+1 ) is applied after gradient track-
ing, so that the msgn operator acts on a network-aggregated quantity rather than a purely
local signal. The stacked update equations are:

4.5   Why Tracking Must Precede Polarization
The previous subsection treated the communication backbone as a modular choice inside
the SUDA template. We now turn to a different design choice that is not modular in
this sense: whether Muon polarizes a tracked quantity or a purely local one. A key
design choice in SUDA–Muon is that the Muon direction is computed from a network-
aggregated / tracked quantity Hik+1 rather than directly from a local stochastic gradient

tracking step while keeping the rest of the algorithm unchanged, then one cannot, in

an additional assumption, since the stochastic-oracle model in Assumption 3 explicitly

must hold in particular for this simplest admissible case. In this setting, we construct
smooth and lower-bounded problems on which the averaged iterate X̄ k gets stuck at a

6     Numerical Experiments
6.1   Experimental Setup
We use two benchmarks that stress different parts of the decentralized Muon story:
CIFAR-100 with strong cross-node heterogeneity, and GPT-2 fine-tuning on Wikitext-2
with a much milder, near-IID partition. Throughout, we compare SUDA–M UON -ED,
SUDA–M UON -ATC-GT, and D E M UON, and all quantitative summaries are computed
from pre-aggregated CSV logs produced by the training scripts.
    For CIFAR-100, we use the standard 50,000/10,000 train/test split, a ResNet-18-style
model with 100 output classes, and a Dirichlet partition with concentration α = 0.05,
which produces severe label skew across workers. The main comparison uses N = 10
nodes on a ring with mixing parameter ρ = 0.25, batch size 512 per node, constant step-
size η = 0.03, EMA parameter β = 0.9, weight decay 5 × 10−4 , and 25 training epochs.
Unless otherwise stated, each configuration is run with 3 random seeds, and we report
the performance of the averaged model.
    We also include two CIFAR-100 extensions aimed at the communication story. First,
fixing SUDA–M UON -ED, we vary the topology and node count over N ∈ {4,8,12} to
isolate how system scale changes the preferred graph. Second, we run a larger and longer
experiment with N = 20 ring-connected nodes for 100 epochs using a slightly smaller
constant stepsize η = 0.01. This long-horizon setting is where steady-state communication
effects should be easiest to see.
    For GPT-2/Wikitext-2, each node hosts the pretrained gpt2 model. We tokenize the
corpus into length-512 sequences, use local batch size 16, train for 4 epochs with base
stepsize η = 0.03, and apply a warmup-plus-cosine schedule. The main comparison uses
a 15-node ring, while the ablation again varies topology and node count for SUDA–
M UON -ED. Each GPT configuration is run with 2 seeds, and we report validation per-
plexity at epoch 4.

7    Conclusion
This paper studies one specific obstruction in fully decentralized Muon: the polarization
step is nonlinear, so it does not interact with gossip averaging in the same way as an
ordinary gradient step. SUDA–Muon addresses that obstruction by placing Muon inside
the SUDA primal–dual backbone, which lets us compare several decentralized correction
mechanisms within one template.
     The main theoretical message is that the dominant stochastic term remains topology-
free, while the graph enters only through faster-decaying corrections. The main structural
message is even simpler: tracking before polarization is not optional in general. Without
it, one can construct smooth heterogeneous problems on which the averaged iterate stays
at a non-stationary point.
     The second conceptual message is a contrast with federated Muon. In our linear-
speedup
    √      counterexample, server-side averaging before polarization recovers the usual
σ/ N variance reduction, while fully decentralized local polarization before averaging
does not, even on a complete graph with exact averaging.
     Our experiments on CIFAR-100 and GPT-2/Wikitext-2 are consistent with this pic-
ture. Short-horizon and near-IID runs make the decentralized Muon variants look simi-

lar, but in the 20-node, 100-epoch non-IID CIFAR-100 setting both SUDA-based variants
outperform D E M UON, with SUDA–M UON -ED performing best. When the partition is
near IID, the different SUDA–Muon instances behave much more similarly and the best
topology can shift.
     Natural next steps include communication-efficient variants with compression,
sharper rates under additional structure such as PL-type conditions, online tuning of the
stepsize and EMA parameters, and larger-scale experiments that test whether the same
topology effects persist for bigger models and graphs.
