                                                                                                       Abstract
                                                   Large language models achieve high scores on medical knowledge assessments, yet clinical reasoning requires
                                               actively deciding what to investigate under uncertainty. We developed an agentic evaluation framework in
                                               hematologic oncology in which models must proactively request clinical data across three sequential rounds
                                               before committing to a diagnosis and treatment plan. Across 32 frontier models, the best achieved only 68%
                                               overall accuracy. Information utilization - the fraction of available data actually requested - was the strongest
                                               predictor of diagnostic accuracy (R = 0.69, P < 0.001), yet utilization collapsed from 57% to 26% in the final
                                               round, leaving molecular and cytogenetic data critical for treatment selection unexamined. Reasoning traces
                                               scored high on a clinical reasoning rubric (91% above threshold) but decorrelated from accuracy, revealing a gap
                                               between locally coherent rationales and globally correct conclusions. Error analysis identified search satisficing,
                                               anchoring and premature closure as the dominant failure modes - the same cognitive biases that characterize
                                               novice clinicians under dual-process models of diagnostic reasoning. These findings demonstrate that the primary
                                               limitation of current models in clinical oncology is not insufficient medical knowledge but a systematic failure of
                                               information-seeking under uncertainty.


                                         Introduction
                                         Clinical decision-making in hematologic oncology requires the sequential integration of incomplete and evolving
                                         information. Resource constraints and clinical urgency demand a stepwise, case-adapted approach in which
                                         each diagnostic step is selected based on its expected impact on the therapeutic decision at hand. Molecular
                                         profiling results arrive incrementally, treatment decisions must account for prior therapies and transplant history,
                                         and concurrent complications may fundamentally alter the therapeutic calculus. At each stage, clinicians must
                                         decide how to interpret partial results and when to act under uncertainty. This capacity to identify what information
                                         is needed and to prioritize appropriately constitutes a core competence of an experienced hematologist. In cognitive
                                         science, this process is formalized in dual-process models of diagnostic reasoning, where the transition from rapid
                                         pattern recognition to deliberate hypothesis testing depends critically on recognizing when initial impressions are
                                         insufficient. 1


                                                                                                           1
Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong performance on medical knowledge assessments and
oncology decision support. 2–4 Yet reliable active information-seeking by these systems remains insufficiently tested
in subspecialty oncology. 5–7 Multimodal AI systems that integrate information from radiology, histopathology,
genomics, and clinical records 8–11 and autonomous AI agents have demonstrated promising results in structured
clinical tasks. However, all existing evaluations share a critical limitation: they present models with complete
clinical information and assess the ability to interpret it, not the ability to decide what information to seek. 12,13
Existing benchmarks present clinical information passively. 14–16 In clinical practice, the direction of information
flow is reversed: clinicians must actively decide what to investigate, interpret results as they arrive, and revise
plans accordingly. This process of sequential planning under uncertainty separates clinical reasoning from medical
knowledge retrieval. In human cognition, novice clinicians characteristically fail at exactly this juncture, exhibiting
search satisficing, premature closure, and anchoring bias as described in Croskerry’s dual-process model. Whether
large language models exhibit analogous information-seeking failures has not been systematically tested. 17,18
Here we test this directly using OncoRounds, an agentic evaluation framework that requires proactive information-
seeking in hematologic oncology. Models are presented with an initial clinical scenario and must actively request
clinical information, one item per turn, across three rounds of increasing complexity. This design separates the
ability to identify relevant clinical data from the ability to interpret it, and evaluates both within a single, integrated
framework. Across 32 frontier LLMs and 20 complex cases,the highest-performing model achieved only 68%
overall accuracy, diagnostic performance deteriorated as cases progressed from syndromic impressions to molecular
diagnoses, and models converged on the same information-seeking failure modes that characterize novice human
clinicians, despite sharing no developmental pathway with them.


Results
We evaluated 32 frontier LLMs (Supplementary Table 1) across 20 complex hematologic malignancy cases (Table 1,
Figure 1c/d), each structured across three sequential rounds that mirror the patient journey from initial presentation
through the first week on the ward to long-term definitive treatment planning, with diagnostic results staged to
reflect realistic clinical timelines. Of the 32 models, 15 had open weights, and 23 provided at least partial access to
reasoning traces (Figure 1c/d). In each round, models were presented with a clinical scenario and allowed to request
additional clinical information one item per turn before being assessed on diagnostic accuracy, differential reasoning,
and treatment recommendations. Given the scale of evaluation, we employed an LLM-as-judge approach, 19
validating its accuracy against an expert hematologist with 10 years of clinical experience.

Overall Performance
The highest-scoring model, Claude Opus 4.6, reached an overall accuracy of 68.1%, while the lowest-performing
model, Llama 3.3 70B, scored 40.2% (mean across all models 56.3 ± 7.4%; Table 2). Performance varied
substantially across scoring components. Diagnostic accuracy was highest (mean 66.6 ± 9.2%), followed by
treatment recommendations (58.8 ± 6.5%), while differential reasoning proved most challenging (43.6 ± 9.4%). All
32 evaluated models showed differential reasoning as their weakest component, regardless of overall performance
level. The gap between diagnostic accuracy and differential reasoning averaged 23.0 percentage points, suggesting
that models can identify a likely diagnosis from available information but struggle to systematically consider and
weigh competing alternatives. More recent models performed better (R = 0.51, p = 0.002; Figure 1a), consistent
with ongoing capability gains across model generations. Model size showed a modest positive correlation with
overall performance among models with publicly available parameter counts (R = 0.45, p = 0.046; Figure 1b),
though this analysis was limited to 15 of 32 models, where the number of parameters was known.
The volume of information requests declined across rounds, from a mean of 7.5 per round (SD 3.9) in R1 to 4.2
(SD 2.2) in R2 and 1.8 (SD 1.0) in R3 (Kruskal-Wallis p < 0.001; Supplementary Figure 1a). Of these requests,
58.8% yielded released information, 17.2% were invalid, 8.8% received guidance redirecting to a similar item,
and 15.2% targeted items not yet available in the current round. The utilization rate (fraction of available clinical
information actually obtained) showed a strong positive correlation with overall accuracy (R = 0.69, p < 0.001,
Supplementary Figure 1b), indicating that models gathering more of the available information performed better.
However, utilization declined sharply in later rounds (Supplementary Figure 1c). In R1, models obtained 56.8%
of available diagnostics, remaining similar in R2 (56.7%) before dropping to 25.7% in R3, with several models
requesting only 1–2 items in R3 before attempting to solve, leaving molecular panels and treatment response data
unexamined.

Round Dynamics
Model performance declined across rounds (Table 3; Figure 2a–c). Mean composite scores dropped from 58.0%
in Round 1 (outpatient presentation) to 55.1% in Round 2 (inpatient workup) and 51.2% in Round 3 (definitive
characterization), and 25 of 32 models scored lower in R3 than R1. Correct diagnosis rates fell from 48.7% in R1 to
41.6% in R3, while partially correct diagnoses rose from 34.3% to 46.8%. Fully incorrect diagnoses decreased
from 17.0% to 11.6%. This pattern reflects the increasing specificity required in later rounds, as R3 demand the
integrative synthesis of molecular profiles, treatment history, and concurrent comorbidities to arrive at actionable
therapeutic recommendations. Models often achieved partial diagnostic precision as additional clinical data became
available but failed to reach the specificity required for full credit.
Not all models finished all cases, being cancelled by the benchmark after too many wrong requests. Analysis of 630
model-case pairs with complete R1 and R3 diagnoses revealed pronounced diagnostic anchoring (Figure 2d,e). Of
323 pairs with an incorrect or partially correct R1 diagnosis, 212 (65.6%) remained incorrect through R3, yielding a
recovery rate of 34.4%. Conversely, of 307 pairs with a correct R1 diagnosis, 156 (50.8%) deteriorated to incorrect
or partially correct by R3.
The asymmetry of incorrect diagnoses persisting at nearly twice the rate at which correct diagnoses were maintained,
suggests that early errors are far harder to recover from. However, recovery and deterioration rates varied substantially
across models (Supplementary Table 2; Supplementary Figure 2c). Claude Opus 4.5 corrected all six of its initially
incorrect diagnoses by R3 (100% recovery), and Claude Opus 4.6 recovered seven of eight (87.5%). Qwen3 Next
80b recovered only one of 14 (7.1%). Deterioration rates ranged from 18.2% for GPT-5.2 Reasoning (two of 11) to
83.3% for Qwen3 Next 80b (five of six). Models with the highest recovery rates also had fewer initial errors.
Anchoring patterns also differed by case (Supplementary Table 5, Supplementary Figure 5). Case 20 (Richter
transformation of CLL) showed zero deterioration among 30 models with a correct R1 diagnosis, consistent with
a case where the core disease identity is apparent at presentation and later rounds add prognostic detail without
changing the diagnostic category. By contrast, Case 3 (relapsed AML with complex karyotype) had 26 of 29 initially
correct diagnoses deteriorate by R3 (89.7%), followed by Case 1 (relapsed AML with FLT3-ITD; 18/19, 94.7%).
Examination of the actual diagnosis text revealed that deterioration predominantly reflected subtype refinement
failure rather than diagnostic category shifts. Models maintained the correct broad disease identity (e.g., "AML,"
"multiple myeloma") but failed to integrate molecular subtyping, WHO 2022 classification, or treatment-refractory
status when these became available in R3 (Supplementary Table 6). In Case 3, for example, models correctly
identified myelodysplastic syndrome in R1 and appropriately updated to AML in R3, but achieved only "partially
correct" scores because they could not specify the complete WHO/ELN classification (AML, myelodysplasia-related
(AML-MR per WHO 2022), with complex karyotype, ELN 2022 adverse risk). Notably, most deterioration
resulted in partially correct rather than fully incorrect R3 diagnoses (Supplementary Figure 5b), indicating that
models incorporated some molecular findings but could not achieve the precision required for actionable clinical
classification.

Clinical Reasoning Quality
To assess reasoning quality beyond diagnostic accuracy, an adapted R-IDEA 20 rubric was applied to 1,008
evaluations across 17 models that produced full thinking traces (Figure 3, Supplementary Figure 4). Models
providing only reasoning summaries (GPT-5 series, Gemini series) were excluded. R-IDEA scores reasoning
across four dimensions: interpretive summary (I; 0–4), differential diagnosis (D; 0–2), explanation (E; 0–2), and
alternatives considered (A; 0–2), yielding a total score of 0–10. 20
The mean R-IDEA total was 8.22 (SD 1.59), and 91.1% of evaluations scored at or above 6, a threshold previously
proposed for high-quality clinical reasoning. 20 Model-level means ranged from 6.45 (GPT-OSS 20B) to 9.58
(Claude Opus 4.5). Claude Opus 4.5, GLM 5 (9.40), and Claude Sonnet 4.6 (9.35) occupied the top three positions,
while GPT-OSS 20B (6.45), Qwen3 235B A22B (6.70), and DeepSeek V3.2 (7.38) formed the lower tier (Table
4). R-IDEA scores slightly increased in R3 compared to R1 for most models, paired with longer traces but fewer
requests (Supplementary Figure 3).
Dimension-level analysis revealed an asymmetry in reasoning quality. When scores were normalized to the
proportion of each dimension's maximum, explanation was strongest (E: 90.8%), followed by differential diagnosis
(D: 89.0%), while alternatives (A: 77.5%) and interpretive summary (I: 76.9%) showed the largest deficits. The
interpretive summary gap suggests that models underutilize the interactive structure of the benchmark, requesting



                                                           3
fewer diagnostic tests than would be optimal, also reflected in the declining number of information requests across
rounds (often only 1–2 in Round 3 before the next solve).
R-IDEA scores showed a positive correlation with overall benchmark accuracy (R = 0.56, p = 0.02), suggesting that
models producing higher-quality reasoning traces tended to achieve better diagnostic outcomes. However, outliers
such as GPT-OSS 120B demonstrated below-median accuracy (56.4%) despite high reasoning quality (R-IDEA
8.90).

Error Taxonomy
Alongside R-IDEA scoring, reasoning traces were annotated for cognitive biases and process errors using a
taxonomy adapted from Croskerry's dual-process model of clinical reasoning. 21 Errors were identified in 89 of
1,008 evaluations (8.8%), yielding 93 individual error annotations across the 17 models with full thinking traces
(Table 4).
Process errors were more frequent than cognitive biases. The most common error types were search satisficing,
premature closure, and anchoring bias. Anchoring bias, the most frequent cognitive bias, appeared across multiple
models, consistent with the diagnostic anchoring observed in the round-level trajectory analysis. Hallucinated
information was rare, suggesting that models generally constrained their reasoning to the presented clinical data
(Figure 3b).
Error rates varied widely across models (Table 4). Claude Sonnet 4.5, Claude Opus 4.5, and GLM 5 had the lowest
error rates (1.7% each), while Qwen3 Next 80B had the highest (26.7%). The majority of errors were of medium
severity (56/93, 60.2%), followed by low (27/93, 29.0%) and high severity (10/93, 10.8%). No critical errors with
the potential to cause direct patient harm were identified (Supplementary Figure 4). Detailed examples on reasoning
errors are available in the supplementary materials.


Discussion
Across 20 complex hematologic malignancy cases and 32 frontier LLMs, the best-performing model achieved
68.1% overall accuracy when required to actively gather clinical information across sequential rounds. Performance
was highest for diagnostic accuracy (mean 66.6%), lower for treatment recommendations (58.8%), and lowest for
differential reasoning (43.6%), a ranking that held across all models regardless of family, size, or generation.These
results indicate that the primary challenge for current LLMs in clinical oncology lies not in medical knowledge
retrieval but in the sequential processes of information gathering, prioritization, and diagnostic updating that
characterize real clinical reasoning. Strikingly, the failure modes observed - search satisficing, anchoring at an initial
level of diagnostic resolution, and premature closure - mirror those documented in novice human clinicians under
dual-process models of diagnostic reasoning. This convergence suggests that the information-seeking deficit reflects
a structural property of how these systems process sequential, incomplete information rather than a domain-specific
knowledge gap.
LLMs now achieve above 90% accuracy on USMLE-style questions, 2–4,15 and multi-task evaluation frameworks such
as MedHELM span dozens of clinical domains. 14 However, these benchmarks present complete clinical vignettes
and ask models to select or generate an answer. AgentClinic demonstrated that models excelling on MedQA perform
poorly when required to gather information through multi-turn dialogue, 22 and MedDialogRubrics, evaluating
over 5,000 synthetic cases with fine-grained rubrics, concluded that improvements require advances in dialogue
management architectures rather than incremental model tuning. 23 MedAgentSim introduced self-improvement
mechanisms for doctor agents and found partial but incomplete gains. 24 These studies have focused on general
internal medicine. OncoRounds extends interactive evaluation into subspecialty hematology/oncology, using 20
cases deliberately constructed to represent the most diagnostically demanding scenarios in hematologic practice,
including relapsed, refractory, and post-transplant presentations that require the integration of molecular diagnostics,
treatment history, and evolving clinical complications into coherent management strategies. A controlled, turn-based
design separates information-seeking from interpretation and tracks both across three clinically staged rounds.
Information utilization, the fraction of available clinical data that a model actually requested, was the strongest
single predictor of overall accuracy (R = 0.69, p < 0.001). Yet models systematically under-requested information,
particularly in Round 3, where utilization dropped from approximately 57% in Rounds 1 and 2 to 25.7%. Several
models requested only one or two items before committing to a diagnosis, leaving molecular panels, cytogenetic
results, and treatment response data unexamined. R-IDEA evaluation of reasoning traces confirmed this asymmetry.

Interpretive summary and alternatives considered were the weakest dimensions across 17 models, while explanation
scored near ceiling. In human clinical reasoning, search satisficing, the premature termination of information search
after identifying a plausible explanation, is a well-characterized failure mode. 21 The present data suggest that LLMs
exhibit an analogous pattern, likely compounded by two known architectural and behavioral limitations. First, LLMs
exhibit positional attention biases that degrade retrieval of information positioned in the middle of long contexts, 25
and performance on complex reasoning tasks deteriorates as context length increases, with models producing
shorter and less reliable outputs. 26 OncoRounds is demanding in this regard. By the final round, the accumulated
interaction history of requests, released information items, prior diagnostic assessments, and reasoning traces can
exceed 10,000 tokens per case, placing critical molecular data at exactly the context positions where retrieval is
weakest. Second, Laban et al. demonstrated that LLMs perform significantly worse in multi-turn conversations
than in equivalent single-turn instructions, with accuracy declining as interaction depth increases, compounded by
premature assumptions from which models fail to recover. 27 The round-over-round performance decline observed
here is consistent with both phenomena, and the interaction between context saturation and multi-turn degradation
may explain why the largest utilization drop occurs in the final round, where context is longest and the number of
prior turns is greatest. Training predominantly on complete-case presentations, where active information gathering
is never modeled, may further amplify this tendency.
Composite performance declined across rounds, with 25 of 32 models scoring lower in the final round than the first.
This pattern does not indicate that additional data harms performance. Rather, later rounds demand progressively
greater diagnostic precision as the reference standard shifts from syndromic identification to molecular subtyping
per WHO classification, risk stratification, and treatment-refractory designations. Models consistently identified
the correct disease category but failed to achieve the specificity required for actionable clinical classification, a
pattern the trajectory analysis characterized as subtype refinement failure. Anchoring bias and premature closure,
among the most frequently cited cognitive errors in clinical medicine, 21 offer a partial mechanistic explanation.
Recent work confirms that LLMs exhibit analogous vulnerabilities. Moëll et al. identified anchoring and difficulty
integrating conflicting data as the most frequent errors in DeepSeek R1's medical reasoning, 28 and Schmidgall
et al. showed that cognitive biases reduce diagnostic accuracy across medical LLMs with varying resilience by
model family. 29 Ke et al. demonstrated that multi-agent frameworks with designated devil's advocate agents can
partially mitigate such biases, improving accuracy from 0% to 76% in diagnostically challenging scenarios. 30
The OncoRounds data extend these observations by showing that anchoring operates at a finer granularity than
previously documented. Models did not anchor on incorrect disease categories but on an initial level of diagnostic
resolution and failed to update toward the molecular precision that later-round evidence demanded. However, in
hematologic oncology, where treatment selection depends directly on molecular subtype and risk classification, this
level of detail is needed.
Differential reasoning was the weakest scoring component for all 32 models, averaging 23 percentage points below
diagnostic accuracy. This pattern was corroborated by the R-IDEA evaluation, where the alternatives dimension was
among the lowest-scoring, and by the error taxonomy, where search satisficing and premature closure were the most
frequent error types. However, differential diagnosis is also the most subjective component in the benchmark, as the
clinical relevance and ranking of alternative diagnoses are less standardized than the primary diagnosis or treatment
plan, making automated scoring less reliable for this category. With that caveat, the consistent deficit across all
models and evaluation modalities suggests a genuine limitation rather than a scoring artifact. In hematologic
oncology, the differential diagnosis determines which molecular and cytogenetic studies to pursue. A model that
commits to AML without adequately considering ALL, or mixed-phenotype acute leukemia will fail to request the
investigations needed to distinguish between them, compounding the information-gathering deficit observed above.
The positive correlation between R-IDEA scores and benchmark accuracy (R = 0.56, p = 0.02) indicates that
reasoning quality contributes to diagnostic outcomes, but the relationship is moderate. R-IDEA scores exceeded
the high-quality threshold (6 of 10) in 91% of evaluations, yet mean accuracy remained 56.3%. This disconnect
aligns with the faithfulness-plausibility gap described in recent reviews of medical LLM reasoning, 31 whereby
models produce well-structured reasoning traces that nonetheless arrive at incorrect conclusions. MedThink-
Bench addressed this by evaluating step-by-step rationales against expert-authored references and achieved strong
correlation with expert judgment (Pearson r = 0.87), but within a static format where all relevant information
is provided upfront. 32 OncoRounds adds a temporal dimension. Even when reasoning quality within a single
solve attempt is high, the cumulative effect of inadequate information gathering together with the context-length
limitations discussed above can undermine the final diagnosis. High R-IDEA scores may therefore reflect local
coherence within individual solve attempts rather than cumulative reasoning fidelity across the full clinical trajectory.
This distinction is particularly relevant if reasoning traces are used to calibrate clinician trust in model outputs.

Wysocki et al. showed that AI-generated explanations can accentuate model over-reliance and confirmation bias
among healthcare professionals rather than support critical assessment of model limitations. 33 Plausible-sounding
rationales that accompany incorrect conclusions could therefore paradoxically increase overtrust rather than mitigate
it.
These findings have implications that extend beyond clinical medicine into the broader understanding of how large
language models reason under uncertainty. The convergence between LLM and human novice failure modes -
despite fundamentally different architectures and developmental histories - suggests that information-seeking deficits
may be an emergent property of systems that learn primarily from complete-case presentations, whether through
medical education or training corpora. Static benchmarks remain valuable for assessing encoded knowledge but
do not capture the sequential decision-making that determines real-world performance. The gap identified here,
between what models know and what they seek to learn, represents a structural limitation that scaling alone is
unlikely to resolve; the positive correlation between model release date and performance (R = 0.51, p = 0.002)
indicates generational improvement, yet the 68% ceiling suggests a qualitative rather than quantitative barrier.
Several directions follow from these results. First, agentic evaluation paradigms that require active information
gathering should complement static benchmarks as a standard component of AI assessment. Second, training
approaches that explicitly model sequential hypothesis testing and information-seeking under uncertainty may
address the utilization deficit observed here. Third, the faithfulness gap between reasoning trace quality and
diagnostic accuracy warrants dedicated investigation, as plausible but incorrect reasoning chains may paradoxically
increase user overtrust rather than support critical evaluation of model outputs.

Limitations
This study has limitations. The benchmark comprises 20 cases in a single subspecialty, all representing complex
scenarios involving relapsed, refractory, or post-transplant presentations. Performance on routine hematologic
conditions or in other medical specialties may differ substantially. Differential diagnosis and treatment recommen-
dations are inherently more subjective than the primary diagnosis, and acceptable answers vary across clinical
settings and geographic regions, as treatment algorithms in the United States differ from those in Europe. Defining a
definitive gold standard at this level of subspecialty complexity is therefore difficult, though some reference standard
remains necessary for systematic evaluation. The approach taken here, expert-authored references reviewed by
independent subspecialists, represents one solution but does not eliminate this ambiguity. The LLM-as-judge
scoring approach, while validated against expert hematologist review (quadratic weighted 𝜅 = 0.804), may introduce
systematic bias in nuanced partial-credit decisions, and using a single model family (GPT-5 Mini) as judge could
introduce model-specific scoring tendencies. All models were accessed through a single API provider with
identical system prompts, and performance could differ with provider-specific optimizations, agentic scaffolding, or
retrieval-augmented generation. The benchmark architecture supports custom agent workflows, but implementing
model-specific optimizations for each of 32 candidates was beyond the scope of this evaluation. Finally, the
benchmark evaluates text-based reasoning only and does not capture the visual information from imaging, pathology
slides, and bedside assessment that informs real clinical decisions.

Conclusion
Across 32 frontier large language models evaluated in complex hematologic oncology, the primary performance-
limiting factor was not medical knowledge but a systematic failure to seek the clinical information on which
treatment decisions depend. Information utilization was the strongest predictor of accuracy, yet models exhibited
search satisficing, anchoring and premature closure - the same failure modes documented in novice human clinicians
under dual-process models of diagnostic reasoning. The convergence of these failure patterns across fundamentally
different cognitive architectures suggests that information-seeking under uncertainty represents a general challenge
for systems trained primarily on complete-case presentations. Addressing this limitation will require training
paradigms that explicitly model active information gathering and iterative hypothesis testing, moving beyond the
passive interpretation tasks on which current systems excel.

Methods
Study Design
OncoRounds is an interactive, turn-based benchmark for evaluating LLM clinical reasoning in hematologic oncology.
The benchmark simulates clinical decision-making through three progressive rounds of information disclosure.
Round 1 (outpatient presentation) provides vital signs, chief complaint, and point-of-care diagnostics. Round 2
(inpatient workup) adds imaging, biopsies, and specialized consultations. Round 3 (definitive characterization)
includes full pathology, cytogenetics, and molecular markers. A detailed treatment history, including prior lines of
therapy and refractoriness status, was withheld until Round 3 to prevent premature unblinding of disease subtypes
in earlier rounds. At each turn, models choose between requesting a single additional piece of clinical information
or committing to a diagnostic and treatment solution. This single-request-per-turn constraint enforces clinical
prioritization, requiring models to reason about which information would be most valuable given what is already
known. The benchmark architecture is depicted in Figure 4. Thirty-two models (Supplementary Table 1) were
evaluated across 20 cases (Table 1), each progressing through all three clinical rounds.

Case Development
Twenty clinical cases were developed spanning the spectrum of hematologic malignancies. Each case comprises a
patient profile (demographics, chief complaint, vital signs), round-specific clinical settings describing the available
diagnostic capabilities, a set of information items gated by round availability, and a reference standard for each
round. Reference standards consist of a working diagnosis, differential diagnoses, and a treatment plan in which
each action is annotated with its intention (therapeutic, diagnostic, or supportive) and priority level (1 through 3).
Information items represent clinical data obtainable in each round's setting: complete blood counts, coagulation
studies, and bedside imaging in the outpatient round; tissue biopsies, immunophenotyping, and focused imaging
during inpatient workup; and molecular panels, cytogenetics, and measurable residual disease assessments in the
definitive phase. Cases were developed de novo by a board-certified hematologist (KB) with 10 years of experience,
rather than adapted from published case reports or public repositories, to minimize the risk of data leakage into
model training corpora. They were then sent out to board certified hematologist (MH, LKS, AB, TW, KT, IM,
SK) for feedback and suggestions for treatment and differential diagnosis. Each expert evaluated three cases.
Discrepancies in clinical judgement were resolved in discussion.

Models Evaluated
Thirty-two models from 12 developers were evaluated: Claude (Haiku 4.5, Sonnet 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.5, Opus
4.6; Anthropic PBC, San Francisco, CA, United States), GPT (4.1, 5 Mini, 5 Nano, 5.2 Instant, 5.2 Reasoning,
OSS-20B, OSS-120B; OpenAI, San Francisco, CA, United States), Gemini (3 Flash, 3 Pro, 3.1 Pro; Google LLC
Mountain View, CA, United States), Kimi (K2 thinking, K2.5; Beijing Moonshot AI Technology Co., Ltd., Beijing,
China), DeepSeek (V3.2, V3.2 Speciale; Hangzhou DeepSeek Artificial Intelligence Basic Technology Research
Co., Ltd., Hangzhou, Zhejiang, China), MiniMax (M2.1, M2.5; MiniMax Group Inc, Shanghai, China), Qwen
(Qwen3 Max, Qwen3 Next 80B, Qwen3 235B 22B; Alibaba Group Holding Limited, Hangzhou, Zhejiang, China),
Grok (4, 4.1 Fast; X.AI Corp., California, United States), GLM (4.7, 5; Zhipu AI, Beijing, China), Mistral (Large,
Medium 3.1; Mistral AI SAS, Paris, France), Llama 3.3 70B (Meta Platforms, Inc., Menlo Park, California, U.S),
and Arcee Trinity Large (Arcee AI, Venice, FL, United States). Total parameter counts ranged from 20 billion to
approximately one trillion, encompassing both proprietary and open-weight architectures. Of the 32 models, 17
produced full chain-of-thought thinking traces, six provided only summary-style reasoning outputs (classified as
obscured), and nine produced no reasoning traces. All models were accessed via unified API (OpenRouter) using
identical system prompts and response schemas.

Automated Evaluation Pipeline
Each model's natural-language information requests were mapped to standardized case information items by a parser
(GPT-5 Mini). The parser received the candidate's free-text request together with the list of available information
items and their round-availability constraints, and returned a structured mapping indicating whether the request was
valid and which item, if any, it corresponded to.
Solution attempts were evaluated by a judge ensemble of five parallel GPT-5 Mini instances. Each judge
independently compared the candidate's working diagnosis, differential diagnoses, and treatment plan against the


                                                          7
round-specific reference standard, assigning one of three labels to each element: correct (clinically accurate and
complete), partially correct (directionally correct but incomplete or imprecise), or incorrect. For the working
diagnosis, the correct label required an exact match or accepted synonym; partially correct was assigned for the
correct disease family with a missing critical modifier. Each candidate differential diagnosis was independently
labeled against the reference set, regardless of how many the model proposed. Treatment plan items were scored
across three sub-dimensions: action (whether the intervention matched the reference), intention (therapeutic,
diagnostic, or supportive), and priority (scored correct if matching, partially correct if off by one level, incorrect if
off by two or more). Final labels were determined by majority vote across the five judges. When no label achieved
a strict majority, partially correct was selected as the tiebreaker, reflecting a conservative approach to borderline
assessments. The judge ensemble was calibrated against independent scoring by a board-certified hematologist (KB)
across all 20 cases (1,007 paired evaluation items spanning all three clinical rounds) to verify alignment on labeling
criteria and partial-credit boundaries. Because the three scoring labels (correct, partially correct, incorrect) form
an ordered scale on which a two-category disagreement carries greater clinical consequence than a one-category
disagreement, quadratic weighted Cohen's 𝜅 was used to assess inter-rater agreement, yielding substantial agreement
(𝜅 = 0.804). The AI judge was systematically stricter than the human expert, with 10.7% of ratings being AI-stricter
versus 7.1% expert-stricter. The most frequent source of disagreement was the AI downgrading expert partially
correct ratings to incorrect (48 items).

Scoring
Overall performance was quantified as balanced accuracy, defined as the unweighted mean of three category-level
accuracies: diagnosis, differential diagnosis, and treatment. Labels were converted to numeric scores of 1.0
(correct), 0.5 (partially correct), and 0.0 (incorrect), and category accuracy was computed as the ratio of earned
points to maximum possible points, expressed as a percentage.
For differential diagnoses, a greedy best-N matching strategy was applied: all candidate labels were sorted by
quality (correct first, then partially correct, then incorrect) and only the top N were counted toward the score, where
N equaled the number of reference differentials. A model proposing 10 differentials against five references would
thus have its five best-matching labels scored. To discourage list-padding, a penalty of one point was subtracted for
each candidate differential beyond a threshold of max(10, 2N), with the total floored at zero. Treatment was scored
identically using the same best-N selection and anti-gaming penalty applied to the three sub-dimensions (action,
intention, priority) jointly, yielding a maximum of three points per reference treatment item. Scores accumulated
across all three clinical rounds, so that the final score reflected performance from the outpatient presentation through
definitive characterization.

Clinical Reasoning Evaluation
Clinical reasoning quality was assessed using an adapted version of the Revised-IDEA (R-IDEA) rubric. 20 The
rubric evaluates four dimensions: Interpretive Summary (I; 0 to 4 points), reflecting synthesis of clinical data into a
coherent narrative; Differential Diagnosis (D; 0 to 2), assessing breadth and prioritization of diagnostic hypotheses;
Explanation (E; 0 to 2), capturing mechanistic reasoning linking findings to diagnoses; and Alternatives (A; 0
to 2), evaluating consideration of competing hypotheses and disconfirming evidence. Dimension scores sum to
a composite total ranging from 0 to 10, with six or above considered high-quality reasoning. This analysis was
restricted to the 17 models that produced full thinking traces, yielding 1,008 model-case-round entries. Evaluator
agents (Claude Opus 4.6) independently scored each thinking trace given the clinical scenario and scoring. A
random sample of 50 entries was independently reviewed by the authors (KKB) to verify agent scoring. Bootstrap
95% confidence intervals for mean R-IDEA scores were computed using 10,000 replicates with the percentile
method.

Cognitive Error Taxonomy
Reasoning errors were annotated using a taxonomy adapted from Croskerry's dual-process model of clinical
reasoning. 21 The taxonomy distinguished cognitive biases from process errors. Cognitive biases comprised
anchoring bias (fixating on an initial impression despite contradictory information), confirmation bias (selectively
attending to supportive evidence), premature closure (accepting a diagnosis before adequate verification), availability
bias (overweighting easily recalled diagnoses), and search satisficing (terminating the diagnostic search after finding
one plausible explanation). Process errors comprised hallucinated information (incorporating findings not present


                                                           8
in the case data), logical contradiction (making mutually incompatible claims), and over-differentiating (generating
excessively broad differential lists without stratification). Three error types captured by the benchmark's existing
scoring mechanism and information-gathering analysis were excluded: incomplete workup, missed key findings,
and incorrect diagnosis. Each error was graded on a four-level severity scale (low, medium, high, critical) based on
potential clinical impact, and was judged against the information available to the model at the time of the error
rather than retrospectively against the complete case. Error annotations were produced by the same evaluator agents
(Claude Opus 4.6) used for R-IDEA scoring, applied simultaneously during trace evaluation.

Information Gathering Analysis
Information-gathering strategy was characterized by two metrics computed per model-case-round entry. Request
precision was defined as the proportion of valid requests (those successfully matched to an available information
item and released to the model) out of total requests made. Information utilization was defined as the proportion of
available information items in a given round that were actually requested by the model.

Diagnostic Trajectory Analysis
Diagnostic trajectories tracked changes in working diagnosis accuracy from the first to the final clinical round.
Model-case pairs with valid diagnosis labels across all three rounds were classified into four mutually exclusive
categories: sustained correct (correct at both R1 and R3), recovered (incorrect at R1 but correct at R3), deteriorated
(correct at R1 but incorrect at R3), and sustained incorrect (incorrect at both R1 and R3). Partially correct diagnoses
were grouped with incorrect for trajectory classification.

Statistical Analysis
Pearson correlations with one-sided tests assessed associations between model-level information-gathering metrics
(request precision and information utilization). R-IDEA scores and overall benchmark accuracy. Kruskal-Wallis
tests evaluated differences in number of requests across clinical rounds. Pairwise Wilcoxon rank-sum tests with
Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons were used for post-hoc group comparisons of R-IDEA
scores and error rates. The maximum sample space comprised 1,920 model-case-round entries (32 models by 20
cases by three rounds), of which 1,896 were completed (634 in R1, 631 each in R2 and R3). R-IDEA evaluation
encompassed 1,008 entries across the 17 models with full thinking traces. Diagnostic trajectory analysis included
630 complete model-case triads. A p value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Supplementary Appendix: Reasoning Trace Excerpts
Introduction
This supplementary appendix presents 49 curated reasoning trace excerpts from 17 large language models,
programmatically selected from 1,066 R-IDEA evaluation entries. Each excerpt includes the clinical context, the
evaluator’s assessment, and a verbatim passage from the model’s internal reasoning trace.
Excerpts are organized into three sections:
    • Error Type Taxonomy (26 excerpts), illustrating all 7 cognitive error types identified during evaluation
    • R-IDEA Dimension Analysis (16 excerpts), contrasting low and high performance on each of the four R-IDEA
      dimensions
    • Exemplary Reasoning (7 excerpts), showcasing high-fidelity clinical reasoning (total R-IDEA ≥ 9, no errors)
Selection methodology. Excerpt selection and annotation were performed with the assistance of Claude Opus 4.6
(Anthropic). A Python script scored candidates by evaluator quote availability, error severity, explanation quality,
and model diversity, then extracted verbatim passages from the corresponding thinking trace files. For error type
excerpts, candidates were scored by evaluator quote availability, error severity, explanation quality, and model
diversity. For R-IDEA dimensions, the two lowest- and highest-scoring entries per dimension were selected with
family-level diversity constraints. Exemplary excerpts required perfect or near-perfect scores (total ≥ 9) with no
identified errors, diversified across cases and model families. All excerpts were reviewed by the authors for accuracy
and representativeness.

Error Type Taxonomy
This section presents excerpts illustrating the cognitive error types identified during R-IDEA evaluation, organized
into two categories following Croskerry’s dual-process taxonomy. Three error types captured by the benchmark’s
scoring mechanism (incomplete workup, missed key findings, incorrect diagnosis) are excluded here, consistent
with the main error taxonomy analysis.
