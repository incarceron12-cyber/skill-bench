  Document Owner: Principal Architect, Security Engineering                                              Revision Date: 2024-05-12
  Scope: Internal Severity Classification Protocol
  About on-call. On-call has its own rhythm: anomalies do not pick convenient hours, and much of the work is sifting context
  under partial information. The discipline is patience, including waiting for the right people to weigh in before deciding.
  Disposition log. When a ticket is opened, the on-call’s job is to assign a severity tier and write a disposition log for it. The log
  records the tier and the findings the on-call gathered from the various roles that resulted in the severity-classification decision.
  How we usually classify. Before deciding, the team usually tries to gather three inputs:
  • whether SRE has confirmed a benign explanation for the anomaly;
  • whether Security Engineering has confirmed unauthorized access to customer-data stores;
  • whether Threat Intelligence is seeing high-confidence active-exploitation indicators.
  The picture these three inputs paint shapes the tier. If SRE has tied the activity back to a benign cause, such as a deploy whose
  timing lines up, a configuration change, or a noisy rule, the team usually calls it sev_4_informational. If there is no clean
  benign explanation and Security Engineering has confirmed unauthorized access to customer-data stores, the tier is normally
  sev_1_critical; this is the high-impact end. When the benign question is unsettled, access is not confirmed, but Threat
  Intelligence is seeing active-exploitation indicators, the team usually uses sev_2_high: active and dangerous, even if the ultimate
  impact is still being established. When none of those is firmly true—no clean benign explanation, no confirmed access, and
  no clear exploitation signal—the tier tends to be sev_3_suspicious, the in-between bucket while the team keeps watching.
