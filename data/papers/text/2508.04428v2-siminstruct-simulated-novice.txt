                                               Abstract. High-quality, multi-turn instructional dialogues between novices
                                               and experts are essential for developing AI systems that support teach-
                                               ing, learning, and decision-making. These dialogues often involve scaf-
                                               folding—the process by which an expert supports a novice’s thinking
                                               through questions, feedback, and step-by-step guidance. However, such
                                               data are scarce due to privacy concerns in recording and the vulnerability
                                               inherent in help-seeking. We present SimInstruct, a scalable, expert-in-
                                               the-loop tool for collecting scaffolding dialogues. Using teaching devel-
                                               opment coaching as an example domain, SimInstruct simulates novice
                                               instructors via LLMs, varying their teaching challenges and LLM’s per-
                                               sona traits, while human experts provide multi-turn feedback, reasoning,
                                               and instructional support. This design enables the creation of realistic,
                                               pedagogically rich dialogues without requiring real novice participants.
                                               Our results reveal that persona traits—such as extroversion and intro-
                                               version—meaningfully influence how experts engage. Compared to real
                                               mentoring recordings, SimInstruct dialogues demonstrate comparable
                                               pedagogical relevance and cognitive depth. Experts also reported the pro-
                                               cess as engaging and reflective, improving both data quality and their
                                               own professional insight. We further fine-tuned a LLaMA model to be an
                                               expert model using the augmented dataset, which outperformed GPT4o
                                               in instructional quality. Our analysis highlights GPT4o’s limitations in
                                               weak reflective questioning, overuse of generic praise, a condescending
                                               tone, and a tendency to overwhelm novices with excessive suggestions.

                                               Keywords: LLM · synthetic data · proof-of-concept · pedagogy.


                                        1     Introduction
                                        Scaffolded multi-turn dialogues, where support is gradually adjusted based on
                                        learner need, form the basis of effective learning in many domains [31, 22]. These
                                        interactions foster metacognition, adapt to individual needs, and encourage re-
                                        flective reasoning rather than direct answer delivery. Scaffolding plays a central
                                        role not only in formal education like mathematics instruction [5], but also in
                                        informal and professional development contexts—including clinical supervision,

counseling, peer mentoring, and coaching—where nuanced feedback and situated
reasoning are essential [27].
    As interest in AI for education grows, so does demand for language mod-
els that provide multi-turn scaffolding rather than straightforward fact delivery.
OpenAI’s recently introduced Study Mode is one such example: a tutoring-like
interface that uses Socratic prompts and scaffolded feedback across multiple
turns to foster active reflection and deeper learner engagement 1 . Other promis-
ing systems—such as CLASS and KG-RAG—demonstrate scaffolded, stepwise
QA and measurable gains in education [25, 6].
    Despite the demand, authentic scaffolding dialogues remain elusive at scale.
Because these interactions are often private, ethically sensitive, and highly context-
dependent—especially in education—they are rarely documented or generaliz-
able [14]. Moreover, traditional data collection relies on post-hoc annotation of
small recordings or static LLM pipelines that omit expert intent and situated
pedagogical reasoning [14, 9]. As a result, coaching-style instructional dialogue
datasets remain scarce.
    In this work, we introduce SimInstruct, a responsible and scalable tool for
generating expert–novice scaffolding dialogues using arge language model(LLM)-
simulated personas. Human experts engage in multi-turn conversations with a
wide range of LLM-simulated novices, offering guidance, reflection, and instruc-
tional reasoning. This expert-in-the-loop design produces pedagogically rich,
contextually grounded dialogue data while avoiding the ethical and logistical
challenges of recruiting real novices. We evaluate the quality of 123 dialogues
from 18 experts by comparing them to real coaching interactions in teacher de-
velopment programs and find that SimInstruct achieves comparable quality.
    Our contributions are as follows: (1) We propose SimInstruct, a novel
expert-in-the-loop and responsible data collection tool that leverages LLM-simulated
novices to elicit rich, multi-turn instructional dialogues grounded in scaffolding
theory. (2) We evaluate the collected dialogues through both qualitative feed-
back and quantitative analysis. By comparing our data with real expert–novice
recordings and fine-tuned LLMs on the augmented dataset, we demonstrate im-
proved instructional quality and pedagogical relevance. (3) We distill practical
lessons for involving human experts in high-quality dataset creation and offer
actionable insights for building domain-specific AI.
    Among the many domains and subjects where scaffolding is critical, we fo-
cus on teacher development as our case study. In both preservice training and
ongoing professional learning, expert–novice coaching helps educators interpret
classroom events, make instructional decisions, and develop their pedagogical
identity [10, 28]. Recent research shows that existing tools often fall short in
addressing the real-world complexities of teaching, with educators expressing
a preference for AI systems that offer more contextualized and reflective sup-
port [3]. We answer the research question–How effectively does the use of LLM-
simulated novices support the generation of high-quality scaffolding dialogues?

2     Background and Related Work
2.1   Human–AI Collaboration in Dataset Collection
Human–AI collaboration has been widely applied in dataset creation, especially
in annotation workflows where humans curate or label AI-generated content,
as seen in projects like Soylent and Garbage-in, Garbage-out [2, 8]. With the
rise of large language models (LLMs), newer pipelines generate synthetic data
across domains including healthcare [16], math tutoring [26], and instruction
tuning [30].
    Despite these advancements, many pipelines treat LLMs as autonomous gen-
erators, with humans only reviewing or filtering content after generation [14]. Ap-
proaches such as GLAN [15] and MIDI-Tuning [29] improve consistency through
structured prompting and multi-round tuning but still fall short in integrating
domain expertise or pedagogical objectives.
    In contrast, recent HCI research demonstrates how domain experts can play
a more central role in AI-supported workflows. Yang et al.[32] emphasize the
co-construction of datasets with AI to enhance interpretability and contextual
alignment. Collaborative tools like DocuViz [12] visualize writing dynamics to
scaffold expert coordination, while Mechanical Novel [11] shows how experts and
non-experts can co-author content through iterative reflection and revision.

2.2   Scaffolding in Teaching and Learning Systems
Effective teaching involves more than delivering correct answers. Scaffolding
strategies—such as metacognitive prompting, formative feedback, and adaptive
coaching—help learners construct knowledge and build autonomy [23, 4]. Yet
many conversational AI systems prioritize factual accuracy or task completion,
reinforced through training methods like reinforcement learning from human
feedback [20].
    Such systems risk “answer-leaking,” which can bypass productive struggle
and hinder learning [13]. Although datasets like MathDial incorporate step-by-
step reasoning [26], they fall short of modeling the coaching-based interactions
essential in teacher development. Our work builds on these insights by simulat-
ing expert–novice scaffolding dialogues that reflect authentic instructional goals.
Recent benchmarks like ClarifyBench assess multi-turn reasoning but lack ped-
agogical context [17].

2.3   Expert–Novice Dialogues in Professional Development
Expert–novice interactions are central to professional learning in fields such as
education, counseling, and clinical supervision [24, 7]. In teacher development,
coaching conversations help novices interpret classroom events, navigate uncer-
tainty, and cultivate their pedagogical identity. However, capturing these di-
alogues at scale remains difficult due to privacy concerns and logistical con-
straints [21].

    Beyond supporting novice growth, such interactions benefit experts as well
by promoting reflection and reinforcing instructional judgment. This dual im-
pact aligns with the learning through teaching paradigm, which suggests that
teaching others enhances the teacher’s own understanding by requiring knowl-
edge reorganization, articulation of tacit reasoning, and anticipation of learner
needs [1, 4]. These benefits have been documented across teacher preparation,
medical supervision, and design-based learning [7, 24]. Recent work has begun
to explore how LLM-simulated learners can support expert tasks. For exam-
ple, Generative Students [18] introduces simulated student profiles to aid item
evaluation in assessment design, illustrating how synthetic learners can enhance
expert workflows.


3     Method

We adopted a human-centered design approach to develop SimInstruct, a web-
based tool for collecting multi-turn instructional dialogues between human ex-
perts and LLM-simulated novice instructors. From January to June 2025, we
conducted weekly design sessions with two senior teacher development experts
to iteratively refine the tool’s interface, persona generation, and study protocol.
In July 2025, human experts used SimInstruct asynchronously, generating dia-
logues. We evaluated the tool through two lenses: (1) content analysis of the col-
lected dialogues and their suitability for fine-tuning AI models (AI perspective);
and (2) expert feedback on usability and perceived realism (HCI perspective).
All procedures were approved by the Institutional Review Board.


4     SimInstruct Tool Design & Development

4.1   Design Goals

G1: LLM-Simulated Novices to Mitigate Privacy Risks We initially
aimed to collect traditional expert–novice teaching dialogues via audio record-
ing, but privacy concerns, scheduling difficulties, and worries that being recorded
might inhibit authentic and often vulnerable help-seeking limited us to being able
to collect just four sessions (around 200 minutes) over two months. To address
                                                                SimInstruct       5

ethical and logistical barriers, SimInstruct uses LLMs to simulate novice in-
structors to talk with human experts, enabling the scalable collection of realistic
dialogues without relying on sensitive or identifiable data. These four real dia-
logues were later used to check the quality of the dialogues collected with our
tool, for example in Fig 2.
    G2: Conversational Data Collection to Capture Expert Reasoning
Naturally We chose a conversation-based approach to data collection because it
better captures the dynamics of real instructional interactions and allows experts
to express their reasoning more naturally, engagingly and flexible than through
static annotations or scoring tasks.
    G3: Persona-Based for Diverse Contexts and Questions We created
diverse, pedagogically meaningful personas to simulate novices with varied sub-
ject areas, experience levels, and instructional challenges, enabling the genera-
tion of dialogue data across a broad range of teaching contexts. We also varied
personality traits, such as introversion and extroversion, to further diversify con-
versational behaviors and content. The goal was to make the dialogues resemble
authentic novice–expert interactions.

4.2   SimInstruct Data Collection Tool Details
SimInstruct is designed to simulate realistic novices, enabling human teach-
ing experts to engage in conversations that resemble typical coaching interac-
tions—offering an alternative to real instructors, thereby addressing G1. To sup-
port G2, we developed a web-based conversational user interface (UI) (Full ver-
sion in Fig.6) where experts interact with an LLM-simulated novice through
multiple dialogue turns. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the simulated novices are as-
signed diverse personas with traits relevant to the teacher coaching domain,
addressing G3. Prompts can be found in Appendix A.
    Below, we provide more details on the three key components of LLM-Simulated
Novice. 1 Persona Profile Generator Each simulated novice is assigned a
persona comprising nine randomly selected attributes for Domain Profile: first
name, last name, classroom context, teaching experience, discipline, course level,
semester context, teaching style, and conversation style. Then for Personal-
ity Profile: Teaching and conversation styles are defined using four of the Big
Five personality traits—openness, conscientiousness, extroversion, and agree-
ableness [19]—which are randomly assigned. Neuroticism was excluded as it
was considered irrelevant to our task. A Domain Challenge was then randomly
selected from a 40 item list created by human expert. To ensure internal con-
sistency, a verification function using GPT-4 checks each generated profile for
logical coherence (e.g., avoiding mismatches like a law professor assigned to a
laboratory classroom). 2 Initial Question Once the persona is generated,
GPT-4 is used to produce an initial question, which is presented to the human
expert. The expert must respond based solely on the information provided, elic-
iting further details through follow-up questions. This interaction design reflects
a realistic coaching scenario and supports G2. 3 Follow-up Responses For
subsequent turns, GPT-4-turbo-preview generates responses aligned with the
6      Authors Suppressed Due to Excessive Length

persona profile and initial question to behavior coherence. The expert continues
the dialogue with the simulated novice until they determine the conversation is
complete and a viable teaching strategy has been identified—or that the novice
appears ready to implement and satisfied with experts’ suggestion.
   When engaging with the tool, experts are presented with a welcome message
from the simulated novice, which includes the novice’s first and last name along
with the initial question. Experts interact with a unique set of novice profiles,
with the option to delete any conversations they find unrealistic. To refine the
generation process, the two human expert was asked to review and evaluate 30
randomly selected novice profiles which included the persona and initial question.
The expert’s feedback was incorporated to enhance realism and coherence of the
generated profiles.

4.3   Data Collection Study with Human Experts
Eighteen human experts based in the US, all with extensive coaching experience
in higher education, participated in the study. They were recruited through email
and word-of-mouth to ensure their expertise and experiences. All expert had ad-
vanced degree. Human experts completed their dialogues asynchronously over
a two-week period. Compensation was based on the number of completed dia-
logues, with per-dialogue pay calculated according to the estimated time taken
(5 to 20 minutes) and the participant’s hourly rate of 50 USD. Throughout the
study, participants were encouraged to communicate with the research team to
ask questions, raise concerns, and share positive experiences. At the end of the
study, all participants were invited to complete a brief post-hoc survey to provide
feedback on the tool, the quality of the dialogues, and their experience.

5                      Evaluation of SimInstruct
5.1                       SimInstruct Collected Data vs. Real Dialogues
We compared four dialogues collected using our tool with four real, face-to-face
coaching sessions totaling of 200 minutes. While this comparison is limited in
scope due to the small sample size, it offers preliminary insights into both the
promise and limitations of using LLMs to simulate novice behavior. As shown in
Fig. 2, approximately half of the collected dialogues fall within the turn range
observed in real dialogues, while the other half exhibit fewer turns. We did not
compare word counts, as face-to-face oral dialogues often include filler words and
self-correction language, that are not typically present in text communication.

Findings We found that the structural scaffolding in both types of dialogues
was similar. In particular, both data included the three-step scaffolding struc-
ture: identifying a problem, reasoning about it, and suggesting a strategy. The
average scores across the four criteria were consistently lower for the SimIn-
struct collected data compared to the face-to-face recorded data. As shown
in Fig. 9, recorded dialogues consistently received scores of 3 across all criteria,
while the SimInstruct collected data had slightly lower averages. A closer look
at the score distribution of the SimInstruct data revealed more specific pat-
terns as seen in Fig. 4. While the ratings show that the tool-generated dialogues
as containing high-quality pedagogical features, the LLM-simulated novices in
these dialogues sometimes appeared overly straightforward and lacked contextual
grounding of own challenges. This suggests that the simulated novice responses
may not yet fully replicate the complexity of human novices.


5.2                  Collected Data to Finetune Expert Model

While our tool collected a set of high quality expert-novice dialogues, the dataset
still remains relatively small and insufficient for fine-tuning of language models.
To address this limitation, we decided to augment the data using synthetic gen-
eration strategies.
                                                               SimInstruct       9

Data Augmentation Starting with a seed dataset of 123 collected dialogues,
we use GPT-4o mini to generate over 1,000 synthesized dialogues within a con-
sistent domain. In each iteration, we randomly sample three real dialogues from
the seed set as examples and prompt the language model to generate a new
multi-turn dialogue in the same format and with a similar tone and style, but
beginning with a different initial question. This process leverages the model’s
in-context learning capabilities. After synthesis, we filter out any dialogues that
do not match the required format, resulting in a final set of 1,415 dialogues.


Finetune Expert Model using Augmented Data We fine-tuned the Llama-2-7b-chat-hf
model using the full augmented dataset described previously, consisting of 1,415
multi-turn scaffolding dialogues. After splitting these into individual turns, we
obtained a total of 9,271 training examples. Fine-tuning was performed on a
single NVIDIA A100 GPU for 435 steps, taking approximately 1.5 hours. We
used a learning rate of 2 × 10−5 , weight decay of 0.01, warmup ratio of 0.05,
and cosine learning rate scheduling. Optimization was done using the AdamW
optimizer from PyTorch.


Human Annotators’ Rating of Expert Model We conducted a human
evaluation of 220 AI-generated instructional dialogues, with the source model of
each dialogue kept hidden and shuffled. Two human annotators independently
rated the expert responses for each dialogue. We used a rubric developed by our
research team with extensive expertise in teaching and AI. The rubric focuses
on how effectively expert responses coach novice instructors, using four criteria
on a 1-3 scale (full definitions provided in Appendix D): Clarity of Expression,
Supportive & Appropriate Tone, Reflective Prompting, and Appropriateness of
Validation. To assess inter-rater reliability across these dimensions, we computed
quadratically weighted Cohen’s κ on the pooled ratings. LLaMA achieved a κ
of 0.65, indicating substantial agreement, while GPT reached a moderate agree-
ment level with a κ of 0.53. See Fig. 10 for detailed agreement matrices.


Findings As show in Fig 5, LLaMA outperformed GPT4o across all four eval-
uation dimensions. The most pronounced difference was in reflective prompting,
where LLaMA received a substantially higher mean score (M = 2.67, SD =
0.55) than GPT4o (M = 1.76, SD = 0.56), indicating that LLaMA was signif-
icantly more effective at encouraging novices to reflect or reason. In terms of
clarity of expression and supportive tone, LLaMA again showed stronger per-
formance, achieving mean scores of 2.62 on both, compared to GPT4o’s 2.12
and 2.00, respectively. Finally, for appropriateness of validation, LLaMA scored
2.59 (SD = 0.57) while GPT4o scored 1.92 (SD = 0.49), suggesting LLaMA pro-
vided more balanced and contextually appropriate validation without overusing
generic praise. These results indicate that LLaMA’s responses were consistently
clearer, more supportive, more reflective, and more appropriately validating than
those of GPT4o.

5.3     Human Expert’s Experience of Using the Tool
Expert participants reported a positive experience using the tool, as reflected
in both informal interactions and a post-study survey. The lightweight, asyn-
chronous interface was described as easy to navigate, and the conversation-based
format aligned with their coaching practices. Survey responses indicated that
most participants found the simulated scenarios realistic and the task intellectu-
ally engaging. Several noted that the process prompted reflection on their own
coaching styles and approaches to feedback. Experts appreciated the flexibility
to skip scenarios, and many expressed interest in using similar tools for training
or professional development.

Encouraging Expert Participation Through Framing and Flexible Task
Design Framing the task as a meaningful contribution to the future of AI in ed-
ucation was key to recruiting and sustaining expert engagement. We aligned the
task with familiar elements of their daily routines and avoided presenting it as
a generic data labeling exercise. Instead, we emphasized that their participation
would directly inform how AI tools support real-world instruction—positioning
their expertise as central to AI design. To accommodate diverse work styles and
schedules, we decided to do flexible compensation options, including task-based
(per-dialogue) payment. This flexibility signaled respect for expert labor and
contributed to higher engagement and completion rates.

Engaging with AI as Reflective Learning Opportunity Creating novel
and intellectually engaging data collection tools was essential for meaningful ex-
pert participation. Rather than assigning routine annotation tasks, we invited
experts to engage in authentic, multi-turn instructional dialogues that mirrored
real coaching practice. This positioned them as co-designers contributing peda-
gogical insight, rather than passive data labelers. Participants had full autonomy
to skip, revise, or decline prompts they found unproductive, reinforcing respect
for their professional judgment. This opt-out flexibility supported sustained en-
gagement and high-quality contributions.
    Many human experts described the task as more reflective and rewarding
than traditional data collection efforts. The interactive format encouraged deeper
professional reflection and engagement. These findings suggest that expert-centered
design—grounded in authenticity, flexibility, and intellectual value—is essential
for responsible and effective data collection in education and beyond.

5.4   Promises and Challenges of LLM for Teaching and Learning
Our results show that dialogue turns and word counts span a wide range. No-
tably, Fig. 3 illustrates how extroverted and introverted LLM-simulated novice
personas influenced expert behavior—even when the dialogues were similar in
length and number of turns. This highlights the importance of persona design in
creating realistic data and suggests that personality traits can shape how human
experts engage with LLMs and the kinds of responses they provide.
    Another challenge, illustrated in Fig. 4 and reinforced by expert feedback, is
that the LLM-simulated novice sometimes posed overly straightforward instruc-
tional challenges—ones that could be resolved with a single suggestion. Experts
observed that human novices are more likely to say “no” or push back on sug-
gestions, whereas the LLM consistently agreed, even when they are prompted
to decline when appropriate. As a result, coaching the LLM felt easier but less
authentic than coaching real novices for human experts. These gaps underscore
the need for future LLM systems to better simulate disagreement and dialogic
complexity, especially since LLMs are typically optimized to be agreeable re-
sponders.
    Third, Fig. 5 shows that GPT was less capable of asking meaningful, reflective
questions—an essential aspect of effective teaching that supports metacognitive
thinking in learners. This raises important concerns about the extent to which
AI, especially current GPT, can serve as a good teacher. In addition to limited
reflective prompting, we observed other problematic behaviors such as overusing
generic praise, adopting a condescending tone, and overwhelming learners with
an excessive number of suggestions. These patterns may hinder the learning
process.
    Last, human-human face-to-face dialogues also often included more turns as
shown in Fig. 2 and shorter utterances like “keep going,” which were absent
in any dialogues collected in our tool. These brief responses, often tied to non-
verbal cues, signaled idea-seeking and created natural pauses. It opens a new
discussion on how human-human data can or cannot can be used to train LLM
doing similar things as human.
    Limitations For this study, we selected teacher coaching as the example
domain, rather than subjects like mathematics. While math-based tasks of-
ten emphasize factual correctness, teacher coaching focuses on reasoning an re-
flection that are more broadly transferable across instructional contexts. That
said, transferring SimInstruct to other domains still requires domain-specific
adaptation. Persona profiles and challenges must be defined in collaboration
with domain experts to ensure realism. Additionally, the domain-specific lan-
guage—including how challenges are described, what counts as effective feed-
back, and how success is framed—would need to be re-authored to reflect norms

6    Conclusion

SimInstruct offers a responsible and scalable approach for collecting expert–novice
scaffolding dialogues using LLM-simulated personas. Our findings demonstrate
that expert engagement, thoughtful persona design, refined evaluation criteria,
and expert-in-the-loop dialogue generation can yield pedagogically meaningful
data while addressing common privacy and ethical concerns. Fine-tuning LLaMA
with this augmented dataset improved its instructional quality over GPT, partic-
ularly in reflective prompting—its ability to ask thoughtful questions. However,
challenges remain: LLM behaviors such as excessive agreement, user-pleasing
tendencies, and limited contextual nuance hinder realistic simulation.
