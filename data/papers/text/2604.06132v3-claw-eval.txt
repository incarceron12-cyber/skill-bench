                                                                                        Abstract
                                                    Large language models are increasingly deployed as autonomous agents for multi-
                                                    step workflows in real-world software environments. However, existing agent
                                                    benchmarks are limited by trajectory-opaque grading, underspecified safety and ro-
                                                    bustness evaluation, and narrow coverage of modalities and interaction paradigms.
                                                    We introduce Claw-Eval, an end-to-end evaluation suite addressing these gaps
                                                    with 300 human-verified tasks spanning 9 categories across three groups: general
                                                    service orchestration, multimodal perception and interaction, and multi-turn profes-
                                                    sional dialogue. To enable trajectory-aware grading, each run is recorded through
                                                    three independent evidence channels: execution traces, audit logs, and environ-
                                                    ment snapshots, yielding 2,159 fine-grained rubric items. The scoring protocol
                                                    evaluates Completion, Safety, and Robustness, with Average Score, Pass@k, and
                                                    Pass^k across three trials to distinguish genuine capability from lucky outcomes.
                                                    Experiments on 14 frontier models show that: (1) Trajectory-opaque evaluation is
                                                    systematically unreliable, missing 44% of safety violations and 13% of robustness
                                                    failures detected by our framework. (2) Capability does not imply consistency, with
                                                    Pass@3 remaining stable under error injection while Pass^3 dropping by up to 24
                                                    percentage points. (3) Agent capability is strongly multi-dimensional, with model
                                                    rankings varying across task groups and metrics, indicating that our heterogeneous
                                                    evaluation coverage is essential. Claw-Eval highlights directions for developing
                                                    agents that are not only capable but reliably deployable.


                                        1    Introduction
                                        Large language models [25, 3, 7] have rapidly evolved from conversational assistants [28, 55]
                                        into autonomous agents [49, 32] capable of executing complex, multi-step workflows [33, 29] in
                                        real-world software environments [10, 39]. Modern agent harnesses, such as Claude Code [2] and
                                        OpenClaw [26], equip LLMs with the ability to call tools, navigate file systems, query databases,
                                        and orchestrate actions across multiple applications, transforming the central evaluation question
                                        from whether a model possesses knowledge to whether it can reliably accomplish a goal through
                                        proactive situated action. This shift demands evaluation methodologies that go beyond static question-
                                        answering benchmarks and instead assess agents in live, interactive environments where success
                                        depends not only on what an agent produces, but also on how it gets there.
                                        Recent benchmarks have made important strides toward this goal, evaluating agents across sandboxed
                                        tool-use environments [13, 20], GUI-driven desktop workflows [39, 40], and real-world coding-agent

pipelines [11, 8]. Yet three critical gaps remain that limit the diagnostic power of current evaluation
practices. (G1) Trajectory-opaque grading. Many benchmarks verify only the model’s output,
without systematically auditing the execution that produced it. This makes faithful execution hard to
distinguish from fabricated or shortcut-based behavior, and leaves output-only evaluation vulnerable
to reward hacking [35, 18]. (G2) Underspecified safety and robustness. In deployment, agents
must not only satisfy task objectives but also adhere to safety constraints and recover from transient
failures. Although prior work has introduced dedicated safety benchmarks and trace-based risk
evaluations, safety is still rarely embedded into ordinary end-to-end workflow tasks, and robustness
under controlled perturbations such as timeouts or rate limits is seldom evaluated [51, 54, 34]. (G3)
Narrow task coverage. Real-world agents must handle heterogeneous scenarios spanning service
orchestration, multimodal perception, and extended dialogue within the same deployment, yet most
benchmarks focus on a single modality or interaction paradigm, such as text-based tool calls [13],
command-line execution [20], or GUI navigation [39].
In this paper, we introduce Claw-Eval, an end-to-end evaluation suite that addresses all three gaps
within a unified framework, organized around three corresponding design principles. (1) Full-
trajectory auditing. Every agent action is recorded through three independent evidence channels,
namely execution traces, service-side audit logs, and post-execution environment snapshots, enabling
grading that verifies what the agent actually did. (2) Integrated multi-dimensional scoring. The
scoring protocol evaluates Completion, Safety, and Robustness as coupled dimensions within the
same task execution. Completion is decomposed into fine-grained rubric items grounded in auditable
evidence. Safety constraints are embedded within normal workflow tasks and robustness is measured
through controlled error-rate injection that simulates realistic deployment perturbations. (3) Unified
heterogeneous task coverage. Claw-Eval uses a single declarative task schema to support a broad
range of heterogeneous agent settings under a shared execution and grading protocol. Across these
components, tasks and fine-grained rubrics are curated and verified by human annotators to ensure
coverage of diverse modalities, interaction paradigms, and deployment-relevant scenarios. Because
agentic execution is inherently stochastic, the evaluation protocol further runs each task over multiple
trials and reports complementary metrics for average performance, capability ceiling, and reliability
floor, providing a more deployment-relevant view of agent performance.
Our experiments across 14 frontier and open-weight models yield the following contributions and
findings: (1) Benchmark and evaluation protocol. We introduce Claw-Eval, an end-to-end evalua-
tion suite of 300 human-verified tasks spanning 9 categories. The framework combines full-trajectory
auditing, unified cross-modal coverage, integrated scoring over Completion, Safety, and Robustness,
and multi-trial evaluation to distinguish average performance, best-case capability, and reliability,
with 2,159 independently verifiable rubric items. We fully release the benchmark and evaluation
framework, making all components open-source and reproducible. (2) Evaluation validity. Eval-
uation design materially affects benchmark conclusions. A vanilla LLM judge misses 44% of the
safety violations and 13% of the robustness failures detected by our hybrid grading pipeline, showing
that trajectory-opaque evaluation is systematically unreliable. Robustness under perturbation cannot
be adequately characterized by single-facet metrics: Pass@3 remains relatively stable under error
injection, whereas Pass^3 drops by up to 24 percentage points. (3) Capability findings. Agent
capability is strongly multi-dimensional. Model rankings shift substantially across heterogeneous
task settings, with no single model dominating all domains. In multi-turn dialogue, success depends
far more on what the agent asks, i.e., whether its questions are targeted and logically sequenced
(r=0.87), than on how long the conversation runs (r=0.07).
Taken together, these results show that trustworthy agent evaluation requires trajectory-level evidence,
multi-dimensional scoring, and broad task coverage working in concert to capture the full complexity
of autonomous behavior. The capability gaps surfaced by Claw-Eval point to concrete and actionable
directions for building agents that are not only technically capable but also reliably deployable.

Evaluation methodology. Output-only grading cannot detect cases where agents fabricate intermedi-
ate steps yet produce plausible artifacts [15, 44, 45]. The LLM-as-a-judge paradigm [42, 41, 43] scales
to open-ended tasks but lacks auditability. Recent work addresses individual gaps: TheAgentCompany
adds sub-task checkpoints, τ -bench separates correctness from consistency via Pass^k, and safety
benchmarks such as ToolEmu [31], R-Judge [51], Agent-SafetyBench [54], and MobileRisk-Live [34]
assess risk awareness over traces. However, no existing framework embeds safety constraints within
normal workflow tasks, and none supports controlled error injection for robustness testing. Claw-Eval
addresses both by combining deterministic checks with LLM judgment, grounding rubric items in
auditable trajectory evidence, and providing error injection as a first-class evaluation parameter.

3     Claw-Eval
In this section, we present Claw-Eval, whose architecture is shown in Figure 1. Its core premise is
that trustworthy agent evaluation must ground scores in evidence of what the agent actually did, not
what it claimed to have done. §3.1 describes the three-phase execution lifecycle, §3.2 introduces the
300 tasks across 9 categories under a unified schema, and §3.3 explains how the resulting evidence is
converted into multi-dimensional scores for cross-model comparison.

3.1   Auditable Execution Pipeline

To close the trajectory-opacity gap (G1), Claw-Eval makes the full run, rather than the final answer
alone, the object of evaluation. At the pipeline level, each run is organized into three temporally
separated phases: Setup, Execution, and Judge. This phase separation establishes a strict boundary
between task solving and evaluation: the agent receives only task-solving resources during execution,
while evaluation resources are isolated until judging time.
To operationalize this pipeline, we design an auditable agent scaffold inspired by commonly used
agent frameworks, such as Claude Code [2] and OpenClaw [26]. The scaffold enforces the pipeline
boundary through fresh per-run sandboxes, controlled tool interfaces, and evidence collectors that
operate outside the agent’s view. It produces three independent evidence channels: structured

3.2   Cross-Modal Task Suite

Real-world agents encounter radically diverse scenarios, from executing practical work to processing
visual media and conducting multi-turn dialogues, often within the same deployment. A benchmark
that covers only one modality or interaction paradigm cannot predict how an agent will perform in the
others. The three-phase lifecycle of §3.1 is deliberately domain-agnostic: it provisions environments
and collects evidence without interpreting what any task means. Built on this lifecycle, the benchmark
comprises 300 tasks spanning 9 fine-grained categories, organized into three complementary groups:
General, Multimodal, and Multi-turn Dialogue, as shown in Table 2. Additional per-group details are
provided in Appendix C, and benchmark curation and release details are provided in Appendix D.
General tasks evaluate an agent’s ability to accomplish practical workflow objectives, ranging from
single-service queries to cross-service coordination and multi-system workflows. These tasks are
curated by human annotators to reflect deployment-relevant operational scenarios, with task goals and
rubrics designed to capture both successful completion and compliance with embedded constraints.
Multimodal tasks evaluate active perception and generation over rich media, including videos,
documents, images, and code-generated visual artifacts. These tasks are curated by human annotators
to cover diverse perceptual and generative demands, with rubrics constructed to assess both functional
correctness and output quality under auditable evidence.
Multi-turn Dialogue tasks assess an agent’s ability to conduct professional consultations through
multi-round interactions across domains such as STEM, social science, and business. These human-
curated tasks center on hidden-intent user scenarios that require active clarification and information
gathering, with rubrics evaluating both questioning strategy and final answer quality.
Though these three groups test radically different capabilities, they all instantiate the same three-
phase lifecycle of §3.1. In each case, scoring evidence is collected independently of the agent during
execution and revealed only at judging time, enabling direct comparison across settings and extension
to new domains without changing the core pipeline.

3.3   Scoring Protocol

The execution architecture of §3.1 produces complete behavioral traces and three independent lines
of evidence; the 300 tasks of §3.2 span service workflows, multimodal processing, and multi-turn
professional dialogue. The remaining challenge is converting this rich evidentiary record into scores
that are comprehensive, precise, and reliable. Claw-Eval addresses each requirement through a
corresponding mechanism: a multi-dimensional scoring structure defines what to measure, a fine-
grained rubric system grounds each measurement in verifiable evidence, and a multi-metric evaluation
protocol ensures that conclusions withstand stochastic variance. We further validate the evaluation
pipeline through complementary human studies (Appendix F).
Multi-dimensional scoring. Claw-Eval scores each task attempt along three dimensions: Comple-
tion, which measures how fully the agent satisfies the task objective; Safety, which measures whether
policy constraints are respected throughout execution; and Robustness, which measures recovery
from transient environmental failures. These combine into a final task score:

Fine-grained rubrics. Each task is decomposed into independently verifiable rubric items grounded
in the evidence sources from §3.1. Rubric items are either deterministic checks for objective
conditions, such as file contents, tool parameters, or forbidden actions, or judgment-based checks
using an LLM judge for open-ended criteria, such as text quality, reasoning coherence, or visual
fidelity. This yields an audit trail from final scores to the underlying behavioral evidence. Across the
benchmark, the 300 tasks comprise 2,159 rubric items, averaging 7.2 items per task.
Evaluation metrics. Because agentic execution is stochastic, we evaluate each task over k trials
and report three complementary metrics: Average Score, the mean score across trials; Pass@k, the
fraction of tasks passed at least once, measuring capability ceiling; and Pass^k, the fraction passed
on every trial, measuring reliability floor. A large gap between Pass@k and Pass^k indicates limited
consistency. Formal definitions are given in Appendix E.

4     Evaluation
In this section, we evaluate 14 frontier models (§4.1) under a uniform protocol (§4.2) and report main
results across all three task groups (§4.3), followed by targeted analyses in §5.

4.1    Evaluated models

We evaluate 14 frontier models across ten families: Claude-Opus-4.6 [3], Claude-Sonnet-4.6 [4], GPT-
5.4 [25], Gemini-3.1-Pro [7], Gemini-3-Flash [6], Qwen3.5-397B-A17B [30], MiMo-V2-Pro [23],
MiMo-V2-Omni [22], GLM-5-Turbo [53], GLM-5V-Turbo [1], DeepSeek-V3.2 [16], MiniMax-
M2.7 [24], Kimi-K2.5 [36], and Nemotron-3-Super [5]. All 14 models are evaluated on General
(161 tasks) and Multi-turn (38 tasks); Multimodal (101 tasks) is restricted to the 9 vision-capable
models (Opus/Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5.4, Gemini-3.1-Pro/3-Flash, Qwen3.5, MiMo-V2-Omni, Kimi-K2.5,
GLM-5V-Turbo). Each model is evaluated under the same scaffold and tools.

4.2    Evaluation settings

All models are accessed with default parameters except temperature=0 and thinking enabled where
supported; each task runs in an isolated Docker sandbox for 3 independent trials with error injection
rate 0. Gemini-3-Flash (temperature=0) serves as the LLM judge for General and Multimodal rubric
items requiring open-ended assessment; for Multi-turn, Claude-Opus-4.6 (temperature=0.7) acts as
both the simulated user and the judge, the elevated temperature producing more varied user behavior.
We report Score (average task score), Pass@3 (any trial exceeds 0.75), and Pass^3 (all three pass).
Human validation of rubric adequacy and judge alignment is reported in Appendix F.

5                        Analysis

This section presents four targeted analyses probing the three evaluation gaps motivating Claw-Eval:
hybrid vs. vanilla LLM judging (§5.1, G1), controlled error injection (§5.2, G2), and cross-modal
coverage via multi-turn and multimodal breakdowns (§5.3, §5.4, G3).

5.1                        Trajectory-opaque judges miss 44% of safety violations

We investigate whether a single vanilla LLM judge call can match the hybrid grading pipeline. To
ensure a fair comparison, we provide the vanilla judge (Gemini-3-Flash) with the full conversation
transcript including every tool call, together with the complete grader source code. The only
information withheld is server-side audit logs and post-execution environment snapshots (§3.1).
Across five models and 2,000+ traces, the vanilla judge systematically underdetects issues caught by
the hybrid pipeline (Figure 3): it misses 12/27 (44%) safety violations and 15/118 (13%) robustness
issues. The hybrid grader detects safety violations via deterministic matching on tool-call param-
eters, whereas the LLM judge cannot reliably apply the same rule from text alone and sometimes
rationalizes the agent’s behavior. Robustness issues are missed less often because they often surface
as visible error codes in the conversation. These error rates support the hybrid design: rule-based
checks for deterministic safety-critical criteria and LLM judges for open-ended assessment where no
deterministic rule exists. Blinded human adjudication further supports this result (Appendix F.1).

5.2                        Injected failures erode consistency far more than peak capability

To understand how agents behave when tool calls intermittently fail, we evaluate three models on
General tasks at error-injection rates of 0.0, 0.2, 0.4, and 0.6 (Figure 4); each mock-service call
independently fails with that probability, drawn from HTTP 429 (35%), HTTP 500 (35%), or a 2–4 s
latency spike (30%). The central finding is that error injection primarily degrades consistency, not
peak capability. From rate 0.0 to 0.6, Pass@3 is nearly flat while Pass^3 drops sharply across different
models, indicating that models can usually find one successful path but rarely all three.

5.3                         Better questions, not more, yield better multi-turn performance

The 38 multi-turn dialogue tasks simulate professional consultations in which a user persona progres-

5.4             Multimodal capability is domain-specific: no model dominates

The 101 multimodal tasks span three domains (Video: 53, Doc & Image: 22, Code: 26), evaluated on
9 models. Table 5 reports per-domain Pass^3. No single model dominates: Video is led by Claude-
Opus-4.6/Sonnet-4.6 (15.4%), Doc & Image by GPT-5.4 (54.5%), Code by MiMo-V2-Omni (33.3%).
Rank shifts are substantial: GPT-5.4 leads overall yet ranks lower on Video, while MiMo-V2-Omni
leads Code despite a lower overall rank. Domain-level breakdown is therefore necessary.
Aggregating across models (Figure 6), capability is unevenly distributed: Video Pass^3 averages only
10.7% vs. Doc & Image 32.3% and Code 23.9%. Consistency also varies, measured by the conversion
ratio ρ = Pass^3/Pass@3: Video 0.37, Code 0.48, Doc & Image 0.53, suggesting tasks with higher
perceptual uncertainty exhibit greater run-to-run variance. The two panels reveal orthogonal findings:
which domain a model excels at is model-specific, while how reliably it excels is domain-specific.

6              Conclusions

We present Claw-Eval, a transparent evaluation suite that assesses LLM agents along completion,
robustness, and safety via full-trajectory auditing, cross-modal tasks, and controlled perturbations.
Across 14 models, trajectory-opaque judging misses 44% of safety violations and 13% of robustness


                                                                                                               9
issues caught by the hybrid pipeline; capability does not imply consistency as Pass^3 drops by up to 24
percentage points under error injection while Pass@3 remains stable; question quality explains 76%
of multi-turn Pass^3 variance versus under 1% for round count, and no single model leads across all
multimodal domains. These results argue for prioritizing consistent error recovery, domain-targeted
multimodal perception, and interaction quality over raw scale in future agent development.

A     Limitations
We identify the following boundaries of the current study.
Perturbation Coverage. The current robustness evaluation focuses on infrastructure-level transient
failures (HTTP 429/500, latency spikes), which are the most frequent perturbations in production
API deployments. The framework’s error-injection mechanism is extensible to semantic-level or
adversarial perturbations, which we leave to future work.
Judgment Calibration. Open-ended rubric items rely on LLM judges, which achieve 95% exact
agreement with human scoring (Appendix F.3). While this demonstrates practical reliability, model-
based judgment remains an approximation that may not generalize to all future task types.
Benchmark Longevity. As with any public benchmark, future models may be optimized against
Claw-Eval’s task distribution. The trajectory-level evidence channels and deterministic safety gates
provide built-in mechanisms for detecting shortcut-based gaming, and the declarative task schema
supports continual expansion to maintain evaluation headroom.

B     Detailed Execution Lifecycle
This appendix provides additional implementation details for the execution pipeline summarized in
Section 3.1. We describe the phase-by-phase runtime behavior of Claw-Eval and list the built-in tool
inventory exposed to agents during execution in Table 6.

B.1   Setup Phase

In the Setup phase, the framework reads the task definition and provisions a fresh sandbox container
for the run. Task resources such as datasets, documents, starter code, and media assets are placed
into the container to form the agent’s working environment. If the task requires external services, the
framework also launches the corresponding mock services outside the sandbox and exposes them
only through designated tool interfaces. These services may emulate platforms such as CRM systems,
email gateways, scheduling backends or knowledge bases. From the moment they start, they record
all incoming requests in service-side audit logs.
This phase establishes a clean separation between task solving and evaluation. By the time execution
begins, the container contains the resources necessary to attempt the task, but none of the grading
artifacts, reference answers, or verification utilities used later for judging.

B.2   Execution Phase

In the Execution phase, the agent attempts the task by interacting with the environment exclusively
through tools. The runtime exposes two capability layers. The first is a fixed system layer that
supports code execution, file operations, codebase search, web interaction, and multimodal media
processing. The second is a task-specific service layer that provides custom APIs for the mock
services declared during setup. The complete tool inventory is listed in Table 6.
Throughout execution, the framework records the full agentic context in a structured execution trace
maintained outside the sandbox. This trace includes the sequence of tool calls and observations
produced during the run, and serves as one of the primary evidence sources during grading. Because
the trace is collected externally, it remains invisible to the agent while the task is being solved.

B.3   Judge Phase

In the Judge phase, the framework introduces grading artifacts into the container only after the agent
has terminated. These artifacts may include evaluation scripts, reference answers, and verification
utilities used to inspect the final state of the environment. At this point, the framework can render
generated webpages, execute verification code, and collect artifacts produced by the agent.
The final score is grounded in three evidence channels assembled at judging time. The execution
trace provides the complete interaction history. The service-side audit logs record the requests that
were actually received by external services together with their parameters. The environment snapshot

C     Additional Task Group Details

We provide additional details for the three task groups summarized in Section 3.2. Although they
differ substantially in surface form, all three are instantiated through the same execution lifecycle and
declarative task schema.

C.1   General Tasks

General tasks target practical workflow execution and are organized by difficulty, from single-service
queries at the Easy level, through cross-service coordination at Medium, to multi-system workflows
at Hard. They include both service-orchestration scenarios, where the agent interacts with mock
services such as CRM, email, or scheduling systems through task-specific APIs, and standalone
analytical scenarios, where the agent works primarily with injected files and built-in sandbox tools.
A subset of tasks further embeds explicit safety constraints, such as forbidden communication or
credential exposure, allowing safety to be evaluated under ordinary task-completion pressure rather
than in isolation. Judging is grounded in service audit logs, environment snapshots, or both.

C.2   Multimodal Tasks

Multimodal tasks evaluate perceptual and generative capabilities over videos, documents, images, and
code-generated media. For Video and Document & Image tasks, the agent must actively choose what
to inspect, such as which segments, frames, or pages to examine, before reasoning over the retrieved
visual content. Code tasks further require the agent to generate visual artifacts, including dynamic
webpages, SVG animations, and edited video clips, that satisfy functional requirements. Depending
on the task, outputs are judged through deterministic matching, LLM-based visual assessment, or a
combination of both.

C.3   Multi-turn Dialogue Tasks

Multi-turn dialogue tasks assess professional consultation through interaction with a simulated user
across domains such as STEM, social science, and business. Each task specifies a user persona with
domain knowledge, a hidden intent, and an information-revealing strategy that withholds key facts
unless the agent asks the right questions. The agent must therefore probe actively, clarify ambiguities,
and synthesize partial information over multiple turns. Judging is based on the dialogue trace and
scores both information-gathering quality and final answer quality against the persona’s hidden
ground truth. Across all three task groups, the same execution lifecycle and declarative task schema
are used, which allows heterogeneous settings to be evaluated under a shared evidence-grounded
protocol.

D     Dataset Curation and Governance

This appendix describes how Claw-Eval was constructed, filtered, validated, and released. It com-
plements Appendix C, which describes the content of each task group, by focusing here on task
provenance, authoring workflow, rubric design, pilot validation, decontamination, and human review.


D.1   Authoring Pipeline

Each task passes through three stages before entering the final release.

(1) Task design. A task author first defines the target capability, writes the task prompt, specifies
the required tools or assets, and drafts the initial scoring plan. Of the 300 released tasks, 250 (83.3%)
are entirely original designs authored from scratch, covering all three task groups. The remaining 50
tasks (16.7%) are drawn from established benchmarks, with 10 from OfficeQA [27], 14 from Pinch-
Bench [11], 12 from FinanceQA, 5 from OneMillion-Bench [48], 5 from Video-MME [9], and 4 from
LongVideoBench [38], and integrated into Claw-Eval with their original prompts and scoring criteria
preserved. These compatibility tasks serve two purposes: they demonstrate that the framework’s
declarative schema and execution lifecycle can subsume heterogeneous existing benchmarks without
modification to their evaluation semantics, and they enable direct cross-benchmark comparison on
shared task instances. All 50 adapted tasks required reimplementation of the runtime environment
(mock services, sandbox provisioning, evidence collection) to fit the three-phase execution pipeline
of §3.1.

(2) Environment and rubric implementation. The runtime environment is then implemented,
including mock services, sandbox assets, or simulated-user setup as needed. In parallel, the scoring
plan is formalized into deterministic checks, judgment-based rubric items, or a hybrid of both.

(3) Review and pilot validation. A second author reviews the task specification, environment, and
rubric for correctness, completeness, and potential leakage. The task is then pilot-tested on multiple
frontier models to verify that the environment behaves as intended, the rubric distinguishes successful
from unsuccessful behavior, and the task is neither trivially easy nor practically impossible. Tasks
that fail review or pilot validation are revised or removed.


D.2   Final Released Benchmark

Only tasks that pass the above process enter the released benchmark. The final release contains 300
tasks with 2,159 rubric items across three task groups and nine fine-grained categories. For each task,
we release the task definition, environment implementation, grading rubric, and associated evaluation
files, so that the benchmark can be independently inspected, reproduced, and extended.


D.3   Reproducibility

To support independent verification and extension, we release the complete evaluation framework as
open-source software. The release includes:
       • All 300 task definitions, including prompts, tool specifications, sandbox configurations, and
         environment setup.
       • All grader implementations with deterministic checks and judgment-based rubric logic.
       • Task fixtures (documents, videos, starter code, mock service data).
       • The sandbox execution runtime (Dockerfile, server, dispatcher).
       • Evaluation scripts for end-to-end reproduction of all reported results.
The full codebase, data, and leaderboard are publicly available at https://claw-eval.github.io.
Researchers can independently inspect any rubric item and evaluate new models using the provided
infrastructure.

E.1   Multi-dimensional Task Score.

For each task attempt, Claw-Eval computes a score from three dimensions: Completion, Safety, and
Robustness. The overall task score is
                                                                             
                          score = ssafety × α · scompletion + β · srobustness ,
where α + β = 1 controls the relative importance of task completion and error recovery. We use
α = 0.8 and β = 0.2 in all experiments, prioritizing successful task completion while still rewarding
recovery under perturbation.
Completion is aggregated from task-specific rubric weights that reflect the relative importance of
different sub-goals. Safety is implemented as a multiplicative gate rather than an additive term, so
that policy violations directly suppress the final score. Most safety constraints are embedded within
normal workflow tasks rather than isolated into standalone safety-only evaluations.
Robustness is measured through controlled error injection. Let Terrored be the set of tool types that
encounter at least one injected error, and let Trecovered ⊆ Terrored be the subset for which the agent

This formulation measures recovery breadth across failure types rather than retry count.

E.2   Fine-grained Rubrics.

Each task is decomposed into a set of independently verifiable rubric items, each corresponding to a
concrete behavioral criterion. Rubric items fall into two categories.

       • Deterministic checks. These verify objective conditions such as whether a required file
         exists with expected fields, whether a specific API is invoked with correct parameters, or
         whether a forbidden action appears in the audit log.
       • Judgment-based checks. These use an LLM judge to score open-ended outputs such as
         response quality, reasoning coherence, or visual fidelity against a reference.

All rubric items are grounded in the independent evidence sources described in §3.1, namely execution
traces, service audit logs, and environment snapshots, rather than the agent’s self-report. Each item
stores the artifact that supports its verdict, enabling an audit trail from the final score to the dimension-
level breakdown, the underlying rubric items, and the corresponding behavioral evidence.
Across the full benchmark, the 300 tasks yield 2,159 rubric items, with a mean of 7.2 items per task.

F     Human Validation of the Evaluation Pipeline

Because Claw-Eval combines deterministic verification, task-specific rubrics, and model-based
judgment, the credibility of its benchmark conclusions depends on the credibility of the evaluation
pipeline itself. We therefore conduct three complementary human studies, each targeting a distinct
threat to validity. First, we perform blinded adjudication on cases where the hybrid pipeline and
a trajectory-opaque vanilla judge disagree, in order to test whether the hybrid pipeline is closer to
human judgment. Second, we audit the adequacy and evidence coverage of judgment-based rubrics,
in order to test whether the rubrics faithfully operationalize the capabilities each task is intended to
measure. Third, we measure alignment between the deployed LLM judges and human scoring on
sampled judgment-based rubric items, in order to test whether model-based judging is reliable in
practice.

F.1   Blinded Human Adjudication on Hybrid–Vanilla Disagreement Cases

The disagreement analysis in the main text shows that the hybrid pipeline and a trajectory-opaque
vanilla LLM judge diverge on a non-trivial set of safety and robustness cases. Disagreement alone,
however, does not establish which system is closer to human judgment. We therefore conduct a
blinded adjudication study on all 27 hybrid-only cases underlying the misses reported in the main
text, including 12 safety-related cases and 15 robustness-related cases. To calibrate the adjudication
protocol and assess its behavior beyond disagreement-only examples, we additionally include a
matched control set of 100 jointly labeled cases, consisting of 50 jointly positive cases and 50 jointly
negative cases.
Each case is independently reviewed by two human annotators. Annotators are provided with the full
evidence bundle required to assess the disputed criterion, including the task description, the execution
trace, service-side audit logs, environment snapshots, and produced artifacts when applicable. To
avoid label leakage, annotators do not see whether a case was flagged by the hybrid pipeline or by the
vanilla judge, nor whether a case belongs to the disagreement set or the control set.
For each case, annotators determine whether the disputed safety violation or robustness failure should
be considered present under the task rubric. When the two annotators disagree, the final label is
resolved through adjudication. We then compare the adjudicated human outcome against the label
produced by each grading system. Table 7 summarizes the results.
Human adjudication aligns overwhelmingly with the hybrid pipeline on these disagreement cases.
On the 12 safety disagreements, the adjudicated outcome agrees with the hybrid pipeline in all
cases, whereas the vanilla judge agrees with human judgment in none of them. On the 15 robustness
disagreements, the hybrid pipeline agrees with human judgment on 13 cases (86.7%), while the vanilla
judge agrees on only 1 case (6.7%). On the matched control cases, where the two systems initially
agree, human adjudication is used only to calibrate the protocol rather than to distinguish between the
two systems; in these cases, the jointly assigned labels are confirmed at high rates by the annotators.
Taken together, these results support a narrower but important claim: for criteria that depend on
external audit signals or post-execution state, the hybrid pipeline is a more evidence-grounded
approximation than judging from the conversational trace alone.
A recurring failure mode of the vanilla judge is that it can read the rule text but cannot reliably
execute deterministic matching over the relevant evidence, especially when violations are only visible
in service-side logs or in post-execution artifacts rather than in the conversational trace itself. In
contrast, the hybrid pipeline directly grounds such criteria in auditable evidence channels, which
reduces under-detection on these cases.

F.2   Human Audit of Rubric Adequacy and Coverage

A distinct validity question concerns the judgment-based rubrics themselves. Even a perfectly
consistent judge would yield a weak evaluation if the rubric failed to capture the intended capability,
omitted key success conditions, or relied on evidence unavailable to the grader. We therefore
conduct a human audit of rubric adequacy and coverage for tasks that contain judgment-based rubric
components.
We draw a stratified sample of 180 tasks, including 100 from General, 50 from Multimodal, and 30
from Multi-turn. For each sampled task, two human auditors review the task specification, the full
rubric, and the evidence available to the grader, including execution traces, audit logs, environment
snapshots, and produced artifacts when applicable.
For each task, auditors answer three structured questions: (1) whether the rubric is aligned with the
capability the task is intended to measure; (2) whether the rubric adequately covers the task’s key
success conditions; and (3) whether the rubric criteria are grounded in evidence actually available to
the grader. Each criterion is coded as satisfied or not satisfied at the task level based on the auditors’
independent review and adjudicated discussion.
Table 8 summarizes the audit results. Across all three task groups, the vast majority of sampled tasks
are judged to have rubrics that are well aligned with the intended capability, adequately cover the
key success conditions, and are grounded in available evidence. This supports the use of the current
rubric design as a reasonable operationalization of the benchmark’s evaluation targets.
Qualitative inspection of the audited tasks suggests that the remaining issues are concentrated in a
small number of borderline cases, typically involving under-specified partial-credit boundaries or
criteria whose scope could be made more explicit. We view these cases as opportunities for further
rubric refinement rather than evidence of systematic weakness in the benchmark design.

All agent rollouts and judge calls were issued through public model-provider APIs; no GPU resources
were used. The full evaluation consumed approximately 2,310 M input and 79 M output tokens, with
an average wall-clock of 212.3 seconds per task trial. The evaluation harness and sandbox ran on a
single cloud VM with 64 vCPU and 512 GB RAM.
