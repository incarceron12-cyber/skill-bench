                                        1   Introduction
                                        Recent advances in large language model (LLM) agents have enabled substantial progress in solving complex
                                        long-horizon tasks that require planning, tool use, and interaction with external environments. A growing line
                                        of work has introduced benchmarks that simulate realistic workflows in web environments, software engineering
                                        tasks, and office applications, including SWE-bench Jimenez et al. (2024), WebArena Zhou et al. (2024),
                                        OSWorld Xie et al. (2024), and more recent workflow-oriented benchmarks such as OdysseyBench Wang
                                        et al. (2025) and CoffeeBench Sugiura et al. (2026). These benchmarks collectively emphasize sequential
                                        decision-making and extended interaction horizons, revealing that current agents still struggle with sustained
                                        execution and robust tool coordination.
                                        Despite these advances, most existing evaluation settings implicitly assume that the entire agentic process
                                        operates within a single linguistic regime. Task instructions, intermediate reasoning, tool interactions, and
                                        final outputs are typically expressed in English, with multilinguality either absent or reduced to surface-level
                                        translation. This assumption simplifies the evaluation pipeline but overlooks an important characteristic of
                                        real-world deployments, where agents must operate over heterogeneous linguistic inputs and outputs within a
                                        single coherent workflow.
                                        In parallel, multilingual NLP research has extensively studied cross-lingual transfer and multilingual gen-
                                        eralization, as reflected in benchmarks such as XGLUE Liang et al. (2020), M-MMLU Lai et al. (2023),
                                        and MGSM Shi et al. (2022). These studies demonstrate that language variation significantly affects model
                                        performance, particularly under low-resource or cross-lingual transfer conditions. However, such evaluations

involve multi-step interaction or tool-based execution.
A key limitation of these two research directions is that they treat multilinguality and agentic reasoning as
orthogonal dimensions. In multilingual benchmarks, language variation is studied in static settings without
execution dynamics. In agent benchmarks, execution complexity is studied under monolingual assumptions.
As a result, an important question remains underexplored: how language variation affects the stability of
reasoning and decision-making trajectories in long-horizon agent workflows.
We argue that multilinguality should not be viewed as a property of inputs or outputs alone, but as a factor
that influences the entire execution trajectory of an agent. In realistic workplace scenarios, agents must
interpret instructions in one language, retrieve or transform information in another language, and produce
structured artifacts in potentially different target languages. This introduces a form of cross-lingual trajectory
coupling, where errors may propagate across reasoning, tool use, and generation stages in ways that are not
captured by existing evaluation paradigms.
To study this phenomenon, we introduce PolyWorkBench, a benchmark for evaluating LLM agents on
multilingual long-horizon workplace workflows. PolyWorkBench consists of 67 tasks spanning five domains,
including commerce, knowledge work, legal analysis, localization, and manufacturing. Each task requires
agents to process heterogeneous inputs such as documents, tables, and multilingual resources, perform iterative
reasoning, invoke external tools, and generate structured outputs.
A central design principle of PolyWorkBench is that language variation is embedded into the execution process
rather than treated as an auxiliary attribute of input data. Agents must maintain semantic consistency across
languages throughout intermediate reasoning steps and final output generation. This setting exposes failure
modes that arise specifically from the interaction between cross-lingual transfer and multi-step execution,
which are not observable in either standard multilingual benchmarks or monolingual agent benchmarks.
We further propose a hybrid evaluation framework combining structural grading, executable verification, and
LLM-based semantic assessment, enabling robust evaluation of both functional correctness and linguistic
consistency.
Empirical results show that state-of-the-art LLM agents experience substantial degradation in multilingual
workflow settings, even when they perform strongly on monolingual long-horizon benchmarks. Our analysis
suggests that multilinguality introduces compounding effects across execution trajectories, affecting not only
comprehension but also planning stability and tool-use reliability. These findings indicate that future agent
evaluation should jointly consider language variation and procedural execution, rather than treating them as
independent dimensions.


2    Related Work
Agent Benchmarks. The emergence of large language models has driven the development of increasingly realistic
benchmarks for evaluating autonomous agents. Early efforts, such as AgentBench Liu et al. (2024), established
unified evaluation across multiple interactive environments, demonstrating the feasibility of assessing LLMs
beyond static language understanding tasks. Subsequent benchmarks expanded evaluation to more realistic
settings, including web navigation (WebArena Zhou et al. (2024)), open-world assistant tasks (GAIA Mialon
et al. (2024)), and desktop computer operation (OSWorld Xie et al. (2024)). Domain-specific benchmarks, such
as SWE-bench Jimenez et al. (2024), MLE-bench Chan et al. (2025), and τ -bench Yao et al. (2024), further
evaluate agents in professional software engineering and tool-use scenarios. More recent workflow-oriented
benchmarks, including Claw-Eval Ye et al. (2026), Claw-Eval-Live Li et al. (2026), WildClawBench Ding
et al. (2026), and ClawMark Meng et al. (2026), emphasize persistent environments, evolving workflows, and
trustworthy evaluation for long-horizon agents. Compared with these benchmarks, PolyWorkBench focuses
on multilingual enterprise workplace tasks that require cross-lingual reasoning, heterogeneous document
understanding, and end-to-end workflow execution across diverse professional domains.
Multilingual and Task-Oriented Benchmarks. Multilingual benchmarks have played an essential role in evaluating
the cross-lingual capabilities of large language models. General-purpose benchmarks, such as FLORES Goyal
et al. (2022), MGSM Shi et al. (2022), M-MMLU Lai et al. (2023), and MMMLU Hendrycks et al. (2020),

Benchmark Evaluation and LLM-as-a-Judge. As benchmarks become more open-ended, evaluation has shifted
from rule-based metrics toward model-based and preference-driven assessment. Traditional metrics such as
exact match are often insufficient for generative and multi-turn tasks. LLM-as-a-judge has therefore emerged
as a scalable paradigm, where strong language models approximate human evaluation.
Representative systems such as MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena Zheng et al. (2023) show that GPT-4-level
models can achieve high agreement with human judgments while enabling large-scale evaluation of open-
ended outputs. Similarly, AlpacaEval Li et al. (2023) uses pairwise ranking to compare instruction-following
performance efficiently across models.
However, prior work has identified limitations including position bias, verbosity bias, and sensitivity to
prompting and model configuration Zheng et al. (2023). These issues raise concerns about robustness,
particularly in complex or long-horizon evaluation settings. Overall, while LLM-as-a-judge is practical and
scalable, reliable evaluation of multilingual and multi-step agent workflows remains an open challenge.

3       PolyWorkBench
PolyWorkBench evaluates LLM agents on realistic long-horizon workplace tasks. Unlike prior benchmarks
that isolate a single capability such as reasoning or tool use, it targets complete task execution in professional
scenarios that require multilingual understanding, iterative reasoning, external tool use, and structured
artefact generation. The benchmark comprises 67 tasks across five workplace domains—Commerce (COM),
Knowledge (KNW), Legal (LEG), Localization (LOC), and Manufacturing (MFG)—and covers ten languages.
Each task is an end-to-end workflow with executable outputs rather than a single-turn question.

3.1       Task Design
Each task is a complete business workflow: the agent receives an instruction with supporting materials—
documents, spreadsheets, logs, contracts, or multilingual resources—and must deliver artefacts that satisfy
predefined requirements.
Tasks require long-horizon reasoning over multiple intermediate steps rather than direct answer generation.
Depending on the scenario, the agent must retrieve information from heterogeneous inputs, perform multilingual
comprehension and translation, invoke external tools, integrate intermediate results, and produce structured
outputs such as reports, spreadsheets, summaries, legal documents, localization artefacts, or operational
analyses.
The five domains cover complementary enterprise workflows. Commerce focuses on cross-border operations,
pricing, logistics, taxation, and marketplace management. Knowledge focuses on information synthesis,
technical reporting, patent analysis, and multilingual fact verification. Legal focuses on compliance analysis,
contract review, regulatory comparison, and legal document drafting. Localization focuses on multilingual
adaptation of software, documentation, subtitles, and marketing material. Manufacturing focuses on quality
management, production reporting, maintenance, safety auditing, and supply-chain analysis.

3.2         Data Overview
PolyWorkBench contains 67 manually curated tasks partitioned into a baseline pool (29 tasks, 6–8 estimated
steps) and a harder stress pool (38 tasks, 8–12 steps). The two pools jointly span difficulty levels 3–6 on a
1–6 scale, with a mean of 8.5 estimated tool-use steps per task and per-task time budgets of 30–60 minutes.
Each domain contains between 11 and 16 tasks (Fig. 2(a,c)).
The corpus covers ten languages: English, Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese, Russian, French, Spanish,
German, and Arabic. Coverage is deliberately trilingual by construction: instruction, source materials,
and expected output are drawn from potentially different languages, so cross-lingual transfer is exercised
throughout the trajectory rather than at a single translation step. Concretely, 59 of 67 tasks (88%) involve
three or more distinct languages across these three roles. Individual language coverage ranges from English
(touched by 66 tasks, mostly as an instruction or output language) to Arabic (1 task); Chinese, Korean,
Japanese, and Vietnamese each appear in 21–51 tasks (Fig. 2(b)).
Each task instance provides an instruction, a heterogeneous input bundle—documents, tables, contracts,
receipts, system logs, localization resources, and business records, with an average of 9.3 input files per
task—executable evaluation scripts where applicable, and a structured reference specification. Instead of
a single reference answer, tasks are scored against fine-grained criteria that capture structural correctness,
functional validity, and output quality. On average each task specifies 3.9 output artefacts (min. 2, max. 9;
total 262). The benchmark is extensible: new tasks can be added without modifying the evaluation framework.

3.3         Evaluation Framework
PolyWorkBench evaluates task completion along three complementary axes: structural grading, executable
verification, and semantic assessment. Each axis targets a failure mode that the others cannot detect on their
own.
Grade measures task completion via task-specific structural rules. Each task defines fine-grained criteria
for the components of the expected output, allowing partial credit on partially completed solutions while
remaining interpretable across heterogeneous artefact types.

3.4       Evaluation Metrics
The primary leaderboard metric is Pass@1, the average Grade across all 67 tasks under a single decoding
sample. Grade is chosen as the ranking signal because it directly reflects overall task completion quality across
heterogeneous workplace scenarios and admits partial credit on complex outputs. Alongside Grade we report
per-task Pytest pass rate and LLM-as-Judge score in [0, 1] where applicable.
Leaderboard entries additionally record the number of runs nruns ; entries with nruns ≥ 2 report Pass@3,
computed as the best-of-k Grade averaged over tasks. Future releases will further report Pass3 to characterise
the stability of autonomous agents under stochastic decoding.

4.2   Main Results
Table 1 reports Pass@1, Pass@3, and per-domain Grade for the 18 representative model×harness pairs on
PolyWorkBench. Three findings stand out. First, the benchmark remains challenging even for frontier models:
the best entry, Claude Opus 4.8 + ClaudeCode, reaches 0.921 Pass@1, and only three entries exceed 0.79; every
other model falls below 0.77. No model is within a few points of ceiling on all five domains simultaneously.
Second, the choice of harness moves scores by 8–24 Pass@1 points on the same underlying model. Claude Opus
4.8 shifts from 0.921 to 0.712 when swapping ClaudeCode for OpenClaw, and DeepSeek-v4-Flash spans 0.239
Pass@1 across four harnesses. This confirms that the agent scaffolding is a first-class variable in benchmark
design. Third, domain profiles are highly non-uniform: strong overall models frequently retain 0.85–0.90
Grade on Knowledge, Legal, and Manufacturing while dropping to 0.50–0.65 on Commerce, indicating that
overall averages hide qualitatively different failure modes at the task-category level.

across all 1,206 observed (entry, task) pairs. Grade and Pytest are strongly aligned (Pearson r = 0.86, not

and only 5.7% lie in the middle band. In particular, 16.7% of scores are exactly 0, indicating that the Judge
emits “pass / near-pass / hard-reject” verdicts rather than a continuous quality score, which mechanically
compresses the range over which any correlation can be computed. Second, if we restrict attention to tasks

Harness sensitivity. Figure 6 isolates the effect of the agent harness by holding the underlying model fixed.

our decision to publish the full model×harness matrix rather than collapsing across scaffolds.

5   Conclusion
In this paper, we introduce PolyWorkBench, a multilingual benchmark for evaluating LLM agents on
realistic long-horizon workflows spanning five representative domains. PolyWorkBench comprises 67 carefully
designed tasks that require multilingual understanding, heterogeneous document processing, structured output
generation, and multi-step tool interaction. To provide reliable and comprehensive assessment, we develop a
unified evaluation framework that integrates task-specific grading, executable verification, and LLM-based
semantic judgment.
Extensive experiments on representative proprietary and open-weight models demonstrate that multilingual
long-horizon workflows remain challenging for current LLM agents. Our analysis further reveals substantial
variation across task domains and languages, underscoring the importance of evaluating multilingual agents
beyond isolated reasoning or single-domain settings. We hope PolyWorkBench will provide a practical
benchmark for measuring progress toward more capable and reliable multilingual LLM agents. As future work,
we plan to continuously expand the benchmark with additional languages, domains, and evolving real-world
workflows, enabling long-term evaluation as multilingual agent capabilities continue to advance.

A.1   Per-Domain Failure Cases
Section 4 noted that Commerce is the systematic weakness of most model×harness pairs in the mid-tier of the
leaderboard. Table 2 lists the eight Commerce tasks on which the largest number of evaluated agents scored
< 0.4 Grade, together with the mean Grade over the failing subset. The pattern is consistent: five of the eight
tasks are failed by ≥ 15 of the 18 evaluated agents. Manual inspection shows that these tasks share the same
structural profile – multi-step numerical reconciliation, currency conversion, or strict spreadsheet schemas
– in which a single arithmetic or format error voids the entire deliverable and yields a near-zero Grade. In
contrast, Knowledge or Legal tasks tolerate partial correctness through structural credit, so the same class
of local error only shaves a few points off Grade rather than collapsing it to zero. This asymmetry is the
mechanical driver of the Commerce dip visible in Figure 4 (a).
Beyond the Commerce cluster, three cross-domain observations are worth recording. (i) Legal is the domain
with the widest Pass@1 spread across models: Opus 4.8/ClaudeCode reaches 0.951 but the median entry
sits at 0.696, indicating that legal analysis remains a differentiator between frontier and open-weight agents.
(ii) Localization is the only domain in which a non-Claude model obtains the second-best score at every
tier (DeepSeek-v4-Flash/Codex, 0.855; Qwen-Agent-World/OpenClaw, 0.822), consistent with the intuition
that localization tasks reward strong multilingual base models more than they reward long-horizon planning.
(iii) Manufacturing is the domain with the highest floor: even the weakest entry in the table (DeepSeek-v4-
Flash/OpenClaw) still scores 0.624, because MFG tasks tend to have shorter step counts and more forgiving
grading criteria.

A.2   Extended Per-Language Results
Table 3 extends Figure 4(b) with per-language mean Grade for two additional model×harness pairs at the
bottom of the leaderboard, so that the reader can see the full range of degradation patterns. The gap between
the strongest language and the weakest language of a fixed model widens monotonically as base capability
drops: Opus 4.8/ClaudeCode has a range of 0.14 (0.822 RU → 0.963 VI), whereas Qwen3.6-35B-A3B/Hermes
has a range of 0.60 (0.352 DE → 0.951 ZH). The Arabic column is uniform at 0.850 because there is a single
Arabic task in the benchmark (n = 1), so it is not informative; it is retained for transparency.
Two failure modes recur inside the low-language cells. Comprehension errors are cases in which the agent
misreads figures or entities in the source language and never recovers – these dominate the Russian and
Spanish drops for mid-tier open models. Cross-lingual coordination errors are cases in which comprehension
is correct but the agent fails to keep source-language content aligned with target-language output across a
multi-step trajectory, so the final artefact drifts even though every intermediate step looked plausible. The
latter mode is what makes multilingual agent evaluation different from static multilingual QA, and it is the
reason PolyWorkBench embeds language variation into the execution trajectory rather than only into task
inputs.
