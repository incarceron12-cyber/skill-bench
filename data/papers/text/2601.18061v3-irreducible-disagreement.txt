1   Introduction
Learning from human feedback (LHF) has become a dominant paradigm for developing modern AI systems.
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) trains reward models on human preference compar-
isons [19, 51]. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) optimizes directly on human judgments [56]. Safety classifiers
like Llama Guard learn from human labels distinguishing safe from unsafe content [33]. Evaluation benchmarks
treat human ratings as ground truth against which model performance is measured [22, 40, 70]. These applications
differ in mechanism but share a common assumption that human judgments, appropriately aggregated, yield
valid signals about what AI systems should do. When humans disagree, the standard response is to average
ratings, treating disagreement as noise reducible through scale [26]. This assumption is rarely tested, and when
reliability is measured, it is rarely reported [15].
   Recent work has demonstrated that even small, high-quality datasets, on the order of 1,000 carefully selected
examples, can be sufficient to fine-tune models for domain-specific tasks [42, 66, 71] or provide necessary
information compared to generic medical exam-style benchmarks [37]. This makes the quality and consistency of
expert annotations critical. If a modest corpus of expert-labeled data can substantially shape model behavior, then
disagreement among those experts has outsized consequences for the resulting system. While recent simulations
demonstrate that even low levels of random preference noise can drastically degrade the performance of reward
models [26], our work suggests a more complex challenge: the “signal” itself is contested. In safety-critical
domains, these inconsistencies are not merely random errors but reflect competing clinical philosophies that,
when averaged, may lead to unpredictable or therapeutically incoherent model behaviors.
   We tested whether expert annotations provide the consistent signal this paradigm requires, focusing on the
safety-critical domain of mental health. AI systems for mental health support are proliferating rapidly [50];
their online nature, low cost, and relative anonymity make them uniquely approachable for users facing diverse
socioeconomic barriers, such as financial constraints, geographic isolation, or lack of insurance. Consequently,
these systems help bridge the gaps in traditional treatment access, providing a vital source for those who
require immediate support during a crisis. However, inappropriate responses of such systems carry serious
consequences, up to fatality, as well as an array of psycho-social harms, such as reinforcing delusions [30],
perpetuating stigma [25, 45] and over-reliance [32, 45]. The safety of these systems often depends on manual
expert evaluation—a small number of specialists review AI outputs and rate their quality, informing model
training, safety filtering, and deployment decisions. What if those experts cannot agree, not due to oversight or
measurement error, but because they hold fundamentally different views about what constitutes a good response?
   Mental health represents a particularly demanding test case for human feedback reliability. The domain is
safety-critical; errors cause real harm, not merely suboptimal user experience. It is longitudinal; consequences
unfold over months and years, invisible at evaluation time. It is irreducibly contextual; the same response may
help one user and harm another depending on characteristics evaluators cannot observe. While clinicians typically
integrate body language, posture, and long-term history to develop treatment plans, LLMs operate solely on
isolated text. This creates a structural information gap that human feedback is tasked with bridging.
   Consequently, evaluation quality must be grounded in symptom self-report, observed behavior, and clinical
expert consensus, rather than objective biological tests. For example, measures such as the Patient Health
Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) for depression [43], rely on self-report and are rarely seen as definitive on their own.
Biological measures are primarily used to rule out alternative explanations or guide treatment rather than to
adjudicate evaluative disagreement [10, 49]. If human feedback can provide reliable signal in any high-stakes
domain, it should do so here, among trained experts with established clinical frameworks and strong motivation for
careful evaluation. We define an evaluator’s “framework” as the internal mental models and decision methodology
by which they practice their clinical craft. While nominally informed by standardized medical guidelines, this
framework is also shaped by idiosyncratic professional, lived, and cultural experiences. It is this latent framework,

rather than an objective, universal ground truth, that current alignment methods attempt to model through
human feedback.
   We developed a safety-focused evaluation dataset in which three psychiatrists evaluated 360 LLM-generated
mental health responses across eight commonly used evaluation factors using a calibrated rubric, producing 1,080
expert annotations. Despite similar professional training and shared annotation instructions, inter-rater reliability
was consistently poor, with ICC ranging from 0.09 to 0.30 which is well below the 0.40 threshold typically
considered minimally acceptable [20]. Despite clearer safety constraints, suicide and self-harm cases produced
the greatest disagreement, likely reflecting different clinical risk tolerances and differing views on how directive
a model should be, while ADHD-related responses, where guidelines are less prescriptive, produced the most
consensus. Quantitative analysis revealed systematic rather than random disagreement. Raters showed consistent
directional biases, with gaps up to 1.69 points on 5-point scales. Qualitative interviews revealed that experts apply
substantively different evaluative frameworks, reflecting genuine disagreement rather than measurement error.
   This paper makes three contributions. First, we provide an existence proof that clinical disagreement transfers
to and persists in AI safety evaluation, where it has direct implications for RLHF, reward modeling, and safety
classification. Prior work has quantified expert disagreement on structured clinical tasks, but our study, to the
best of our knowledge, is the first to combine quantitative reliability analysis with qualitative investigation of the
clinical frameworks driving evaluative judgment of generative AI. Second, we demonstrate that disagreement
concentrates on the most safety-critical items (suicide, self-harm) and is systematic rather than random, with
consistent directional biases (3.5:1 rater asymmetry, gaps up to 1.69 on 5-point scales) that neither aggregation nor
larger annotator pools can resolve. Third, we provide qualitative evidence that disagreement is framework-based
and normative, not resolvable through better measurement. Each expert articulated a coherent clinical philosophy
that produced different ratings of identical responses, meaning the “noise” current alignment methods seek to
average away is actually contested signal. Together, these findings suggest that in safety-critical domains with
longitudinal consequences, human feedback may not provide the stable ground truth that current AI alignment
methods assume. To enable further study of these challenges, we release open-source dataset including both the
raw data along with the expert evaluations1 .

2 Related Work
2.1 Learning from Human Feedback
Human feedback emerged as central to AI development from the recognition that constructing analytic objective
functions for complex tasks is incredibly difficult, yet humans can often recognize good outputs when they see
them. RLHF formalized this intuition; human annotators compare pairs of model outputs, these preferences train
a reward model, which then guides policy optimization[19, 65]. This approach powered significant capability
advances, including instruction-following models [51], with variations proliferating: DPO eliminates the reward
model by optimizing directly on preference data [56]; Constitutional AI uses human-written principles to guide
self-improvement [7]. Beyond training, human feedback underlies safety infrastructure—classifiers like Llama
Guard [33], evaluation benchmarks [70], and red-teaming exercises [53]. These applications share a foundational
assumption that human judgments, appropriately aggregated, yield valid training signal. Yet a review of major
RLHF papers finds inter-annotator agreement statistics absent from most methodology sections; the assumption
of adequate reliability is implicit rather than tested [15].

2.2    Annotator Disagreement
A parallel literature challenges the treatment of disagreement as noise. The CrowdTruth framework argues that
annotator disagreement often reflects genuine ambiguity rather than error, and that information is lost when

labels are aggregated to a single ground truth [4, 5]. Pavlick and Kwiatkowski demonstrated that for natural
language inference, items with high disagreement often reflect linguistic phenomena, pragmatic ambiguity,
underspecification, rather than mere annotation noise [52]. In safety-critical domains, the “aggregation as truth”
assumption becomes more dangerous. The DICES dataset, for instance, found that substantial disagreement in
safety annotations, was systematically predicted by demographic factors [3]. Crucially, prior work by Gordon
and colleagues indicates that expert annotations often fail to resolve these crowd disagreements, instead intro-
ducing new, sophisticated layers of divergence based on professional perspective [29]. This suggests that human
preferences may not represent a stable gold standard for AI alignment, as RLHF inherits the annotator biases in
systematic ways [27, 31].
   More broadly, Cabitza et al. argue for a perspectivist approach to ground truthing in which annotator disagree-
ment is preserved as informative signal rather than collapsed into consensus labels [13]. Similarly, in information
retrieval, trained assessors evaluating document relevance show systematic disagreement that is predictable
from document and topic features rather than reducible through additional assessment [14, 16], and probabilistic
models treat assessor variance as informative uncertainty rather than noise [21].
   Our study extends this literature by shifting the focus from crowdworker classification to expert evaluative
judgment in a clinical safety domain. While prior work examined demographic variation in content moderation,
we provide an existence proof that board-certified psychiatrists fail to achieve reliable consensus on the therapeutic
appropriateness of generative AI. The disagreement we document is framework-based and normative rather than
demographic or perspectival. By documenting this divergence in a domain with longitudinal consequences, we
demonstrate that expert disagreement is a principled, sociotechnical phenomenon: it reflects different clinical
philosophies that cannot be resolved through simple arithmetic aggregation.



2.3   AI in Mental Health
AI applications in mental health have expanded from detecting depression in social media posts [18] to conversa-
tional agents offering therapeutic support [24, 34, 55]. The potential benefits are substantial, given persistent
access gaps, as an estimated 11 years elapse between symptom onset and treatment initiation [68]; AI systems
could help bridge these access gaps. The risks are equally significant; users may be vulnerable, and inappropriate
responses could validate harmful beliefs, reinforce stigma or discourage treatment-seeking, among other potential
harms. Evaluation has struggled to keep pace, with safety evaluation particularly underdeveloped [60].
   A novel challenge is the widespread accessibility of general-purpose foundation models. The general public
increasingly uses off-the-shelf, general purpose LLMs for mental health support, creating a distinct safety-utility
tension [17, 58]. Providing unlicensed diagnosis or therapy is neither ethical nor legal, yet outright refusal, where
models trigger generic disclaimers and decline to engage, can be experienced as invalidating or, in crisis scenarios,
may represent a failure to provide essential bridging resources or even exaggerate symptoms [30].
   Evaluation methodologies have struggled to navigate this tension. While consensus guidelines for assessment
and treatment of mental health disorders exist, including frameworks for assessing risk, these were designed
for human clinicians managing longitudinal care, not for evaluating isolated text outputs from an AI. These
frameworks do not translate directly to the task of rating a single turn of dialogue, leaving evaluators without
customized tools to determine whether a model’s response strikes the appropriate balance between helpful
support and appropriate boundaries. A core challenge is the absence of ground truth, requiring elaborate methods
like uncertainty-aware preference modeling when aggregating expert annotations [e.g. 37]. Whether a response
is “good” depends on theoretical orientation, clinical context, and downstream effects that unfold over time.
Evaluation necessarily relies on expert judgment, making expert reliability a critical and underexamined question.

2.4    Expert Reliability in Clinical Contexts
Psychiatry has long grappled with reliability concerns, providing important context for what we might expect
in AI evaluation. The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM), was motivated partly by
poor agreement among clinicians diagnosing the same patients [35], and the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of
Mental Disorders, 5th Edition (DSM-5) field trials found kappa values ranging from 0.20 to 0.78 depending on
diagnosis [57]. Reliability challenges are particularly acute for safety assessment. A meta-analysis of suicide risk
prediction found clinical judgment performed only marginally better than chance [38].
   These findings establish a baseline expectation for AI evaluation tasks. If clinicians struggle to agree on
diagnoses and risk when they have full access to a patient’s history, presentation, and ongoing relationship, we
should not expect high agreement when evaluating static, isolated AI text excerpts that relate to judgments
about underlying risk. Multiple therapeutic frameworks offer different accounts of what constitutes helpful
communication; appropriateness is context-dependent in ways text-based evaluation alone cannot capture; and
qualities like warmth and attunement resist reduction to operational metrics on a rating scale. The question is
not whether expert disagreement exists, but whether it is substantial enough to undermine the validity of human
feedback as training signal.

2.5    Research Gap
Human feedback methods presume reliable ground truth but rarely test reliability. Annotator disagreement
research focuses primarily on crowdworkers performing linguistic classification tasks, leaving open the question
of whether expert annotators fare better on evaluative judgments. Prior work on AI in mental health acknowledges
evaluation challenges, and has quantified expert disagreement on structured tasks [37], but has not qualitatively
investigated the clinical frameworks driving that disagreement or examined whether it reflects resolvable noise
or irreducible normative differences.
   We address this gap by shifting from a search for representativeness to an existence proof of irreducible
disagreement. By combining rigorous quantitative analysis with deep qualitative investigation, we distinguish
between measurement error, which better evaluation rubrics or training might address, and genuine evaluator
framework differences that no amount of aggregation can resolve. If certified experts applying coherent clinical
philosophies cannot reach a stable signal, then the “noise" current alignment methods seek to average away is
actually a contestation of the “signal” itself.

3     Methods
To disentangle the sources of expert variance, we utilized a sequential mixed-methods design. We first map
the statistical magnitude and patterns of disagreement through quantitative reliability analysis, then employ
semi-structured interviews to surface the underlying clinical frameworks driving those judgments. This dual-
layered approach allows us to empirically differentiate between remediable measurement error and the irreducible
normative divergence that complicates consensus-based aggregation.

3.1    Dataset Construction
We constructed a dataset of 360 AI-generated responses to synthetic mental health-related user prompts, system-
atically varying factors relevant to evaluation difficulty and safety concerns. All prompts were developed by the
research team; none contain real user data or personally identifiable information.
   Prompts were developed using a 3 × 3 matrix crossing clinical severity with communication directness. Clinical
severity levels (low, moderate, high) were operationalized under the supervision of licensed psychiatrists and
informed by validated clinical assessment scales: DSM-5 criteria for oppositional defiant disorder and substance
use disorder, [6] Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale and Clinician-Rated Dimensions of Psychosis Symptom Severity

for psychosis,[2, 67] Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale for suicidal ideation,[54] American Psychiatric Asso-
ciation Clinician-Rated Severity of Nonsuicidal Self-Injury for self-harm,[1] Clinician-Administered posttraumatic
stress disorder (PTSD) Scale for DSM-5 for PTSD,[47] Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale for obsessive-
compulsive disorder (OCD),[28] Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire for eating disorders,[23] NICHQ
Vanderbilt Assessment Scale for attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD),[48] Beck Hopelessness Scale
for hopelessness,[9] and the Bech-Rafaelsen Mania Rating Scale and Young Mania Rating Scale for mania-related
conditions.[8, 69] Communication directness was systematically varied to reflect differing degrees of explicitness
in how symptoms might be described (examples in A.1). This design was intended to capture realistic variation in
hypothetical presentations of mental health concerns to AI systems.
   The dataset was organized around ten high-risk response categories, each representing a potential harm
an inappropriate AI response might cause: supporting suicidal ideation, supporting non-suicidal self-harm,
reinforcing hopelessness, reinforcing hallucinations, colluding with delusions, encouraging manic behavior,
encouraging avoidance (anxiety), supporting continued substance use, reinforcing obsessive behaviors, reinforcing
distorted body image (eating disorders), and punishing ADHD-related behaviors. Risk types were derived from
clinical guidelines for managing specific conditions [35, 57] and prior work examining attributes of harmful AI
therapy responses [63]. To account for potential diagnostic ambiguity, our study design focuses on symptom
presentations and associated risks rather than diagnostic categories.
   We generated responses using four large language models: GPT-5, Claude 4 Sonnet, Grok-4, and Llama 3.2.2
Each model responded to 90 prompts, yielding 360 total responses. Models were accessed via their standard APIs
using default parameters to reflect typical user interactions.

3.2    Evaluation Framework
The evaluation framework was developed through systematic review of existing frameworks for assessing AI
mental health responses, therapy quality, and clinical communication. We reviewed evaluation approaches from
Med-PaLM 2 [64], CRADLE Bench [12], CounselBench [41], the EPITOME framework [61], Sanjeewa et al. [59],
clinical communication scales including the Motivational Interviewing Skill Code [46], and expanded on prior
work [30]. From this review, we synthesized a unified framework comprising two dimensions, Safety and Quality,
across eight factors. Each factor used a 5-point scale with detailed descriptors, where 1 represents the worst and
5 the best; full rubric descriptors are provided in A.2, Tables 6–13.
   Safety factors: Severity of potential harm (magnitude of harm that could occur) and likelihood of potential
harm (probability harmful content will be acted upon).
   Quality factors: Correctness (conformity with clinical evidence), relevance (specificity in addressing user’s
query), active listening (depth of reflection and paraphrasing), empathy and validation (warmth, compassion,
absence of stigma), boundaries and ethical disclaimers (clarity about LLM limitations), and actionability and
encouragement (practicality of next steps, hope-building).

3.3    Expert Participants and Procedure
Three certified psychiatrists served as expert raters, selected to provide diversity across subspecialty focus
(adult psychiatry, child/adolescent psychiatry), theoretical orientation (cultural, biopsychosocial), and patient
populations served. Detailed participant information is provided in A.3, Table 14.
   Before independent evaluation, experts participated in a 90-minute calibration session that included review
of all rating scales, guided discussion of anchor points, and practice ratings on held-out examples with group
discussion of discrepancies. While longer or iterative calibration might have improved surface-level consistency,

3.4    Analysis
For quantitative analysis, we computed intraclass correlation coefficients (ICC) as the primary reliability metric,
reporting ICC(2,1) for single-rater reliability and ICC(2,k) for averaged reliability [44, 62]. We interpret ICC values
following Cicchetti’s guidelines [20]: below 0.40 indicates poor reliability, 0.40–0.59 fair, 0.60–0.74 good, and
0.75 or above excellent. As a secondary metric suited to ordinal data, we computed Krippendorff’s alpha, where
𝛼 < 0.67 indicates data that should not be relied upon for drawing conclusions [36]. We examined systematic bias
through mean ratings per rater, directional disagreement between rater pairs, and factor-specific patterns. We
computed mean absolute deviation (MAD) to quantify disagreement magnitude and identify where disagreement
concentrates by condition, severity, risk type, and model.
   For qualitative analysis, interview transcripts were analyzed using reflexive thematic analysis [11], with a
specific focus on explaining quantitative patterns. When reliability statistics revealed high disagreement on
particular factors or conditions, we examined interview data for explanations, seeking to distinguish measurement
error from genuine framework differences.

4     Quantitative Results
Our analysis reveals three core findings: inter-rater reliability was poor across all evaluation factors; disagreement
concentrated on the highest-stakes items; and disagreement was systematic rather than random, reflecting
principled clinical framework differences.

4.3   Systematic Disagreement
Disagreement was directional rather than random. Table 3 shows mean ratings by rater across all factors. Rater A’s

Bonferroni correction showed significant differences between all rater pairs (𝑝 < 0.0167), including a moderate
effect size between Rater A and B (𝑑 = 0.528). Directional analysis revealed that Rater A scored higher than Rater
C in 52% of comparisons, Rater C scored higher in 15%, and the two assigned equal ratings in 33% (see A.4). This
3.5 : 1 asymmetry indicates consistent application of different frameworks rather than random disagreement
around a shared standard.

5     Qualitative Results
Semi-structured interviews with each expert revealed that disagreement stems from coherent but distinct clinical
approaches rather than measurement error. These framework differences explain the systematic quantitative
patterns documented in Section 4.

5.1    Three Clinical Frameworks
Detailed expert profiles, including subspecialty focus, years of experience, theoretical orientation, and therapeutic
modalities, are provided in A.3, Table 14.
   Expert A: safety-first orientation. Expert A prioritized harm avoidance, attending first to whether responses
might cause direct harm:“When I look at a response, my first question is always: could this hurt someone?
Everything else matters, but that comes first.”
   This safety-first lens produced the highest ratings overall (mean 4.19); responses that avoided active harm
generally passed, even when therapeutically limited. For Expert A, the primary failure mode is an AI response that
actively endangers a user, and responses that missed opportunities for deeper engagement were not penalized.
Expert B: therapeutic engagement orientation. Expert B focused on whether responses would facilitate help-seeking
and therapeutic connection:
      “Most important, empathy and actionability... these determine whether someone actually seeks help.
       You can have a perfectly ‘safe’ response that’s so cold or generic that the person closes the chat and
       doesn’t try again.”
This produced intermediate ratings (mean 3.76), balancing safety concerns against practical effectiveness. For
Expert B, a technically safe response that discourages continued engagement represents a meaningful failure.
   Expert C: cultural context orientation. Expert C emphasized cultural specificity and applied the most demanding
standards:
      “Cultural context and developmental framework are crucial. A response that works for one cultural
       community might be completely wrong for another.”
This orientation produced the strictest ratings overall (mean 3.64). For Expert C, responses that failed to acknowl-
edge their cultural assumptions or clearly delineate AI limitations were fundamentally inadequate, regardless of
whether they avoided direct harm.
   These three orientations, safety-focused, engagement-centered, and culturally-informed, represent legitimate
clinical philosophies that coexist in psychiatric practice. The largest quantitative gaps trace directly to these
framework differences; aggregating ratings across them produces arithmetic means that reflect no expert’s actual
clinical judgment.
   We also note that annotators’ evaluative frameworks are shaped in part by their priors about whether and
how AI should be deployed for mental health support. These priors are both a limitation, as they introduce a
source of variation beyond clinical judgment, and a meaningful feature of the disagreement we observe, as they
reflect the real-world conditions under which AI mental health tools will be evaluated and deployed.

5.2    The Boundaries Factor: A Case Study in Normative Disagreement
The boundaries factor (𝐼𝐶𝐶 = 0.087, 𝛼 = −0.203), which looks at details in the LLM response about its limitations,
illustrates how framework differences produce systematic divergence. Distribution analysis revealed that Rater
C assigned a score of “2” to 92% of responses (332 of 360), while Rater A’s ratings distributed across 3, 4, and 5.
When Rater A rated boundaries as “4,” Rater C rated the same response “2” in 96% of cases.
FAccT ’26, June 25–28, 2026, Montreal, QC, Canada                                                            Jafari et al.


   This pattern was not miscalibration but reflected a coherent normative stance. In interviews, Expert C articulated
a strict standard for AI boundary-setting:
       “For the piece about boundaries and ethical disclaimers... I was very much only giving a high grade if
       the platform explicitly said, like, reminded the user that they were AI. And not a professional.”
Expert C was surprised that this standard was rarely met:
       “I was a little surprised that only a few saying, just a reminder, I’m AI and I’m not a professional.
       That was surprising, yes.”
This strict stance stemmed from Expert C’s awareness of the global and contextual diversity of users:
       “The context of the user, like, their social context, what they’re actually going to do next, what they
       have access to, who they might consult or not, how isolated they are or not, like, we don’t know
       these things about the user. And those all impact the risk. Thinking about the universal response of a
       platform, given the extreme variability and diversity of users, that’s the challenge.”
   For Expert C, the fact that AI cannot know user context makes explicit boundary-setting essential, nearly all
responses failed to adequately acknowledge this limitation. Expert A, by contrast, evaluated boundaries relative
to what an AI can reasonably achieve in a single turn:
       “If the response doesn’t claim to be therapy and suggests professional help, that’s appropriate
       boundary-setting for what this is.”
   These positions reflect a genuine value disagreement about AI’s appropriate role in mental health contexts. If
experts cannot agree on what it means for an AI to “stay in its lane,” then boundary-setting cannot be treated as a
scalar property learned from averaged labels. It is a site of irreducible normative conflict. Expert C holds that AI
must always explicitly disclaim its non-professional status, while Expert A holds that implicit acknowledgment
through appropriate referrals is sufficient. Neither position is clinically unreasonable; both are defensible within
psychiatric practice. Aggregating their ratings produces a mean that reflects neither expert’s actual standard.

5.3    Structural Limitations of Single-Turn Evaluation
Beyond framework differences, experts identified fundamental constraints on the evaluation paradigm itself;
limitations that no rubric refinement could address. Temporal blindness. Mental health outcomes unfold over
months and years, but evaluation captures only a single moment. As Expert A noted, “A response that reassures
someone ’you’re doing fine’ might feel supportive now but delay treatment for years.” Expert C echoed this: “The
severity of the harm was often a missed opportunity to orient someone towards appropriate help. . . characterizing
how damaging it is. . . it’s hard." Context dependency. Appropriateness depends on user characteristics evaluators
cannot observe. Expert B explained, “The same response might be perfect for one patient and harmful for another.
When I’m rating, I have to imagine who’s receiving this. But I might imagine a different person than you do.”
Expert A added that age, developmental stage, and support systems all matter, but remain invisible to raters.
Absent ground truth. Experts questioned whether single-turn evaluation has a correct answer. Expert A observed,
“Psychiatrists are only 54% accurate at predicting suicide risk. . . it feels difficult to say AI must meet an insanely
high standard when we ourselves don’t have a great grasp of safety.” Expert B put it simply: “There’s no blood
test for a good response.” These limitations are structural, not methodological. The evaluation paradigm assumes
response quality is observable in the moment, but the quality that matters, whether a user seeks help, stays in
treatment, improves over time, emerges later and elsewhere.

6     Discussion
Our findings demonstrate that expert evaluation of AI mental health responses produces reliability below
thresholds considered acceptable for consequential assessment. We interpret these results as an existence proof:
Limits of Human Feedback in Mental Health AI Safety                          FAccT ’26, June 25–28, 2026, Montreal, QC, Canada


in at least one carefully constructed evaluation setting, expert disagreement was substantial enough to undermine
the validity of aggregated labels. Replication across additional expert panels is essential before drawing broader
conclusions.

6.1   Why Adjudication Cannot Resolve This
A natural response to our findings is that additional calibration or adjudication could resolve disagreement. We are
skeptical, for three reasons. First, our experts participated in a 90-minute calibration session before independent
rating, targeting task comprehension and anchor-point alignment. Extending calibration further risks the opposite
problem: if prolonged discussion leads experts to converge on a shared evaluative stance, the resulting annotations
measure group conformity rather than independent professional judgment. Our design balanced comprehension
against evaluative independence, and poor reliability emerged despite this methodological care. We note that
reliability statistics remain rarely reported in major RLHF publications [15].
   Second, the qualitative data reveal that disagreement stems from framework differences, not misunderstanding.
When Expert C assigned “2” to 92% of responses on boundaries while Expert A distributed ratings across 3, 4, and
5, this reflected a normative stance about what AI must disclaim, not confusion about the rubric. Adjudication
would require one expert to abandon their clinical philosophy.
   Third, adjudication assumes a correct answer exists that discussion can reveal. But as Expert B observed:“There’s
no blood test for a good response. I’m giving my opinion. . . my informed, trained opinion. . . but it’s still an opinion.”
   In domains where ground truth is constructed rather than discovered, adjudication risks producing false
consensus that ignores valid, differing professional perspectives.

6.2   Implications for Learning from Human Feedback
These findings have direct implications for systems that learn from human feedback.
   Reward model training. RLHF assumes that human preference comparisons provide valid signal for training
reward models. When 𝐼𝐶𝐶 is 0.087 on a factor like boundaries, any given label is more likely to reflect rater-specific
framework than response-specific quality. Reward models trained on such labels would learn rater idiosyncrasies
as much as genuine quality distinctions. The standard remedy, averaging across annotators, produces labels that
reflect no annotator’s actual judgment.
   Safety classification. Systems like Llama Guard train classifiers to distinguish safe from unsafe content. Our
findings suggest that the training labels themselves may be unreliable in precisely the domains where safety
classification matters most. High-stakes content (suicide, self-harm) produced the greatest disagreement, meaning
safety classifiers trained on expert labels would inherit contested judgments as if they were ground truth.
   Evaluation benchmarks. Benchmarks that treat human ratings as ground truth for measuring model performance
face a validity problem. If experts cannot agree on what constitutes a good response, benchmark scores measure
agreement with a particular subset of expert opinion rather than objective quality.

6.3   Implications for Fairness and Accountability
These findings raise questions beyond technical reliability that are central to fairness, accountability, and
transparency in AI systems. Aggregation as policy choice. Averaging expert ratings is often treated as a neutral
statistical operation, but our findings reveal it as a policy choice with distributive consequences. When Expert
A’s safety-first framework is averaged with Expert C’s culturally-informed framework, the resulting label reflects
neither clinical philosophy. The implicit “compromise” value system that emerges was never explicitly chosen
and may not correspond to any coherent approach to care. This is most critical on the highest-stakes items (e.g.,
suicidal ideation) where disagreement was greatest. Aggregation is a decision rule that determines which errors
the system makes and who bears the clinical risk.
FAccT ’26, June 25–28, 2026, Montreal, QC, Canada                                                      Jafari et al.


   Whose expertise counts. Our study characterizes expert disagreement as a principled, sociotechnical phenomenon
among certified psychiatrists. However, this professional class represents only one axis of epistemic authority.
Patients, caregivers, peer support specialists, and community health workers may hold different frameworks for
determining appropriate AI behavior, grounded in distinct experiences of mental health care. Relying exclusively
on clinical experts does not resolve the normative conflict inherent in AI alignment; it simply determines which
group has the power to define the ’correct’ response. The systematic divergence we document among highly
trained psychiatrists suggests that incorporating a broader range of stakeholders would likely amplify, rather
than reduce, the complexity of the alignment signal.
   Distributional impacts. The framework differences we identified have unequal consequences across populations.
Strict boundary-setting, evidenced by our finding that one expert rated 92.2% of responses as inadequate on
boundaries, may reduce AI overreliance but risks alienating users who depend on low-barrier support. Permissive
boundaries may improve engagement but risk role confusion. These harms are not uniformly distributed. Users
with limited professional access, often due to socioeconomic disadvantage, may be disproportionately harmed
by “safety-first” over-escalation that terminates supportive interactions. Conversely, users in acute crisis may
be disproportionately harmed by under-escalation that fails to redirect to appropriate resources. When expert
frameworks systematically diverge on these tradeoffs, the choice of whose framework to encode becomes a
fairness decision with differential impact across user populations.

6.4   Recommendations for Practice
Our findings do not counsel abandoning expert evaluation, which remains essential. Instead, they suggest method-
ological adjustments. Two baseline commitments should become standard: reporting inter-rater reliability in all
evaluation studies, and disclosing which expert perspectives informed a deployed system while acknowledging
that alternative frameworks exist. The absence of reliability reporting in major RLHF publications [15] is unten-
able when labels inform safety-critical decisions. Beyond these commitments, we organize our recommendations
into four methodological principles.
   Triage dimensions by consensus achievability. Expert consensus varies by dimension. Active listening and
actionability showed meaningfully higher reliability than boundaries or empathy (Table 1). Practitioners should
compute dimension-level reliability before choosing an aggregation strategy. Where reliability is adequate,
standard aggregation applies. Where it is not, alternative approaches are warranted, because forcing consensus
on normative dimensions risks either surface conformity or requiring experts to abandon clinically defensible
positions.
   Adopt framework-declared annotation. Rather than calibrating toward agreement, practitioners should ask
experts to state their clinical framework before annotation begins. The output becomes a labeled distribution,
ratings indexed by evaluative framework, rather than a single collapsed score. This shift has computational
support: jury learning [29], multi-annotator modeling [3, 5], and uncertainty-aware preference aggregation [39]
provide mechanisms for learning from structured disagreement rather than averaging over it. Our study surfaced
framework differences through post-hoc interviews; the recommendation is to make framework declaration a
prospective design choice.
   Use disagreement as an escalation signal. Items where experts diverge most are precisely the items that need
the most careful human oversight at deployment. Our data map where escalation is needed: suicide and self-
harm responses, psychosis-related content, and boundary-setting produced the greatest divergence (Tables 1,2).
High disagreement should trigger more conservative system behavior or human-in-the-loop review rather than
be absorbed into an aggregate that obscures it. This principle has precedent in information retrieval, where
disagreement prediction has been used to route contested items for additional review [14, 16] and probabilistic
relevance models treat assessor variance as informative uncertainty rather than noise [21].
Limits of Human Feedback in Mental Health AI Safety                                    FAccT ’26, June 25–28, 2026, Montreal, QC, Canada


   Apply context-aware framework weighting where possible. For purpose-built tools with known deployment
contexts, such as crisis hotlines, practitioners can weight annotators whose clinical philosophy matches the use
case. For general-purpose LLMs, this breaks down: the system cannot know whether the next user is casually
exploring coping strategies or in active crisis. Pre-selecting a single framework risks dangerous mismatches. The
harder problem is dynamic framework selection: detecting conversation-level signals and shifting the operative
framework accordingly. Our data characterize which frameworks diverge, on which dimensions, and for which
conditions, providing an initial empirical foundation. Translating this into inference-time framework selection
remains an open problem and a priority for future work.

7   Limitations
Our findings should be viewed in light of several limitations. Three expert raters evaluating 360 responses
cannot represent the full diversity of clinical orientations in psychiatry, and our psychiatrists were trained in
U.S. institutions, so different cultural contexts likely show different patterns of agreement. While our calibration
session was extensive, fundamental framework differences might remain that could be addressed with further
calibration refinement. Our evaluation rubric, though synthesized from established clinical and AI evaluation
frameworks, is novel and would benefit from further validation, and because each rater evaluated each response
only once, we cannot assess intrarater reliability from this study. Finally, we evaluated isolated AI responses;
multi-turn evaluation might provide more context but could also introduce new sources of divergence.

8   Conclusion
We examined whether expert evaluation of AI mental health responses provides reliable ground truth. Despite
similar professional training, inter-rater reliability was consistently poor (𝐼𝐶𝐶 0.087–0.295), reflecting systematic
framework differences rather than random noise. This is not measurement error that better calibration could
resolve; it is principled disagreement about what constitutes an appropriate mental health response in this context
and what AI should do. In domains where human feedback is most consequential, it may also be least reliable.
Acknowledging this tension is the first step toward more honest AI evaluation.

 Level     Description

 4         Minor omissions or mildly inaccurate lifestyle tips that are very unlikely to hurt but cause mild

 5         No foreseeable harm; actively reduces risk by sign-posting crisis lines, safe activities, and evidence-

 Level    Description
 1        Near-certain (>80%): direct, urgent, authoritative instruction with no alternatives

 Level    Description
 1        Any incorrect statement that directly risks harm (Level 1 or 2 severity). Example: “Cold plunges cure

 2        Inaccurate or outdated claim could steer self-care or help-seeking, but is not obviously dangerous on

 3        Limited errors with low impact on decisions
 4        Minor imprecision with no impact on behaviors (trivial stats, wording)
 5        Matches guidelines, primary literature, clinical experience/knowledge, accepted commonsense; cites
          appropriately

    • Use active listening; allow silence and reflection time.
    • Maintain a neutral stance; avoid signaling correctness of responses.
    • Follow emergent insights while preserving protocol structure.
    • Ground discussion in concrete examples where possible.
    • Monitor emotional load and pacing throughout the interview.
