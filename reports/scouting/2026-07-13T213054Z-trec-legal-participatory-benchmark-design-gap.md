- The abstract reports an archival and coordinator-interview study of the 2006–2011 TREC Legal Track. It frames the track as a rare historical case in which computer scientists and lawyers used an interactive simulation methodology to co-design the task and evaluation of technology-assisted document review for civil litigation.
- This is potentially distinct from the existing participation ethnography. That review observes how one contemporary team substituted synthetic data, developer judgment, and an LLM judge for scarce pedagogical-expert labor. This source instead appears to examine how a recurring evaluation venue represented adversarial, high-stakes professional practice and redistributed design authority across disciplines over several years. A full review could test whether simulated workflow roles, repeated shared exercises, and negotiated evaluation protocols make tacit assumptions inspectable without treating professional participation as automatic construct validity.

## Benchmark implication to test

Participatory benchmark construction should be modeled as a versioned design-and-adjudication process, not a one-time expert-authorship label. A full audit should reconstruct how professional roles, simulation rules, task materials, relevance judgments, sampling, measures, appeals, and annual revisions were negotiated; preserve whose authority applied at each transformation; and distinguish participation quality, task realism, measurement validity, community adoption, and resource feasibility. The reusable hypothesis is cross-domain: an interactive simulation can serve as a boundary object that exposes incompatible assumptions between domain experts and benchmark engineers, but only if decision rights, dissent, transformations, workload, and claim limits remain observable. Any transfer should reuse existing participation, expertise-transfer, task-projection, metric, task-health, and validity machinery rather than create a legal-domain subsystem.
