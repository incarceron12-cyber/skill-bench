## Substantive finding (triage only)

**The Benchmark Ceiling: Human Judgment, Evaluation Scarcity, and the Political Economy of AI Capability Measurement** — Mark Esposito, Liu Zhang, and Ali Ansari, arXiv:2607.01254v1.

- Immutable record: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01254v1
- Immutable PDF: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.01254v1
- The arXiv API identifies v1 and reports a formal model of benchmark-signal depreciation plus platform data from micro1 covering more than 1,000 credentialed professionals.
- The abstract claims that discriminating signal shifts toward hard-tail items as models saturate easier items, that replacement cost rises convexly with frontier capability, that private producers underinvest in validity, and that high-judgment/low-codifiability evaluation labor carries a scarcity premium. These are claims to audit, not established findings for skill-bench.
- The canonical arXiv record was reachable and the title/platform claim was independently returned by web search. This is **metadata and abstract triage only**. The paper was not read in full during scouting, and no claim is made about its methods, data access, statistical validity, conflicts, or applicability beyond the sampled commercial platform.

## Why this is distinct

The existing full review of domain-expert participation documents consent, trust, fatigue, role substitution, and missing decision rights in one embedded project, but explicitly lacks rates, total cost, recruitment conversion, and evidence that free or near-zero participation is feasible. ResearchRubrics supplies aggregate expert hours but under-specifies labor and authority. This candidate is the first located primary source claiming labor-market evidence directly tied to judgment intensity and codifiability.

The transferable question is not whether skill-bench should buy platform labor. It is whether measurable scarcity, task decomposition, credential requirements, contribution duration, reciprocal value, and replacement cost can constrain the project's expert-participation and sponsorship hypotheses. Full review must treat company-platform selection, occupational mix, seniority, geography, client selection, and unavailable underlying data as potential claim boundaries.
