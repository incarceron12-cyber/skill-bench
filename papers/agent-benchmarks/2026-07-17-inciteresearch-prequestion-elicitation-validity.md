## Verdict

InciteResearch contributes a useful design hypothesis: scientific assistance can begin before a crisp question, and a candidate proposal can be made more inspectable by preserving a structured profile, an assumption-breaking move, a falsifiable claim, predictions, constraints, a method derivation, and an explicit challenge to method necessity. Its released prompts and state machine make this proposed decomposition unusually concrete.

TF-Bench does **not**, however, test whether the system recovers a researcher’s tacit understanding. Its 52 inputs were generated by Grok 4.3 and revised by undisclosed human annotators; no input is traced to a researcher’s privately held concern, no researcher profile has independent ground truth, and no contributor accepts, corrects, rejects, or uses the inferred profile or proposal. Each input already consists of one paragraph stating a failure/friction and a second gesturing toward a reframing. For the nominally “domain-unrelated” cases, the evaluation supplies the scientific domain separately. The system therefore need not discover either a real person’s latent state or the target domain.

The experiment supports a narrower configured-package result: under a coupled rubric that explicitly rewards broken assumptions, problem framing, and method necessity, two model judges gave the much larger Claude 4.6 Sonnet EVN pipeline higher novelty and impact scores than a two-turn direct-prompt baseline and AgentLaboratory. It does not identify elicitation, semantic fidelity to a person, scientific correctness, researcher utility, proposal impact, or transfer. The main contrast is also not an equal-resource test: the qualitative example reports 43,547 output tokens plus 34,322 cache-creation tokens for InciteResearch versus 4,371 output tokens for the prompt baseline.

The paper’s human check is useful but bounded. Two professors scored 26 cleaned proposals from only the cancer-prognosis domain. Human–human weighted kappa is reported as 0.748 and the average of four LLM–human kappas as 0.624, but item-level ratings, per-dimension kappas, confidence intervals, reviewer qualifications, cleaning attrition, and model-vs-human score effects are unavailable. Agreement about proposal impressions is not evidence that a proposal faithfully externalizes anyone’s tacit knowledge or will survive scientific execution.

For `skill-bench`, the unique lesson is to treat **profile reconstruction as a claim that needs its own respondent-grounded observation**, not as an intermediate object whose fluency becomes self-validating. Preserve the chain:

`question opportunity → respondent observation caused by the question → supported profile/claim update → respondent correction or acceptance → objective/artifact consequence`

InciteResearch directly records candidate objects in the middle of this chain, but TF-Bench supplies no authority-bearing observations at either end.

## One-sentence contribution

The paper proposes an Elicit–Violate–Necessitate state machine and a 52-item synthetic scientific-ideation suite, showing higher coupled judge scores for a high-compute structured proposal pipeline while leaving tacit-state recovery, researcher acceptance, scientific validity, downstream utility, and reproducibility unestablished.

## Why this matters for skill-bench

This review advances charter objectives A, B, D, and E through targeted expansion at the boundary **before** a professional task is explicit. Scientific ideation is a methodological test bed, not a scope commitment.

It is distinct from nearby evidence:

- **Data Therapist** exposes generated questions to six real experts, but does not validate downstream truth. InciteResearch has no real elicitation participants at all; it contributes a more explicit candidate profile and derivation representation.
- **YIELD** learns interviewer-like next turns from real historical dialogues but never observes answers to generated questions. InciteResearch stages a short interaction, yet its benchmark inputs are synthetic and provide no independently known respondent state.
- **SimInstruct** studies production from simulated novice dialogue. InciteResearch’s benchmark likewise cannot promote generated interaction into evidence about real people.
- **Organizational tacit-knowledge simulation** can compare recovered identifiers with planted synthetic facts. TF-Bench lacks even a planted profile/claim oracle, so it cannot measure recovery separately from proposal generation.
- **Benchmark-to-risk elicitation** concerns judgment after benchmark evidence exists. InciteResearch concerns problem formulation before a target exists, but similarly needs explicit authority, disagreement, revision, and consequence joins.

Useful completion is therefore a bounded retain/repair/test account, not a new science-specific schema or an assertion that the framework elicits expertise.

## Contribution and research question

The paper asks whether an AI research assistant can operate before the user has an actionable research question, transforming vague “tacit friction” into an explicit, inspectable, actionable proposal (Abstract and Section 1, pp. 1–3). It contributes:

1. an **E** stage intended to elicit a five-field researcher profile: friction points, motivation, constraints, research taste, and refined topic;
2. a **V** stage that mines hidden assumptions, selects one by a model-assigned feasibility × novelty product, reframes the problem, and creates a seven-stage causal derivation;
3. an **N** stage that critiques necessity, sufficiency, counterexamples, post-hoc inversion, and—at least in the appendix/release—uniqueness;
4. TF-Bench, 52 two-paragraph inspirations spanning four scientific modes and two ambiguity labels;
5. comparisons with a direct prompt and AgentLaboratory;
6. E/V/N ablations; and
7. a small expert/LLM-rater agreement study.

The evidence supports that the package creates structured proposal artifacts and receives higher configured judge scores. It does not support the paper’s stronger language that the output is “a clarified form of what the researcher may already know tacitly” (Section 1, p. 2) because no researcher-held target or acceptance event is observed.

## Methodology and system

### The evaluand is synthetic proposal elaboration, not participant elicitation

TF-Bench was first generated by Grok 4.3 and then “reviewed and revised through multiple rounds of human interaction” (Section 4.1 and Appendix E, pp. 5, 18). The paper does not report:

- the number, identity, scientific expertise, recruitment, training, or compensation of annotators;
- generation prompts or model sampling settings;
- item-level edit histories or rejection/attrition counts;
- whether any item came from an annotator’s own research experience;
- independent labels for the latent friction, intended profile, acceptable reframings, or proposal consequences; or
- agreement, author review, source grounding, or scientific-validity checks for the 52 inputs.

The benchmark’s “human inspiration” label is therefore not participant provenance. Human editing can improve surface authenticity without making the input evidence of a real researcher’s latent state.

Each domain has ten related and three unrelated items, for 40 related and 12 unrelated items. Each item has two paragraphs: the first states a perceived failure and the second already gestures toward a reframing (Section 4.1, pp. 5–6; Appendix E, p. 18). The example related input names positional decay, loss of low-magnification context, a failure mode, and a hierarchical multi-centric candidate. It is under-specified, but not merely an ineffable feeling. The unrelated example supplies a luggage-rearrangement metaphor, while the evaluation separately supplies “Attribution of Extreme Weather Events in Climate Models.” This tests whether a model can map a furnished metaphor into a furnished domain—not whether elicitation discovers the domain or caused concern.

### E: a generated profile with no reference state

The manuscript describes semantic alignment, information-seeking questions, dynamic triage, and two rounds of interaction (Section 3.1, pp. 4–5). The official post-v1 implementation is more precise:

- `agents/dialogue_agent.py` asks one model-generated question from the topic;
- after one answer, it asks one more question based on prompt-defined triage;
- after the second answer, the same model summarizes strict JSON;
- `research_taste` is explicitly “inferred”; and
- malformed JSON falls back to placing raw model text in `friction_points` rather than invalidating the episode.

The paper says the next question should maximize expected profile-uncertainty reduction (p. 4), but neither the paper nor release defines a belief distribution, uncertainty estimator, candidate-question set, or optimization. This is motivational language for a prompted policy, not a realized information-gain algorithm.

The five dimensions are useful authoring fields, but they are not measured constructs here. No target profile exists; no respondent reviews the fields; and no locator links a field to an exact answer span. “Research taste” and motivation can be model hypotheses. Without spontaneous/probed/inferred status and read-back correction, an apparently coherent profile can launder the model’s interpretation into attributed expertise.

### V: useful derivation discipline, self-scored selection

V mines hidden assumptions and asks the model to emit novelty and feasibility values in `[0,1]`, then conceptually selects the assumption maximizing their product (Section 3.2, pp. 4–5). In the release, `assumption_breaking_node` parses those values from the same Claude response and defaults both to `0.7` on parse failure (`agents/refine_agent.py`, lines 86–186). There is no independent feasibility check, literature exhaustiveness test, calibration, or candidate-level judge record.

The seven-stage chain—Problem → Broken Assumption → Insight → Claim → Predictions → Constraints → Method—is the paper’s strongest reusable artifact. It forces a proposal to expose why a method is claimed to follow from a reframing. But structural completion is not semantic entailment. The same model authors the premises, derivation, and method; a fluent internally coherent chain can form around a false factual premise or an invented interpretation of the user.

The release optionally retrieves abstracts and partial full-text excerpts, but full-text reading is off by default and excerpts are truncated. The benchmark release contains no search traces, source locators, retrieved corpus, or factual checks. Novelty and feasibility remain proposal impressions, not literature-grounded or execution-grounded observations.

### N: critique without demonstrated repair

The manuscript describes necessity, sufficiency, and counterexample checks and acknowledges that N is mainly a post-hoc structural verifier rather than a revision-integrated controller (Figure 2 and Sections 3.3/5.3, pp. 3, 5, 8). Appendix D and the post-v1 release add anti-inversion and uniqueness checks. The N report is injected into final proposal generation, but the experiment supplies no before/after repair trace showing which defect was detected, changed, preserved, or resolved.

Release inspection also shows a timing boundary: the acquired commit is eleven days after v1, and its five-test N prompt is not proof of the exact paper-time implementation. The graph includes later human direction selection and proposal-review interrupts, while the paper’s evaluation describes a lightweight E-then-V-then-N run. Paper claims and release behavior must not be silently merged.

## Evaluation and evidence

### Systems and resource envelope

All InciteResearch nodes use Claude 4.6 Sonnet with stage-specific temperatures; Gemini 3.1 Pro and GPT-5.2 judge at temperature zero (Section 4.3, p. 6). The main baseline gets the same two inspiration paragraphs in two turns plus a detailed prompt requiring a broken assumption, logical inevitability, ablation matrix, baselines, tasks, metrics, and failures (Appendix A, p. 12). AgentLaboratory is an additional structurally different baseline (Appendix B, pp. 13–14).

“Input-matched” is not resource- or opportunity-matched. For the qualitative case, InciteResearch uses 4,471 input, 43,547 output, and 34,322 cache-creation input tokens; the direct baseline uses 1,573 input and 4,371 output tokens (Section 5.1, p. 6). The paper does not provide per-item distributions, API calls, latency, price, retries, invalid runs, cache-read tokens, or complete AgentLaboratory resources. Higher scores estimate the package effect of more prompted stages, intermediate state, retrieval opportunities, and token budget—not the isolated value of elicitation or a more efficient method.

### Judge coupling and evidence-view asymmetry

The novelty rubric explicitly rewards “problem framing, method necessity, and broken assumptions,” the exact structures InciteResearch is prompted repeatedly to produce (Appendix A, p. 11). Impact rewards paradigm-shift language, while V is asked to maximize paradigm shift. This is criterion–intervention coupling: the treatment teaches the surface and structure the grader values. It may still improve the configured artifact, but it cannot establish external scientific novelty or latent-state fidelity.

The judge prompt also says to use “cached intermediate state if provided” to avoid information asymmetry. That creates a different observer view for InciteResearch unless equivalent evidence is constructed for baselines. A judge can reward a visible derivation trace or self-declared feasibility even when the final proposal alone would not support it. The paper does not state exactly which state fields each judge saw for each condition, how state was serialized/truncated, or whether human professors saw the same intermediate evidence. Model-judge and human scores may therefore target different artifacts.

Temperature zero does not “guarantee strict reproducibility and stability.” API model aliases can change; deterministic kernels and service routing are not established; and no repeated identical-judge-call reliability is reported. The two-judge average also hides judge disagreement and invalid outputs.

### Units, aggregation, uncertainty, and denominators

The paper says proposal scores are averaged across Gemini and GPT, and reports mean ± standard deviation over five repeated experiments (Sections 4.2 and 5.2, pp. 6–8). It does not define whether repetition occurs at generation, judging, or both; whether the same proposal is judged repeatedly; how item, domain, ambiguity, run, and judge levels are aggregated; or what the standard deviations summarize.

The “overall” means are exactly the unweighted averages of the related and unrelated stratum means—for example `(4.172 + 4.328) / 2 = 4.250`—despite 40 related and 12 unrelated items. That can be a legitimate equal-stratum estimand, but it is not the mean over the 52-item inventory and is not disclosed as such. The overall standard deviations also do not transparently follow from a stated hierarchical estimator. No paired item-level effects, confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, domain slices, judge variance, cluster-aware model, missingness, cleaning attrition, or multiplicity account is provided.

The headline comparison therefore lacks a reconstructible response matrix. Because TF-Bench examples, generated proposals, judge records, and scripts are absent from the acquired official release, even the item denominator cannot be audited against the reported tables.

### Human evaluation

Appendix C narrows the human study to 26 cleaned proposals in only Multimodal Learning for Cancer Prognosis Analysis: apparently 13 items × two systems (pp. 14–15). Two domain professors independently score shuffled, source-stripped proposals on the same three dimensions. This supplies a useful perceptual reliability check in one domain.

Its claim ceiling remains low:

- “after data cleaning” is unexplained and no pre-cleaning denominator or exclusion reason is reported;
- professor identities, subfield fit, conflicts, training, time, compensation, and source access are absent;
- filenames `008.md` to `104.md` do not expose item/system pairing;
- reviewers appear to see proposals, not originating dialogues, profiles, intermediate state, literature, executable artifacts, or outcomes;
- no item-level ratings, comments, score distributions, system means/effects, per-dimension kappas, or confidence intervals are released;
- averaging four pairwise LLM–human kappas discards rater and dimension heterogeneity; and
- agreement measures consistency, not correctness, tacit fidelity, or usefulness.

The paper earlier states that two corresponding-domain professors review proposals (Section 4.2, p. 6), which could imply broader coverage; Section 5.2 and Appendix C specify only one domain. The narrow appendix should govern the evidence claim.

### Ablations do not establish causal operator necessity

Removing V produces the largest score decrease; removing E lowers novelty/impact; removing N changes little (Table 4 and Section 5.3, p. 8). These are configured component-removal contrasts under the same coupled judges, not evidence that the operators are logically or scientifically necessary.

The paper does not disclose exact ablation prompts, state substitutions, calls/tokens, failed episodes, paired item outcomes, or whether removal also changes context length and proposal structure. “w/o E” removes externalization but the system still receives authored inputs containing friction and reframing; it does not compare correctly elicited, incorrectly elicited, and oracle-profile conditions. “w/o V” removes the component most directly aligned with the novelty rubric. “w/o N” removes a post-hoc report that the authors acknowledge is weakly integrated. The results establish sensitivity of judge scores to package components, not caused tacit recovery or ordered causal indispensability.

## Unique insight

InciteResearch reveals a previously under-specified boundary in expertise-to-evaluation: **the object being elicited must have an authority-bearing referent**.

A generated profile can be:

1. structurally complete;
2. textually supported by a dialogue;
3. accepted as an interpretation by the respondent;
4. predictively useful for selecting questions or work;
5. causally improved by the elicitation interaction; and
6. consequentially useful in an artifact or decision.

These are different claims. TF-Bench mostly observes (1), with weak evidence for (2), and no evidence for (3)–(6). A benchmark that grades only downstream proposal polish can reward a productive **reinterpretation** even when it misrepresents the contributor. That may be valuable co-creation, but it is not tacit-knowledge recovery.

A second insight is that “domain-unrelated” input is not a hard elicitation condition when the target domain is supplied out of band. It tests metaphor-conditioned generation under a domain anchor. To test pre-question discovery, the benchmark must separate legitimate hidden context from information that no fair question could recover, and score the system’s questions, respondent observations, posterior/profile revisions, and abstention—not only the final proposal.

Third, derivation traces should be treated as **falsifiable warrants**, not proof. The seven-stage chain is valuable because it exposes where to challenge a proposal. Each edge needs evidence status, alternatives, and a downstream test. A chain authored and judged by aligned models can be coherent without being true.

Finally, greater proposal ambition can mechanically raise novelty/impact while lowering feasibility. Without later execution and scientific review, that frontier is not calibrated. Benchmark design should keep semantic fidelity, novelty, feasibility, impact, user utility, and factual/scientific validity separate rather than promoting their judge average into “thinking extension.”

## Limitations and validity threats

1. **No elicitation participants:** TF-Bench items are model-generated and human-edited, not recorded researcher concerns.
2. **No latent-state ground truth:** there is no independently known intended friction, motivation, constraint, taste, or refined topic.
3. **No respondent validation:** inferred profiles are not accepted, corrected, rejected, or confidence-rated by an originating person.
4. **No caused-information measure:** question-level answers and profile deltas are not scored.
5. **Input already externalizes friction:** each two-paragraph item states a failure and gestures toward reframing.
6. **Domain side channel:** “unrelated” items receive the target scientific domain separately.
7. **Synthetic-authenticity uncertainty:** Grok generation prompts, sampling, edit lineage, and annotator authority are absent.
8. **Unreleased benchmark:** the acquired official 41-blob tree contains no TF-Bench examples.
9. **Unreleased results:** generations, state traces, judge outputs, human sheets, ablation records, and analysis scripts are absent.
10. **Criterion coupling:** novelty explicitly rewards broken assumptions, framing, and necessity—the intervention’s required outputs.
11. **Potential judge-view asymmetry:** cached state is available “if provided,” with no equivalent baseline evidence contract.
12. **No factual/source verification:** novelty and impact are judged from proposal/state text, not an authoritative literature search.
13. **Self-scored assumption selection:** the generator supplies its own novelty and feasibility values.
14. **Fallback laundering:** release parse failures can become profile friction text or default 0.7 scores rather than invalid episodes.
15. **Structural coherence substitution:** a seven-stage trace is treated as support without edge-level entailment validation.
16. **N is post hoc:** necessity checking is not shown to cause accepted repairs.
17. **Paper/release drift:** the inspected commit postdates v1 by eleven days and adds behavior not proven paper-time.
18. **Unequal compute:** the reported InciteResearch example uses about ten times the baseline output tokens plus large cache creation.
19. **Compound treatment:** stages, prompts, calls, search, intermediate context, and budget change together.
20. **Under-specified baseline parity:** model revision, state access, retrieval, retries, and AgentLaboratory resources are incomplete.
21. **Unclear repetition unit:** five runs are not mapped to generation, judging, item, or pipeline seeds.
22. **Unclear standard deviations:** the hierarchy and estimator behind `±` are not defined.
23. **Undisclosed equal-stratum aggregation:** overall means weight 40-item and 12-item strata equally.
24. **No paired uncertainty:** item-level paired effects and confidence intervals are absent.
25. **No invalid/missing ledger:** generation, parser, judge, cleaning, and API failures are not reported.
26. **Alias reproducibility:** commercial model names and temperature zero do not freeze model weights or service behavior.
27. **Judge disagreement hidden:** averaging two judges removes diagnostic heterogeneity.
28. **Human study only one domain:** 26 proposals do not validate all four domains or the unrelated condition broadly.
29. **Human attrition opaque:** “after data cleaning” has no initial count or exclusion rules.
30. **Human evidence view is narrow:** proposal-only review cannot assess elicitation fidelity or derivation provenance.
31. **Human reliability incomplete:** no raw labels, dimension-specific agreement, confidence intervals, or adjudication.
32. **Agreement is not validity:** kappa does not establish scientific correctness, impact, or user utility.
33. **Ablation-score alignment:** removing V removes the structure most directly named by the novelty rubric.
34. **No oracle-profile ablation:** there is no comparison among true, inferred, corrupted, and absent profiles.
35. **No question-policy baseline:** random, fixed ACTA/CDM, information-gain, human mentor, or minimal-clarification policies are absent.
36. **No downstream execution:** proposals are not implemented, falsified, or compared with simpler alternatives.
37. **No scientific impact observation:** judge ratings of potential impact are not realized impact.
38. **No user utility/burden:** satisfaction, correction effort, ownership, agenda change, and time are unmeasured.
39. **No consent/authority model:** generated benchmark items cannot validate contribution rights or attribution.
40. **Narrow scientific coverage:** four computational/empirical modes do not establish transport to other sciences or knowledge work.
41. **Safety and agenda-shaping unmeasured:** plausible false premises and homogenized research directions are acknowledged but not evaluated.
42. **Claim overreach:** configured judge gains do not demonstrate that AI extends “thinking itself.”

## Reproducibility and operational realism

The immutable paper is unusually helpful in publishing the judge, baseline, and EVN prompts. The post-v1 repository exposes the profile fields, graph nodes, temperatures, parser fallbacks, optional retrieval, human interrupts, state persistence, and proposal assembly. That supports a substantive implementation audit rather than abstract-only interpretation.

Exact paper reproduction is nevertheless poor. The official snapshot has application code and prompts but no 52-item TF-Bench corpus, annotator/edit records, evaluation harness, paper-time configuration lock, generated proposals, intermediate states, judge outputs, human sheets, ablation outputs, or table scripts. The README repeats paper scores without adding result provenance. Commercial API aliases are mutable, dependency versions are not locked, and the acquired release is later than v1. The reported tables cannot be replayed or independently reaggregated.

Operational realism is mixed. The framework represents an important real phenomenon—research often begins with dissatisfaction rather than a specification—and the released CLI permits actual iterative use. But the experiment does not observe real research collaboration. Two short answer opportunities, synthetic authored prompts, automatic profile inference, proposal-only judging, no literature authority audit, no implementation, and no later researcher correction or adoption omit the very longitudinal reflection loop that Figure 1 treats as central. The paper correctly calls the implementation a first lightweight step (Section 3, p. 4); benchmark claims should remain at that level.

## Transfer to skill-bench

### Retain

- Permit task authoring to begin from a **question opportunity or friction record**, not only a complete request.
- Keep profile candidates explicit: friction, motivation, constraints, preferences/taste, and refined objective.
- Preserve the seven-stage derivation as a challengeable warrant graph: problem, assumption, insight, claim, predictions, constraints, method.
- Require necessity, simpler-alternative, counterexample, anti-inversion, and uniqueness challenges before promoting a method.
- Keep semantic fidelity, novelty, feasibility, impact, scientific correctness, user utility, and downstream consequence separate.
- Preserve exact prompt/state/model/release identities and paper-versus-release timing.

### Repair before reuse

1. **Give elicitation an authority-bearing target.** Use a consented contributor, an independently authored claim pack, or a frozen simulator with planted states. Record what is genuinely held, unknown, sensitive, and inferable.
2. **Type every profile field.** Preserve spontaneous/probed/respondent-inferred/analyst-inferred/model-inferred status, answer locators, confidence, context, contradiction, and read-back correction.
3. **Score question effects.** For each opportunity, record the question, answerability/authority, respondent realization, newly supported/corrected/bounded claim, and burden—not just the final artifact.
4. **Separate elicitation from co-creation.** A respondent may reject fidelity but value a reframing. Record both rather than calling useful reinterpretation recovered tacit knowledge.
5. **Hide only fair recoverable state.** If the domain is part of the construct, do not supply it out of band; if supplied, name the task metaphor-to-domain reframing.
6. **Use oracle and corruption conditions.** Compare absent, true, inferred, and deliberately wrong profiles under equal proposal-generation budgets. This identifies whether profile accuracy matters downstream.
7. **Equalize resource envelopes.** Match model, retrieval, calls/tokens/time, and observer views, or report a package-value frontier rather than an operator effect.
8. **Decouple criteria from intervention language.** Independent experts should author outcome-facing criteria before seeing EVN outputs; include counterexamples that are structurally elegant but scientifically false and plain proposals that are correct.
9. **Validate trace edges.** Require source/evidence locators, surviving alternative methods, falsification tests, and execution results. Treat the trace as a warrant under review.
10. **Preserve plural observers.** Proposal judges, source auditors, originating contributors, domain experts, implementers, and downstream users have different evidence and authority.
11. **Use hierarchical inference.** Publish item × condition × run × judge observations, exact invalid/missing episodes, paired effects, item/domain clusters, and the intended ambiguity-stratum weighting.
12. **Close the consequence loop.** Observe contributor correction/acceptance, objective changes, proposal adoption, implementation, falsification, artifact quality, time/cost, and new harms separately.

### Bounded validation design

A cross-domain pre-question conformance slice can be built with independently authored respondent/claim packs, without pretending that synthetic respondents are experts:

- author one latent concern, several plausible rival interpretations, constraints, preferences, sensitive facts, and one genuinely unknown field;
- expose a vague opening statement and a bounded number of question opportunities;
- compare no-elicitation, fixed-probe, adaptive-probe, oracle-profile, inferred-profile, and corrupted-profile conditions under equal downstream-generation resources;
- require line-level profile evidence plus respondent read-back in simulated episodes;
- score supported profile recovery, unsupported attribution, correction uptake, question burden, stop/escalate quality, and downstream artifact/check effect separately; and
- plant an elegant but wrong assumption-breaking chain to test whether graders prefer EVN-shaped rhetoric over evidence.

This would validate the benchmark machinery and the difference between profile fidelity and useful reframing. It would not establish real expert participation, researcher benefit, professional transport, or scientific impact. A later consented session remains necessary for those claims.

The existing expertise-transfer, interaction-evidence, provenance-boundary, validity-argument, metric-monitoring, task-health, plural-judgment, and blocked elicitation-session contracts can house these requirements. No new source-specific build task is justified.

## Concrete repository actions

1. Index this review as full-paper and release-audited evidence with the claim ceiling **structured scientific-proposal package under coupled model/human proposal ratings**.
2. Add no duplicate build task; route any future experiment through existing elicitation, interaction, validity, metric, and participation machinery.
3. Carry the five-link chain into the next elicitation synthesis: question opportunity → caused respondent observation → supported profile update → correction/acceptance → objective/artifact consequence.
4. Keep the consented elicitation-session contract blocked. Synthetic TF-Bench editing does not exercise participant consent, read-back, contribution authority, or downstream approval.
5. Before reusing TF-Bench claims, obtain the exact 52 items, paper-time generations/states, judge outputs, human sheets, ablation configuration, and aggregation code; otherwise treat the reported tables as non-replayable paper evidence.

## Bottom line

InciteResearch is valuable because it makes pre-question ideation legible as a sequence of candidate states and falsifiable warrants rather than pretending every user arrives with a task specification. Its seven-stage derivation and adversarial necessity checks are promising authoring aids. TF-Bench, however, evaluates synthetic two-paragraph inputs with no originating researcher state and grades final proposals using criteria tightly aligned with the framework’s required rhetoric. The result is evidence for a configured structured-generation package, not for eliciting tacit knowledge, extending a researcher’s own thinking, producing scientifically valid ideas, or improving research outcomes. `skill-bench` should retain the explicit profile and derivation objects while demanding respondent-grounded updates, correction, independent criteria, equal resources, trace-edge evidence, and downstream consequences before promoting them into expertise-transfer claims.
