## One-sentence contribution

AsymmetryZero usefully turns prompt, reference, criteria, grader types, weights, threshold, and judge panel into a portable executable evaluation package, but its study validates only **configured frontier-versus-compact jury behavior on selected available outputs from a private STEM-heavy benchmark**; neither the paper nor release tests whether a human expert preference was elicited faithfully, preserved in the contract, or judged correctly.

## Why this matters for skill-bench

This source addresses the charter's central expertise-to-evaluation question at the evaluator boundary:

> When does an explicit, executable rubric preserve expert judgment, and when does it merely make one unvalidated specification reproducible?

Its strongest design move is to treat evaluation policy as a versionable object rather than a hidden judge prompt. That is directly reusable. Its strongest warning is more important: **contract explicitness, cross-harness portability, jury consensus, and cheap task-level stability are four different properties, none of which establishes semantic authority or correctness**.

The relevant cross-domain chain is:

```text
expert/stakeholder and decision authority
→ elicitation evidence and disagreement
→ requirement interpretation and scope
→ criterion projection and public basis
→ evidence-view/admissibility contract
→ configured grader observations
→ aggregation and escalation policy
→ criterion/task measurement
→ bounded interpretation and use
```

AsymmetryZero starts at the criterion projection but repeatedly describes that starting object as an expert preference. The absent upstream links are therefore not incidental documentation gaps; they are the untested construct in the paper's title.

## Research questions and defensible claim boundary

The paper asks three questions (Introduction, pp. 2–3): why explicit semantic contracts are preferable to answer keys or open-ended judging; how a common contract can execute for models and agents; and whether compact judges can substitute economically for larger judges.

The strongest defensible empirical claim is:

> For 429 available semantic criterion instances generated by four named solver configurations under one ten-turn Harbor agent on a proprietary 75-task, predominantly STEM suite, strict-majority decisions from one selected five-model compact panel agreed with one selected five-model frontier panel on 75.9%–89.6% of instances by solver run. Compact panels had more cross-model vote splits and substantially lower provider-reported cost and transport latency. These are panel-comparison results, not correctness or expert-alignment results.

The paper and release do **not** establish that:

- the tasks, criteria, weights, thresholds, or grader types faithfully encode expert preferences;
- the task authors are domain experts, represent target stakeholders, or have decision authority;
- criteria are atomic, independent, complete, applicable, publicly grounded, or calibrated to consequence;
- the frontier jury is correct, expert-equivalent, stable across repeated calls, or an appropriate teacher;
- a 3–2 split measures temporal instability rather than expected heterogeneity among five different models;
- the compact panel is operationally substitutable at criterion, task-decision, ranking, or consequential-use level;
- Inspect and Harbor scores are measurement-equivalent merely because they read the same criteria;
- the released implementation reproduces the private study;
- the reported scores establish agent capability, professional judgment, post-training reward validity, production fitness, or readiness.

## Methodology and system

### Evaluation contract

The contract separates execution inputs (task prompt, optional reference, environment metadata) from grading inputs (criteria and threshold). Each criterion has an ID, prompt, weight, and either `ExactMatch` or `llm-judge`; semantic judges see task, final submission, and criterion, produce a binary vote and rationale, and strict majority determines criterion credit. Criterion weights are normalized to 100 and summed; the task passes when its sum reaches `passThreshold` (Section 3.1–3.2, pp. 4–5).

This is an economical interface, not a complete validity contract. It lacks typed requirement authority, source/evidence locator, applicability, mandatory/gate semantics, dependency and overlap relations, admissible artifact/trace views, alternatives, abstention/insufficiency, uncertainty, protected/private basis, threshold warrant, and use claim. Binary criterion votes also collapse `not applicable`, `insufficient evidence`, `invalid artifact`, and substantive failure.

The release confirms the minimal contract. `docs/bundle-format.md` requires only task ID/prompt, nonempty criteria, numeric weight, grader type, one prompt-like field, and optional reference. `portex_eval/bundle/formatter.py` can induce two to five criteria from a reference answer using another LLM at temperature .3, normalizes weights to 100, and discards the source answer when writing the bundle. It records no inducer output provenance, human approval, authority, applicability, criterion dependence, or transformation review. This is a useful authoring convenience, but an induced criterion is not an expert preference.

### Cross-harness realization

Inspect generates a final answer and applies the contract. The Harbor adapter turns each task into a container-oriented directory with instructions, references, environment settings, and verifier package, then grades `/app/answer.txt` with the same core (Section 3.3–3.4, pp. 6–7).

A shared contract controls one source of drift, but it does not make the treatments or evidence views equivalent:

- Inspect and Harbor differ in agent policy, turn structure, filesystem, tool access, reference materialization, and error modes.
- The empirical study runs only Harbor, so no Inspect–Harbor differential evidence exists (p. 6).
- The release's `portex_eval/benchmark/inspect/scorer.py` and `grading/core.py` send semantic judges task text, final submission, and criterion text. They do not send reference-file content, tool trace, intermediate state, native artifact, or environment state.
- The Harbor verifier records a reference filename but likewise calls the shared final-text grader without the reference contents (`portex_eval/benchmark/harbor/verifier.py`, lines 129–159 in the pinned archive).

Thus a candidate may receive an image/document that the judge cannot observe. Conversely, the judge receives the criterion's expected semantic content, potentially creating a reference-aware answer check rather than an independent assessment of the underlying artifact or workflow.

### Empirical population and configured systems

PORTEX-COMPOSITE is proprietary. The paper says 75 frontier tasks, 54 unique exact-match criteria, and 132 unique semantic criteria; four frontier-class solvers each attempt all tasks through `terminus2-multimodal` with a ten-turn cap. Only 245/300 attempts are successful, with 46–71 graded tasks per solver; 32 tasks and 63 semantic criterion keys overlap all four runs (Section 4.1–4.2, p. 7).

The frontier jury is DeepSeek v3.2, GLM-5, GPT-OSS-120B, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, and Kimi-K2.5. The compact jury is Gemma-3-4B-IT, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, OLMo-3-7B-Instruct, Qwen3-8B, and Ministral-8B-2512 (Table 3, p. 8). Task prompts, submissions, criteria, and weights are held fixed between panels; each criterion receives five votes per panel.

This is a paired panel comparison, but not a randomized or calibrated capacity experiment. Model inclusion, family overlap, exact endpoint snapshots, decoding parameters, ordering, provider routing, and panel-selection rationale are not reported. “Frontier” is an operational label, not a criterion standard. There is one call per judge–criterion, so call-level stochastic reliability is unknown.

### Metrics and evidence

Primary results are majority-decision agreement between panels and within-panel vote splits. Secondary results compare weighted task-score vectors and provider telemetry (Section 4.4, pp. 8–10):

- majority agreement: 75.9%–89.6% by solver, mean 83.7%;
- common 63-key subset: 77.8%–92.1%, mean 85.7%;
- 3–2 splits: 6.1%–11.5% frontier versus 28.7%–32.4% compact;
- weighted-score correlations: .81–.93 across graded tasks;
- 70%–87% of tasks have no score change, but mean absolute gaps range 3.7–14.0 points and one task differs by 100 points;
- benchmark aggregates differ by +2.7 to +12.3 points in favor of frontier for Opus/Gemini, while compact is about .4 points higher for GPT/Grok;
- pooled five-call judge telemetry averages `$0.064` versus `$0.0019`, 496 ms versus 88 ms, 23.0k versus 5.8k output tokens, and 28.4k versus 11.2k total tokens per semantic criterion.

The result that coarse aggregates can look similar while criteria differ is robustly useful. It shows that aggregation can hide measurement disagreement rather than resolve it. Yet the paper does not report task-pass agreement, confusion matrices, criterion-family slices, threshold sensitivity, ranking effects, uncertainty intervals for agreement/score differences, repeated-call variance, or decision loss. Run-wise ranges over four solver configurations are not sampling uncertainty.

The “common subset” is common across solver runs, not an independently sampled holdout. It conditions on completion and criterion presence. The full comparison is already available-case analysis because 55 attempts fail. Neither view estimates behavior on the original 300-attempt population without an explicit missingness policy.

### Ordinal disagreement analysis

The authors model disagreement severity (`5:0`, `4:1`, `3:2`) over 429 criterion instances × two panels with solver fixed effects, a criterion-instance random intercept, response length, panel, and interaction (pp. 9–11). The compact-panel odds ratio is 7.56 (95% HDI 5.29–10.93), while length and the interaction remain uncertain. Leave-one-run-out and binary checks reportedly preserve the qualitative result.

This analysis supports a panel-level difference in vote concentration for the available sample. It does not establish “internal stability”: each vote comes from a different model and there are no repeated calls. A split may reflect correlated family behavior, criterion ambiguity, evidence insufficiency, or legitimate interpretation differences. The model also omits explicit task/criterion-family hierarchy despite criteria sharing tasks and contracts, and the analysis artifacts are unavailable.

### Qualitative example

The one observable case is a Lie-algebra prompt whose semantic criterion requires a specific cochain `γ`, map `i`, and injectivity argument. The submission instead argues that the underspecified problem permits only a general characterization. All frontier judges fail it; the compact panel splits 3–2 (pp. 11–13).

This example undermines a simple “compact variance” account. It exposes a **rubric–problem validity dispute**: two compact judges treat mathematical substance as sufficient while others enforce the expected notation/path. With no expert adjudication, either majority may be wrong. The criterion could be answer-derived, overly specific, or based on omitted context. The example therefore demonstrates why dissent should trigger contract review, not automatically produce teacher labels for evaluator distillation.

## Evidence quality

### What the study genuinely supports

1. A criterion-centric package can make grader type, criterion prompt, weights, threshold, and individual votes inspectable.
2. The same grading core can be wired into two harness implementations.
3. On one private available-case sample, the selected compact panel had lower cross-model consensus and nontrivial majority divergence from the selected frontier panel.
4. Weighted aggregation and deterministic criteria attenuated visible criterion disagreement in many task totals.
5. Smaller selected judges were much cheaper and faster under one provider snapshot.

### What is missing

- No expert count, qualifications, roles, elicitation method, authoring protocol, disagreement, review, approval, time, or cost.
- No released PORTEX-COMPOSITE task, contract, submission, judge record, or analysis table.
- No human adjudication or gold labels, including on the disagreement subset.
- No criterion validity, dependence, applicability, evidence-view, alternative-path, or threshold calibration study.
- No repeated calls or temporal/model-snapshot reproducibility.
- No balanced sampling frame for task, criterion, solver, or judge families.
- No uncertainty for headline panel agreement or task-score comparisons.
- No cross-harness measurement-equivalence trial.
- No downstream decision or post-training experiment.

The authors acknowledge several of these limits (Section 6, pp. 13–14), especially absence of human labels, one harness, ten-turn failures, STEM-heavy private tasks, provider telemetry, and dependence on rubric quality. They do not fully acknowledge that upstream expert-preference transfer is entirely unmeasured.

## Release audit and reproducibility

The MIT-licensed commit `d71d84b...` predates v1 and contains 79 files (1,768,190 uncompressed bytes): package code, docs, examples, providers, Inspect/Harbor adapters, reporting, tests, and lockfile. This is meaningful implementation evidence, but it is not the empirical release.

Important paper–implementation boundaries:

1. **“ExactMatch” is substring inclusion.** `grading/core.py` normalizes reference and extracted terminal answer, then passes when `reference in predicted`; `tests/test_scorer.py` explicitly calls this “passes on inclusion.” A negated answer containing the expected text can pass. The paper's “compares against” description does not expose this semantic choice.
2. **Evidence views are final-text only for judging.** The shared semantic prompt contains task, submission, and criterion, not the reference artifact or trace. The contract can name a reference file without proving the judge observed it.
3. **Invalid judge output fails closed but is conflated with substantive failure.** A response without `GRADE: C/I` becomes incorrect, with no separate invalid/insufficient state.
4. **Judge exceptions are not partially aggregated.** `asyncio.gather` has no exception return handling in the shared core; a provider exception can invalidate the verifier, while the paper says unusable votes would be dropped. The study reports none occurred.
5. **Weights are summed without dependency or gate semantics.** The validator does not require sum 100 for pre-authored bundles, nonnegative weights, threshold bounds, unique criterion IDs, or criterion independence.
6. **Formatter transformation loses information.** The formatter's task normalization keeps task ID, prompt, and reference only, despite documentation saying metadata/environment are preserved for Harbor; the output path drops them. This makes “portable contract” transformation itself a possible source of drift.
7. **No immutable configured-study record.** The release supplies default judge strings but no PORTEX-COMPOSITE manifest, exact solver/provider snapshots, prompt hashes, run seeds, attempts, votes, costs, or results.

A local test collection attempt was blocked by absent `inspect_ai`, `typer`, and `pandas` dependencies in the repository environment. That is not counted as a release failure. Static inspection was complete, and the exact-match inclusion behavior is directly asserted by release tests.

## Unique insight

The paper's most valuable result is not that compact juries are cheaper. It is an identification boundary:

> **An aggregate can be stable because disagreement is canceled, compensated, threshold-insensitive, or diluted by deterministic criteria—not because the underlying semantic measurement is equivalent.**

This implies a four-level substitution test:

```text
observation equivalence
→ criterion-decision equivalence
→ task-decision/ranking equivalence
→ use/decision-loss equivalence
```

A panel may pass a later level for one use while failing an earlier level, but the discrepancy must remain visible. Coarse monitoring could tolerate some criterion mismatch; diagnosis, reward construction, safety gating, or expert audit may not. The acceptable level is a stakeholder loss decision, not a correlation threshold discovered from the same corpus.

The second unique insight comes from the release: **a portable contract needs transformation conformance, not only a common source file.** If formatter, Inspect adapter, and Harbor adapter expose different metadata, references, artifacts, invalid states, or execution evidence, “same contract” is a claim to test with paired fixtures.

## Limitations and threats to validity

### Construct validity

“Human expert preference” is asserted but not operationalized. The empirical construct is agreement between two model panels executing private author-provided binary criteria. Frontier-majority behavior is neither expert truth nor criterion validity.

### Internal validity

Panel capacity is confounded with model family, training, provider behavior, prompt following, and panel composition. Available-case filtering depends on solver completion. No repeated calls distinguish stochastic instability from cross-model heterogeneity. Contract authorship may be coupled to expected solutions and judge prompts.

### Measurement validity

Binary votes cannot express applicability or insufficiency. Criterion dependencies and compensatory weights can hide shared errors. Exact-match substring semantics admit false positives. Semantic judges often lack source/artifact/trace evidence. Invalid outputs can become failures. The paper does not report pass-decision agreement or threshold sensitivity.

### Statistical validity

Only four solver-run summaries support headline ranges. No confidence/credible intervals accompany agreement or task-score differences. Criteria cluster within tasks and likely rubric families. The ordinal model handles paired pool rows with a criterion-instance intercept but does not expose task-level clustering or release analysis code.

### External validity

The suite is proprietary and predominantly frontier-knowledge STEM. One ten-turn Harbor policy, one provider, and selected open-weight panels do not generalize to realistic artifact-rich knowledge work, other judge prompts, direct models, human raters, or consequential decisions.

### Reproducibility and operational realism

The framework is inspectable; the study is not reproducible. Provider pricing, endpoint routing, latency, and model snapshots are mutable. No task/results release or exact run manifest exists. The system grades a terminal text artifact, even though the paper motivates long-horizon agents and intermediate decisions. It therefore supplies operational evaluator plumbing, not operational evidence that the evaluator measures professional work.

## Transfer to skill-bench

### Retain

1. **Versioned evaluation package:** keep criterion text, grader mode, weight, threshold, panel, and aggregation explicit and independently hashed.
2. **Criterion and judge traces:** retain every raw vote/rationale and configured evidence view; never overwrite these with the majority.
3. **Mixed deterministic/semantic checks:** preserve typed check families under one task while keeping their estimands separate.
4. **Cost/latency alongside quality:** record per-criterion usage and latency as operational outcomes, not substitutes for validity.
5. **Escalation from dissent:** use split/invalid/insufficient signals to trigger adjudication or contract review.

### Repair

1. Bind every criterion to requirement authority, public basis, evidence locator, applicability, evidence view, alternatives, dependencies, criticality, and threshold warrant.
2. Distinguish `supported`, `contradicted`, `not_applicable`, `insufficient_evidence`, `invalid_grader`, and `invalid_artifact` before binary or weighted aggregation.
3. Treat panel definition, prompt, call topology, decoding, provider snapshot, parser, missing-vote rule, and aggregation as immutable grader identity.
4. Keep expert/source authority separate from criterion author, transformation author, judge, and aggregation-policy owner.
5. Require native-vs-adapter differential tests for task content, reference access, evidence views, invalid handling, score, and output artifacts.
6. Test deterministic comparators adversarially; do not label substring inclusion as exact equality.
7. Report original eligible population, solver failures, grader failures, selected/comparable subsets, and task/rubric clustering.

### Validate

A compact reusable study, not a new subsystem, should cross:

- independently adjudicated positive, negative, not-applicable, insufficient-evidence, and invalid-artifact cases;
- exact equality, normalized equivalence, containment, deterministic artifact checks, and semantic judging;
- one model judge, heterogeneous jury, and human/expert adjudication;
- final text versus source/trace/native-artifact evidence views;
- repeated calls and panel-composition resamples;
- criterion, task decision, rank, decision loss, cost, and audit burden;
- native bundle versus each adapter realization.

Pre-register escalation and use thresholds. A compact panel can be accepted for a narrow monitoring decision without being promoted to criterion truth or reward-label authority.

- `schemas/task-health.schema.json` for grader defects, adjudication, revision, and role transitions;
- `schemas/validity-argument.schema.json` for bounded panel-substitution and use claims;
- existing plural-judgment, artifact-admissibility, procedure-package, and adapter-conformance validation work.

The immediate consolidation question is whether those records explicitly distinguish **cross-model dissent**, **repeated-call instability**, **criterion correctness**, and **decision loss**. They must not share one field named reliability.

## Bottom line

AsymmetryZero contributes good evaluator engineering and a revealing panel-comparison result. Explicit contracts and raw jury traces are worth preserving. But the title's expert-preference claim is upstream of everything the study measures: no expert process, authority, criterion validation, human adjudication, or downstream use is observed. The private available-case experiment shows that one compact panel disagrees more often with one frontier panel and is cheaper; the release shows how to execute that comparison, while also exposing evidence-view, exact-match, invalid-state, and transformation limits. For `skill-bench`, the right lesson is **contract-first, but authority-before-contract and validity-after-execution**.
