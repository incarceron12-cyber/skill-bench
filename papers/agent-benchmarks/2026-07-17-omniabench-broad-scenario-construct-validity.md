## One-sentence contribution

OmniaBench contributes an unusually broad, inspectable **synthetic environment-and-task generator output**—354 domain-covering multi-turn DAG tasks plus 290 single-turn DAG-S/Solver/Program tasks—but its 90/354 taxonomy counts measure category enumeration, not a sampled distribution of consequential work; its challenge set is selected on generated chain length and unspecified low-complexity exclusion; its released data omit the labels needed to replay capability/domain analyses; and its human refinement, rubric correctness, simulated-user fidelity, verifier completeness, and professional acceptance are not validated, so the evidence supports a difficult configured-package conformance suite rather than “general-agent capability boundaries.”

## Why this matters for skill-bench

This review advances charter objectives A, B, and D through comparative design research. OmniaBench is not a reason to make `skill-bench` a universal app simulator. It is a useful stress test of a more general inference problem:

> **When does breadth of category names become breadth of measured work?**

A benchmark can have hundreds of domain labels while still being generated by one construction family, one environment abstraction, one reference-path logic, and one scoring package. Cross-domain breadth requires evidence at several distinct layers:

```text
coverage frame and source authority
→ sampled demand or justified authored portfolio
→ source-to-scenario projection
→ environment/task realization
→ evaluator-valid observations
→ repeated configured-system measurements
→ domain/capability inference
→ external professional or workflow consequence
```

OmniaBench supplies substantial evidence for the middle two links and some configured-system measurement. It does not establish the first, last, or the bridges among them.

## Research question and strongest defensible claim

The paper asks whether a single benchmark can cover a much wider range of consumer-facing (ToC), business-facing (ToB), and employee-oriented (ToE) scenarios while combining stateful environments, single- and multi-turn interaction, tools, files, code, personas, ten capability labels, eight difficulty factors, and route-specific grading (pp. 1–8).

The strongest defensible empirical claim is:

> On one public, generated 644-task challenge suite, the authors report one Pass@1 attempt for 22 named model/API configurations under a shared function-calling harness, a DeepSeek-V4-Pro simulated user and rubric judge, route-specific rubric or program checks, and generous 200-tool-step/20-environment-exception limits. The reported micro-averaged full-pass rate ranges from 9.16% to 58.54%; two selected models receive eight-run and aggregate-stability analyses, while selected trajectories receive judge-substitution and error analyses.

This is a **configured synthetic-suite observation**. It does not identify a general-agent trait independent of the harness, user simulator, judge, route mixture, language mixture, generated environments, challenge selection, or all-items-pass rule. It does not estimate prevalence-weighted workplace ability, professional artifact quality, safety, productivity, deployment fitness, or readiness.

## Methodology and system

### Scenario frame: taxonomy synthesis, not task-population sampling

The paper reports 90 level-1 and 354 level-2 domains:

- 22/101 ToC categories derived from multiple app stores;
- 38/186 ToB categories described as GDPval-style tasks and industry classifications; and
- 30/67 ToE categories described as industry-general employee tasks from GDPval and industry templates (Table 2, p. 5).

For each level-2 category, a Web agent collects information from applications, product functions, business workflows, and user needs; humans “refine” that material; Qwen3.5-397B-A17B helps synthesize environments and tasks (pp. 6–9). Environment specifications become Python classes containing entities, attributes, states, constraints, tools, initialization data, and state transitions.

This is a sensible **coverage-first authoring strategy**, but the evidentiary chain is missing:

- no app-store list, search query, retrieval timestamp, source span, PRD, industry taxonomy version, inclusion rule, or normalization crosswalk is released;
- no category frequency or target-use weighting is reported;
- no sampling frame connects 354 labels to actual user or workplace demand;
- no contributor roles, qualifications, annotation protocol, hours, agreement, rejection rate, or item-level approval are reported for “human refinement”; and
- ToC/ToB/ToE are origin labels, not independent evidence that the realized tasks preserve consumer, business, or employee practice.

The taxonomy is therefore an authored scenario inventory. Counting labels establishes nominal variety, not content validity, occupational representativeness, ecological validity, or inference to “general” agent work.

### Four routes and their dependence

The construction pipeline uses four routes (pp. 6–7, 22–23):

1. **DAG:** sample tool dependency chains/DAGs from generated environments; condition requests, reference trajectories, and final states on those chains; run execution/repair/state-consistency checks; evaluate in multi-turn interaction.
2. **DAG-S:** query-refine DAG-derived tasks into single-turn tasks.
3. **Solver:** generate selection, scheduling, allocation, and optimization tasks using implicit or explicit solver anchors.
4. **Program:** generate procedural tasks involving branches, iteration, program synthesis, debugging, and refinement; grade via executable `VerifyCode`.

The diversity is real at the interface level, but the routes are not independent evidence families. DAG-S is explicitly derived from DAG. All routes share generated environment machinery, synthesis-model choices, human curation, and benchmark-owned targets. A task can therefore look cross-domain while testing common generator conventions: exhaustive retrieval, long conjunctive instructions, exact state mutation, and reference-aligned reporting.

The paper says execution, syntax, state consistency, solvability, semantic consistency, evaluation validity, and ambiguity are checked, but provides no acceptance counts, before/after defects, independent oracle derivation, alternative-solution study, or held-out expert audit. A successful reference process proves at most that one benchmark-authored path can execute. It does not prove that the request is natural, the environment is professionally faithful, the oracle is complete, or all legitimate alternatives pass.

### Challenge-set selection changes the estimand

The full pool contains 1,431 tasks: 1,102 DAG, 222 DAG-S, 60 Solver, and 47 Program (Table 9, p. 22). The public challenge set contains 644:

- one DAG task per level-2 domain, chosen as the task with the **longest generated reference tool chain** (354);
- 200 of 222 DAG-S tasks after excluding a limited number of low-complexity cases;
- all 60 Solver tasks; and
- 30 of 47 Program tasks after similar exclusion (pp. 7–8).

This gives each level-2 label one DAG representative and increases nominal difficulty, but it does not preserve a demand distribution. “Longest reference chain” is a benchmark-construction proxy, not observed human difficulty or consequence. It can select verbose generator paths, brittle reference decompositions, or unusually large tool graphs. The criteria for the other exclusions are not operationalized. No full-versus-challenge bridge reports score correlation, rank stability, capability mix, language mix, or error transport.

The contamination rationale is also incomplete. Withholding 787 tasks limits exposure of that unreleased pool; it does not make the **public 644-task leaderboard set** contamination-resistant after release. There is no sealed test, rotating form, exposure ledger, canary, retrieval restriction, or versioned replacement policy. Hardness and contamination are distinct properties.

### Interaction and user simulation

DAG tasks use a persona-grounded simulated user. Persona cards specify identity, communication style, emotion, disclosure behavior, patience, preferences, and practical constraints. The simulator receives the persona, environment summary, and full task description; it progressively reveals task information and emits `###STOP###` to terminate (pp. 8, 23–25).

The paper substitutes three user models for a test involving three evaluated models and reports unchanged ranking (Kendall’s τ = 1) with 7.24% average coefficient of variation (p. 11). This is useful sensitivity evidence, but weak validation:

- three evaluated models permit only six possible rankings, so τ = 1 is coarse;
- moderate absolute-score movement remains;
- no human-user trajectories, preference fidelity, disclosure calibration, correction behavior, or stop appropriateness are measured;
- the same named DeepSeek-V4-Pro configuration is the default user simulator and rubric judge, creating shared-model dependence; and
- the user sees benchmark-authoritative instructions, so successful clarification can reflect answer-bearing simulator policy rather than discovery under authentic uncertainty.

The release contains 334 nonempty personas among 354 route-1 rows; 20 route-1 rows have no nonempty persona despite the route's persona framing. Solver, Program, and DAG-S rows have no nonempty persona. Persona effects are therefore route- and task-composition dependent, not a separately randomized treatment.

### Grading: inspectable but not validated against authority

DAG, Solver, and DAG-S use weighted rubric items worth one to three points. An LLM judge sees the task, complete initial and final serialized state, final response, a deterministic trajectory summary, and rubric list. Every item is binary; a task passes only if all items pass. Program uses a benchmark-authored Python verifier over a candidate answer and one ground-truth answer (pp. 8–9, 15–19, 23).

Strengths worth retaining:

- complete environment state is available to the rubric judge rather than only the final prose;
- all-or-nothing task success prevents serious omitted obligations from being averaged away;
- rubric items and program verifiers are public and inspectable;
- program verifier failures are separated from rubric outputs in the runner; and
- the paper explicitly notes that judge agreement does not prove correctness (p. 16).

But the observations remain co-designed with tasks and references:

- no criterion-level source or expert-authority lineage is released;
- no human correctness labels, accepted-alternative set, false-positive/false-negative tests, or consequential state-transition calibration are reported;
- the judge prompt declares a prerequisite-dependent item satisfied when its prerequisite is false, which may be legitimate `not_applicable` handling but is collapsed into pass rather than typed applicability;
- task-level conjunction is dominated by criterion count and dependence, yet no criterion dependence or weight calibration is studied; and
- a single canonical ground-truth object and verifier can reject valid alternatives or accept semantically wrong outputs that match encoded fields.

Judge substitution covers 1,102 trajectories and 9,808 trajectory-item units with five model judges. Reported agreement is ICC(A,1) 0.780 for total scores, Fleiss’ κ 0.573 for binary item decisions, 86.3% unanimous items, and 4.4% 3–2 splits (pp. 15–19). This demonstrates configured **inter-judge stability**, not accuracy. The units' route/source and selection are not fully explained; complete-case filtering drops invalid judge outputs; item micro-averaging gives criteria-rich trajectories more weight; and no human or deterministic authority anchors the shared errors.

### Metrics and uncertainty

The manuscript's Overall Pass@1 is a **task-micro average**: total full passes divided by 644. Domain splits use their task counts; multi-label capability scores average task scores for every assigned capability (pp. 8–10). This is transparent, but it mixes:

- 354 multi-turn DAG, 200 DAG-S, 60 Solver, and 30 Program items;
- rubric and executable-verifier observers;
- Chinese and English tasks;
- one-item versus roughly 13-item criteria structures;
- very different tool and entity counts; and
- one selected task per level-2 DAG domain rather than domain-level repeated samples.

No prevalence, consequence, cost, or construct weights justify the mixture. Capability labels overlap and are assigned during the same synthesis process, so radar axes are descriptive slices rather than separable latent abilities. No standard errors, task-cluster bootstrap, paired model intervals, multiple-comparison controls, or missing/invalid-run denominator policy accompany the main 22-model table.

The paper reports Pass@8 and all-eight-pass (`Pass-hat@8`) only for two models, and aggregate Pass@1 standard deviations of 1.07 and 2.68 points (p. 11). This reveals substantial run unreliability but cannot support model-wide reliability claims. Run independence, seeds, task-level reliability distribution, and route/domain clustering are not documented.

The error analysis assigns 53.8% of Claude-Opus-4.7 errors to reasoning (36.7% planning/decomposition and 16.5% constraint violations) and 31.0% to metacognition (p. 11). The paper gives no coding guide, unit count, multilabel policy, annotator identity, evidence view, agreement, adjudication, or uncertainty. These are hypotheses about failure surfaces, not validated root causes.

### Configured-system comparability

All tools use OpenAI-style function schemas, but decoding is not identical: most models use temperature zero where supported, while Claude and Gemini retain default temperature one; GPT gets high reasoning effort, Claude gets maximum reasoning budget, and other models use their maximum/explicit thinking mode when available. Evaluations use provider APIs available from 29 June to 15 July 2026 (pp. 8–9).

That is a reasonable “best configured package” policy, not a controlled base-model comparison. Provider snapshots, request/response receipts, token budgets, context truncation, exact tool-result policies, API retries, latency, and cost are not reported per cell. Up to 200 tool calls and 20 environment exceptions make completion possible but also mask environment defects differently across trajectories. Leaderboard differences combine model, provider, decoding, reasoning budget, stochasticity, user model, judge, and retry behavior.

## Release audit

### What is inspectable

The official Hugging Face revision contains exactly the advertised 644 rows:

| Route | Rows | Language | Grading | Audit notes |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| DAG / route1 | 354 | 221 Chinese, 133 English | 7–13 rubric items | 334 nonempty personas; 9 filesystem inputs |
| Solver / route2 | 60 | 13 Chinese, 47 English | 2–9 rubric items | full generated environment code/state |
| Program / route3 | 30 | no `lang` field | 30 executable verifiers | one ground-truth object per task |
| DAG-S / route4 | 200 | all English | 10–17 rubric items | full generated environment code/state |

All 644 `global_id` values and exact task texts are unique. The 30 verifier strings parse as Python. The 36 ordinary Python files in the repository compile. The release has a practical runner, route registry, state-diff capture, deterministic trajectory summaries, filesystem path checks, rubric output validation, and post-hoc rescoring.

### Release defects and claim blockers

1. **Two task environments do not parse.** Embedded environment code fails Python syntax parsing for global ID `785` / task `10069_task_001` (a split `if ...` colon) and global ID `3000093` / task `ZBB_3.0_2739_G_3.1_2_en` (nested f-string quoting). These rows cannot instantiate under ordinary `compile`/`exec` until repaired. The repository has no tests.
2. **Taxonomy/diagnostic labels are stripped.** No released task row has top-level domain, capability, or difficulty labels. The dataset card explicitly says taxonomy/debug metadata were removed. Thus the paper's domain/capability/difficulty analyses cannot be recomputed from the public task files. Website aggregate JSON contains displayed values and denominators, not the task-label/trajectory evidence needed to audit them.
3. **The full selection frame is absent.** The 1,431-task pool, reference paths, level-2 labels, chain lengths, low-complexity exclusions, and selection decisions are not released. The claimed one-longest-DAG-per-domain construction cannot be independently replayed.
4. **Result evidence is absent.** There are no manuscript trajectories, outputs, state diffs, rubric judgments, verifier rows, invalid-run records, repeated runs, error annotations, or scripts that regenerate tables and figures. The website contains aggregate display data only.
5. **Score semantics drift inside the release.** The paper defines Overall as micro-averaged passes over 644. `evaluation/scripts/route_scores.py` instead prints an “overall” arithmetic mean giving each of four routes equal weight. Both can be legitimate estimands, but they are materially different and share the same label without a bridge.
6. **Task identity is unsafe without `global_id`.** Route 1 has only 159 unique `task_id` strings among 354 rows; 73 duplicated IDs occur because generated environments reuse local names. The runner's global IDs avoid collision, but downstream tooling must not assume `task_id` is unique.
7. **The “sandbox” is not an outer execution sandbox.** Filesystem tools confine paths to a copied root, but `python_executor` directly executes model-provided code with full Python builtins in the evaluator process. Its `timeout_seconds` value is merely returned in metadata; no timeout, process boundary, import restriction, network restriction, CPU/memory limit, or syscall isolation is enforced. Benchmark-authored verifier strings are likewise executed with full builtins. This blocks a production-safety interpretation and weakens reproducible containment.
8. **Language and route are confounded.** Route 4 is entirely English, route 1 is mostly Chinese, route 2 is mostly English, and route 3 lacks an explicit language field. Aggregate route/capability/domain differences can absorb language effects.
9. **Dataset terms are unresolved.** The card declares `omniabench-research-only` while saying final terms are pending; the code repository's Apache-2.0 license does not settle dataset redistribution.
10. **No clean-release validation exists.** There are no tests, lockfile/container for the Python harness, expected canary outputs, or released no-cost fixture that proves all 644 tasks load and graders execute.

## Evidence and claim boundaries

### Supported

1. The paper defines a broad three-view taxonomy and a coherent four-route generation/evaluation architecture.
2. The released challenge set contains exactly 354 DAG, 60 Solver, 30 Program, and 200 DAG-S rows with unique global IDs and task texts.
3. Generated state, tools, environment code, rubrics, and program verifiers are substantially more inspectable than a prompt-only benchmark.
4. The manuscript reports low full-pass rates and heterogeneous route/domain/capability slices for 22 configured model/API packages.
5. Five model judges show moderate-to-high agreement on selected fixed trajectories while the authors correctly avoid calling agreement correctness.
6. Two models' eight-run analysis shows that occasional success and all-run reliability diverge.
7. The release permits concrete auditing and exposes two invalid environment programs, stripped diagnostic labels, score-estimand drift, and unsafe Python execution.

### Partially supported

- **Broad scenario coverage:** many named categories and generated environments are represented; demand prevalence, source lineage, within-domain variation, and ecological validity are not.
- **Executable environments:** most embedded code parses and tools expose explicit state transitions; two released tasks do not parse, and execution equivalence/professional fidelity are unvalidated.
- **Diagnostic capabilities:** ten labels are conceptually plausible; labels overlap, are co-authored with tasks, are not released per item, and lack external construct validation.
- **Evaluation stability:** judge and user substitution/repeated-run analyses exist for selected slices; no human accuracy anchor, complete uncertainty model, or all-system replication exists.
- **Contamination mitigation:** withholding the non-challenge pool reduces its public exposure; the public challenge set itself has no post-release contamination defense.
- **Reproducibility:** tasks and current runner are inspectable; paper-time revisions and result evidence are not, and the released suite has static/runtime-boundary defects.

### Not supported

- a prevalence-weighted estimate of general-agent capability;
- representative consumer, business, employee, occupation, or industry performance;
- domain expertise, professional artifact quality, user benefit, productivity, or economic value;
- causal attribution to planning, state management, metacognition, or another capability label;
- correctness or completeness of all rubrics, verifiers, reference paths, or generated environments;
- human-user fidelity, appropriate clarification, or preference/consent validity;
- safety of model code/tool execution or safe deployment behavior;
- fair model-only ranking independent of provider, decoding, reasoning budget, user, judge, retries, route, or language;
- post-release contamination resistance of the public challenge set;
- reliable success for one model-task pair from Pass@1;
- production fitness, professional substitution, or readiness.

## Unique insight

OmniaBench's distinctive lesson is a separation that benchmark designers often miss:

> **Coverage breadth is a graph, not a count. Category breadth, task breadth, environment breadth, observer breadth, and inference breadth are different objects, and no one inherits validity from another.**

A reusable breadth record should preserve:

```text
coverage-frame node
source/version + inclusion rule + target use + prevalence or portfolio rationale

category node
definition + parent + overlaps + authority + source locators

realized-task node
projection path + generator/reviewer + route + language + environment + artifacts

measurement node
observer type + evidence view + applicability + alternatives + uncertainty

inference node
eligible population + weighting + cluster structure + generalization boundary
```

OmniaBench has a large category graph and many realized tasks, but the source, projection, measurement, and inference edges are mostly unreleased or unvalidated. `skill-bench` should never use “number of domains” as a proxy for breadth without reporting how many independent work shapes, evidence structures, artifact types, consequence types, authority sources, and observer families those labels actually instantiate.

## Reproducibility and operational realism

**Task inspectability is high.** Public rows expose natural-language requests, full synthetic initial states, generated environment code, tools, personas where used, rubric lists, and program verifiers. The current runner captures trajectories, state diffs, scores, and failure data and can use multiple providers.

**Exact paper-result reproducibility is low.** The audited official releases postdate arXiv v1, and there are no paper-time commit IDs, full pool, task-label crosswalk, result rows, traces, judge outputs, error labels, seeds, API receipts, or figure-generation artifacts. Public website aggregates are not a replay package.

**Operational conformance is defective.** Two of 644 embedded environment programs fail syntax parsing. The repository has no tests. The filesystem adapter constrains file paths, but the Python executor runs arbitrary model code in-process with full builtins and no enforced timeout or network/process/resource boundary. The runner's per-route display uses a different “overall” weighting than the paper.

**Operational realism is bounded.** The generated tasks contain substantial structured state, long requests, conflicting records, files, post-state checks, and multi-step writes. Those are valuable knowledge-work ingredients. But the environments are benchmark-authored simulations distilled through unreleased Web-agent and human-refinement steps; there are no real workflow logs, domain-expert approvals, accepted alternative artifacts, stakeholder consequences, or evidence that the long conjunctive requests reflect natural work frequency. Realistic surface semantics do not establish operational or professional validity.

## Transfer to skill-bench

### Retain

1. **Coverage frame plus multiple views.** Separate where tasks arise, what capabilities they invoke, and what difficulty mechanisms they instantiate.
2. **Explicit state spaces.** Preserve initial state, observations, actions, transitions, final state, and termination reason.
3. **Plural routes.** Mix multi-turn stateful work, single-turn artifact work, constrained optimization, and executable procedural checks when they represent genuinely different work shapes.
4. **Route-specific observers.** Do not force all artifacts through one judge; use deterministic state checks, executable verifiers, artifact inspection, and human review where admissible.
5. **Strict critical obligations.** Required failures should not average away, while partial scores remain diagnostic.
6. **User/judge sensitivity checks.** Treat simulator and evaluator identity as configured components and test substitutions.
7. **Repeated-run reliability.** Report occasional success separately from all-run consistency.
8. **Inspectable release rows.** Publish task, state, tools, rubric/verifier, and runner contracts where rights allow.

### Repair

1. **Turn breadth into an evidence graph.** Bind every category and task to source, authority, projection, reviewer, intended population, and inference boundary.
2. **Sample work shapes, not just category labels.** Track artifact, evidence, interaction, state-transition, consequence, and observer diversity; cluster near-duplicate generator templates.
3. **Freeze selection history.** Retain full candidate/admission/rejection records and bridge full-pool versus challenge-set metrics.
4. **Separate challenge from contamination.** Use sealed or rotating forms, exposure ledgers, canaries, retrieval controls, and retirement policies.
5. **Release diagnostic labels.** Domain/capability/difficulty claims require item-level labels, versions, annotation provenance, and analysis code.
6. **Type applicability.** Keep `pass`, `fail`, `not_applicable`, `invalid_artifact`, and `insufficient_evidence` distinct.
7. **Calibrate observers.** Plant positive/negative/alternative cases; measure human agreement, false positives/negatives, criterion dependence, and evidence-view sufficiency.
8. **Validate simulated users.** Compare disclosure, clarification, correction, stop, burden, and preference consequences against consented human interactions where legitimate.
9. **Estimate uncertainty by cluster.** Pair model comparisons by task; cluster by environment/category/template; report route/language slices and missing/invalid denominators.
10. **Harden execution.** Run model code and benchmark verifiers in separate resource-limited, filesystem- and network-confined processes with canaries and receipts.
11. **Use one named aggregate per estimand.** Distinguish task-micro, route-macro, domain-macro, prevalence-weighted, consequence-weighted, and cost-aware scores.
12. **Require clean-release conformance.** Load every task, compile every embedded program/verifier, execute no-cost canaries, and publish expected outcomes before release.
13. **Close with external acceptance.** Synthetic conformance may precede but cannot replace expert artifact review, workflow uptake, or measured consequences.

### Map to existing repository machinery

No new queue task is warranted. The findings refine existing, already implemented homes:

- `schemas/expertise-transfer.schema.json` for category/source/task projection and authority;
- `schemas/benchmark-bundle.schema.json` for configured systems, state/artifact observations, typed outcomes, traces, and execution identity;
- `schemas/task-health.schema.json` for candidate admission, challenge selection, defects, revisions, and retirement;
- `schemas/metric-monitoring.schema.json` for eligible populations, clustering, weighting, missingness, uncertainty, and aggregation semantics;
- `schemas/validity-argument.schema.json` for limiting synthetic-suite measurements to bounded claims;
- `schemas/compounding-lessons.schema.json` and longitudinal machinery for contamination/exposure and benchmark-version boundaries; and
- existing execution-isolation and artifact-admissibility contracts for fail-closed environment/verifier/tool evidence.

## Action items

1. Add OmniaBench to the next state-of-the-art consolidation as a **Tier B enabling benchmark**: retain broad explicit-state synthetic coverage; mark task-population, professional-validity, and general-agent claims unsupported.
2. When expanding `skill-bench` domains, report a coverage matrix over **work shape × evidence structure × artifact × interaction × consequence × observer**, not only profession/domain counts.
3. Add an all-bundle release gate that compiles embedded environment/verifier code, checks globally unique identity, replays no-cost controls, and asserts a single versioned aggregation specification.
4. For any challenge subset, preserve candidate/selection history and test whether model ranking, capability mix, failure rates, language mix, and uncertainty transport from the parent pool.
5. Treat simulator, judge, and generator as potential shared-method factors; include human or deterministic anchors before assigning root-cause labels such as planning or metacognition.

## Bottom line

OmniaBench is valuable because it releases much more than category names: hundreds of large synthetic states, tools, tasks, rubrics, verifiers, and a usable current runner. Its scale makes generated-workflow breadth and cross-route stress testing concrete. The same release also demonstrates why **breadth cannot be inferred from taxonomy cardinality**. The source frame, human authority, selection history, diagnostic labels, result rows, uncertainty, containment, and professional acceptance needed to turn 354 category names into a general-agent claim are absent or defective. `skill-bench` should retain OmniaBench's explicit-state and plural-route machinery while making each coverage-to-inference edge independently inspectable and fail-closed.
